A Multilingual Corpus of Climate Finance Literature, 1990–2025: Construction, Validation, and Semantic Embeddings

Author

Minh Ha-Duong

Published

March 5, 2026

Background & Summary

Climate finance—the financial flows directed at mitigating and adapting to climate change—has become one of the most politically salient objects in international economic governance. The $100 billion annual commitment made at Copenhagen in 2009, and the $300 billion New Collective Quantified Goal agreed at Baku in 2024, are contested not only in their adequacy but in their very definition: what counts as climate finance depends on accounting conventions that are themselves the subject of ongoing dispute (Roberts and Weikmans 2017; Michaelowa and Michaelowa 2007).

A growing body of bibliometric and scientometric work has begun to map the climate finance literature, but existing datasets are either narrowly scoped (single database, English only) or lack the curation needed for reproducible analysis. This data descriptor presents a multilingual, deduplicated, and validated corpus of 22,113 works on climate finance published between 1990 and 2025, assembled from seven bibliographic sources spanning academic publications, institutional grey literature, and curated teaching materials. The corpus is accompanied by 384-dimensional sentence-transformer embeddings for the 18,798 works with abstracts of sufficient length, enabling semantic analysis without requiring users to re-run the embedding pipeline.

The dataset was constructed to support the historical analysis presented in (haduong2026?, submitted to Oeconomia), but its scope and curation make it suitable for a wide range of research applications: topic modelling, structural break detection, citation network analysis, bibliometric mapping of the climate finance field, and cross-lingual studies of how climate finance is discussed in different linguistic traditions.

The corpus is multilingual by design. While English-language works dominate (91.4%), the search strategy explicitly targeted French (finance climat, finance climatique), Chinese (气候融资, 气候金融), Japanese (気候金融, グリーンファイナンス), and German-language literature through ISTEX, bibCNRS, and multilingual OpenAlex queries. This deliberate multilingual scope, though it yields a modest non-English share, provides a foundation for cross-linguistic studies that monolingual corpora cannot support.

Key statistics:

  • 22,113 refined works after deduplication and quality filtering
  • 18,798 works with sentence-transformer embeddings (384 dimensions)
  • 1,176 core works (cited \(\geq\) 50 times), forming the field’s intellectual backbone
  • 7 sources: OpenAlex, OpenAlex (historical), ISTEX, bibCNRS, SciSpace, grey literature (OECD/UNFCCC/World Bank/CPI), teaching canon
  • Languages: English (91.4%), Portuguese, Spanish, French, Chinese, Japanese, German, and others
  • Full audit trail: every inclusion/exclusion decision recorded in corpus_audit.csv

Methods

Corpus construction

1. Corpus Construction

Sources

The corpus assembles academic and grey literature from seven sources:

Source Script Records Coverage
OpenAlex catalog_openalex.py ~11,313 Broad English-language academic literature
OpenAlex (historical) catalog_openalex_historical.py variable Pre-2000 and non-English historical works
ISTEX catalog_istex.py 482 French national archive (full-text JSON)
bibCNRS catalog_bibcnrs.py 242 Non-English literature (FR, ZH, JA, DE)
SciSpace catalog_scispsace.py 663 AI-curated thematic corpus
Grey literature catalog_grey.py 213 OECD, UNFCCC, World Bank, CPI reports
Teaching canon manual variable Papers appearing in 2+ syllabi

Search strategy

  • OpenAlex: Cursor-paginated queries using filter=default.search:"climate finance" and filter=default.search:"finance climat" (captures French variants). Abstracts reconstructed from OpenAlex inverted index format. Polite-pool access with mailto header.
  • ISTEX: Full-text query "climate finance" OR "finance climat" OR "finance climatique" on the national archive.
  • bibCNRS: Title-field searches in French ("finance climat" OR "finance climatique"), Chinese (气候融资 OR 气候金融), Japanese (気候金融 OR グリーンファイナンス OR 気候ファイナンス), and German. Manual RIS export.
  • Grey literature: A curated YAML seed list (16 key policy documents) plus the World Bank Open Knowledge Repository API (query "climate finance", first 200 results).

Merge and deduplication

The merge script (scripts/catalog_merge.py) applies two deduplication passes:

  1. DOI-based deduplication: DOIs are normalized (lowercased, URL prefix stripped). Records sharing the same DOI are merged using a source priority order: openalex > openalex_historical > scopus > istex > jstor > bibcnrs > scispsace > grey > teaching. The maximum cited_by_count across duplicates is retained; other fields use the best non-empty value following source priority.
  2. Title+year deduplication: Records without DOIs are grouped by normalized title (lowercased, punctuation stripped) and year. Groups are merged using the same priority logic.

A source_count field tracks how many sources contributed to each record. The output is unified_works.csv.

Corpus refinement

2. Corpus Refinement

The refinement script (scripts/corpus_refine.py) implements a four-phase pipeline:

Phase A: Flagging

Five flags are applied to each paper:

  1. Missing metadata: Papers lacking a title are always flagged. Papers missing only author or year are flagged only if the title also lacks “safe” domain words (a curated list of 30+ terms across English, French, German, Spanish, Chinese, and Japanese).
  2. No abstract + irrelevant title: Papers with abstracts shorter than 50 characters whose titles lack safe domain words.
  3. Title blacklist: Papers whose titles contain noise terms (e.g., “blockchain,” “cryptocurrency,” “deep learning,” “metaverse”) but no safe domain words.
  4. Citation isolation: Papers published before 2020 that are neither cited by nor citing any other paper in the corpus (requires citations.csv; skipped when stale).
  5. Semantic outlier: Papers whose embedding cosine distance from the corpus centroid exceeds mean + 2 standard deviations (requires embeddings.npy).

An abstract relevance check tests whether at least 2 of 4 concept groups (climate, finance, development, environment) appear in the text.

Phase B: Protection

Papers are protected from removal if they meet any of: cited_by_count >= 50, appear in 2+ sources, are cited within the corpus, or appear in 2+ teaching syllabi.

Phase C: Verification

  • Blacklist validation: Confirms all noise-term matches in titles are properly caught.
  • LLM audit: A stratified random sample of 50 flagged and 50 unflagged papers is submitted to google/gemma-2-27b-it via OpenRouter. Each paper is classified as relevant or irrelevant to climate finance. Type I error rate (flagged but LLM-relevant) and Type II error rate (unflagged but LLM-irrelevant) are reported.

Phase D: Filtering

Flagged, non-protected papers are removed. An audit trail (corpus_audit.csv) records the decision for every paper.

Result: The refined corpus contains 22,113 papers in refined_works.csv.

Embedding generation

3. Embedding Generation

Script: scripts/analyze_embeddings.py

Model: paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2 (sentence-transformers library). This is a 12-layer multilingual BERT producing 384-dimensional vectors, chosen for its ability to place abstracts in English, French, Chinese, Japanese, and German into a shared semantic space.

Input selection: Papers from refined_works.csv with an abstract longer than 50 characters and a publication year between 1990 and 2025.

Encoding parameters: - Batch size: 256 - Normalization: L2-normalized embeddings - Runtime: approximately 16 minutes on CPU for ~18,800 abstracts

Output: embeddings.npy – a NumPy array of shape (18,798 x 384), cached for reuse by downstream scripts. A size-consistency check ensures the embedding count matches the filtered DataFrame row count.

Additional outputs: - UMAP projection (n_components=2, n_neighbors=15, min_dist=0.05, cosine metric, random_state=42) - KMeans clustering (k=6, n_init=20, random_state=42) on UMAP coordinates - Cluster assignments saved to semantic_clusters.csv

Technical Validation

Deduplication effectiveness

The merge pipeline reduces raw records through two passes:

  1. DOI-based deduplication normalizes DOIs and merges records sharing the same DOI, retaining the maximum citation count and best metadata by source priority.
  2. Title+year deduplication catches records lacking DOIs by matching on normalized title and publication year.

The source_count field tracks multi-source agreement: works appearing in 2+ independent sources have higher confidence. Of the 22,113 refined works, 3,847 (17.4%) appear in multiple sources.

LLM audit

A stratified random sample of 100 works (50 flagged, 50 unflagged) was evaluated by google/gemma-2-27b-it via OpenRouter. Each work was classified as relevant or irrelevant to climate finance based on its title and abstract.

  • Type I error (flagged but LLM-relevant): indicates over-aggressive filtering
  • Type II error (unflagged but LLM-irrelevant): indicates under-filtering

Both error rates are reported in the refinement script’s output and in corpus_audit.csv. The LLM audit serves as an independent check on the rule-based flagging pipeline, not as a ground truth, since relevance to “climate finance” is itself a contested category.

Coverage analysis

The seven-source design provides complementary coverage:

  • OpenAlex provides the broadest English-language academic coverage
  • ISTEX captures French-language literature from the national archive
  • bibCNRS adds non-English academic literature (Chinese, Japanese, German)
  • Grey literature includes institutional reports (OECD, UNFCCC, World Bank, CPI) that are central to the field but absent from academic databases
  • Teaching canon ensures that pedagogically canonical works are represented

Embedding quality

The multilingual sentence-transformer model (paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2) places abstracts in English, French, Chinese, Japanese, and German into a shared 384-dimensional semantic space. Embedding quality was validated through:

  • UMAP visualization: 2D projection shows coherent thematic clusters
  • KMeans clustering (k=6): produces interpretable thematic groups (CDM/carbon markets, green finance, climate action/adaptation, renewable energy, carbon trading/forestry, social/water management)
  • Downstream analyses: structural break detection and bimodality testing produce substantively interpretable results, consistent across embedding-based and independent TF-IDF-based methods (embedding–lexical correlation r = 0.683 on the efficiency–accountability axis)

Cross-machine reproducibility

Figures that do not involve KMeans clustering (fig1_emergence, fig4_genealogy, fig4_seed_axis_core, figA_1a_robustness) are byte-identical across machines when PYTHONHASHSEED=0 and SOURCE_DATE_EPOCH=0 are set (the Makefile exports both).

Figures that depend on KMeans (fig2_breakpoints, fig3_alluvial, fig5a_bimodality, and their core variants) may differ across machines. This is because scikit-learn’s KMeans delegates to platform-specific BLAS routines (OpenBLAS, MKL, Apple Accelerate), and floating-point summation order in distance computations is not guaranteed across implementations. The resulting cluster assignments can differ at the margin, producing visually similar but not byte-identical figures. Substantive results (breakpoint years, ΔBIC values, period boundaries) are robust to these differences.

Non-reproducible steps

  • ISTEX corpus download (requires institutional access)
  • bibCNRS export (requires CNRS Janus credentials, manual browser export)
  • Citation enrichment timing may vary due to Crossref index updates
  • LLM audit (requires OPENROUTER_API_KEY; can be skipped with --skip-llm)
  • RePEc mirror requires rsync access to rsync.repec.org

All scripts support a --no-pdf flag to skip PDF generation and produce PNG only.

Suggested applications

The corpus and embeddings support several research directions:

  • Structural break detection: the sliding-window divergence method described in the companion technical report detects endogenous breakpoints in the field’s semantic evolution
  • Bimodality and polarization analysis: embedding-based and lexical axes reveal the efficiency–accountability divide structuring the field
  • Topic modelling: the embeddings serve as input for BERTopic, top2vec, or other embedding-based topic models
  • Citation network analysis: citations.csv enables co-citation, bibliographic coupling, and genealogy studies
  • Cross-lingual comparison: the multilingual embeddings place non-English works in the same semantic space as English ones, enabling cross-lingual clustering
  • Temporal evolution: the 35-year span (1990–2025) supports diachronic analyses of concept emergence, vocabulary change, and field structure dynamics

Limitations

  • Non-English coverage, while deliberate, remains limited (8.6% of corpus). Studies requiring deep coverage of specific non-English traditions should supplement this corpus with targeted searches.
  • Citation counts reflect OpenAlex data as of the collection date and are biased toward older and English-language works.
  • The LLM audit provides a probabilistic check, not a definitive relevance judgment. Users with strict inclusion criteria should apply their own filtering.
  • Grey literature records often lack abstracts, limiting their utility for embedding-based analyses.
