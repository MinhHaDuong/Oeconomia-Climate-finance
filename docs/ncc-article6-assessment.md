# Article 6 and the NCC piece: assessment

## 1. Article 6 status: what is resolved, what is not

### Resolved (formally)

**Rulebook complete (COP29, Baku, November 2024).** After nearly a decade of negotiation, COP29 finalised the rules for all three Article 6 sub-paragraphs:

- **6.2 (bilateral cooperative approaches):** Countries may trade Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes (ITMOs) under bilateral agreements. As of early 2026, 106 bilateral arrangements exist between 59+ countries, but only a handful of actual ITMO transfers have been completed (the first: Switzerland-Thailand, January 2024).
- **6.4 (Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism, PACM):** The UN-supervised successor to the CDM. The Supervisory Body adopted five methodological standards in 2025 (baseline-setting, additionality, leakage, suppressed demand, non-permanence). Carbon removals confirmed eligible. CDM wind-down by end of 2026, with USD 26.8M transferred to PACM (decided at COP30, November 2025).
- **6.8 (non-market approaches):** The Glasgow Committee on Non-market Approaches (GCNMA) is in Phase 2 (2025-2026): implementing the work programme, building capacity, broadening stakeholder engagement. An NMA Platform is operational.

### Unresolved (substantively)

Despite the formal "full operationalisation" announced at COP29, serious implementation gaps persist:

1. **Corresponding adjustments remain untested at scale.** The mechanism for avoiding double counting (host country deducts sold credits from its NDC; buyer adds them) depends on national registries that most countries have not built. Only 13 of 85 countries with readiness activities have both authorization and tracking in place. The first Technical Expert Reviews of Initial Reports were published only in May 2025.

2. **CDM transition threatens quality.** The rush to migrate CDM projects into PACM before the 2026 deadline creates a loophole: projects can use old CDM methodologies until their current crediting period ends. Carbon Market Watch (April 2025) found the first approved transition project plans to issue 26x more carbon credits than scientifically justified. The additionality standards that took years to negotiate risk being undermined by the transition pathway.

3. **Article 6.4 has issued zero credits.** The first issuance of an Article 6.4 credit, originally expected by mid-2025, was pushed to early 2026. The mechanism exists on paper but not in practice.

4. **Environmental integrity is structural, not procedural.** The fundamental tension --- whether carbon markets deliver real emission reductions or license continued emissions through accounting arbitrage --- has not been resolved by the rulebook. It has been deferred to implementation, where national interests, capacity gaps, and information asymmetries will determine outcomes.

5. **Article 6.8 remains marginal.** Non-market approaches lack the political and economic momentum of the market mechanisms. Phase 2 activities are capacity-building workshops, not operational programmes.

## 2. Mapping to the efficiency-accountability axis

Article 6 maps directly onto the efficiency-accountability divide detected in the corpus:

| Article 6 component | Efficiency pole | Accountability pole |
|---------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| 6.2 bilateral ITMOs | Cost-effective mitigation; market-based price discovery; leverage of private capital through compliance demand | Double counting risk; corresponding adjustments as accounting fiction; sovereignty over NDC integrity |
| 6.4 PACM | Successor to CDM; private sector participation; scalable project-based crediting | Additionality testing (was the CDM project additional?); environmental integrity; quality vs quantity |
| 6.8 non-market | (Less relevant: alternative to market logic) | Finance, technology transfer, capacity building without commodification of emission reductions |

The Article 6 debate reproduces the same bifurcation visible in the broader climate finance literature. The efficiency pole argues markets deliver cost-effective mitigation and mobilise private capital; the accountability pole argues they enable double counting, undermine additionality, and defer real action. This is not a coincidence --- it is the same conceptual infrastructure, inherited from the Kyoto CDM debates, operating within categories set during crystallization.

**Key observation:** The vocabulary of Article 6 --- additionality, double counting, corresponding adjustments, environmental integrity, baseline, crediting period --- is the vocabulary of the CDM, lightly updated. The corpus evidence (manuscript §2.3, companion paper §5.3) shows CDM/Kyoto vocabulary contracting sharply after 2012 in the thematic composition. But the *conceptual categories* underlying Article 6 are the same ones. What changed was the institutional wrapper (UNFCCC Supervisory Body replacing CDM Executive Board), not the analytical framework.

## 3. Is Article 6 stasis evidence of category lock-in?

**Both, but the analytical distinction matters.**

Article 6's slow operationalisation has two drivers that are partially independent:

**Category lock-in (supports the NCC argument).** The Article 6 negotiations were fought within categories inherited from Kyoto: additionality, baseline-and-credit, corresponding adjustments, environmental integrity. These are the same concepts the CDM used. The Paris Agreement tried to redesign the market mechanism (from top-down Kyoto targets to bottom-up NDCs), but the conceptual toolkit remained unchanged. The negotiations stalled for a decade not because the problems were new but because they were the same problems that the CDM never solved --- and the available categories did not permit a fundamentally different solution. This is direct evidence that even in the formal negotiation process (not just scholarship), Copenhagen-era categories constrained what was thinkable.

**Institutional/political failure (separate from category lock-in).** Article 6 was also delayed by geopolitics: Brazil's insistence on avoiding corresponding adjustments for voluntary market credits, the EU's demand for high environmental integrity standards, the CDM's legacy interests, and the sheer complexity of building national registries in 100+ countries. These are political failures that would exist regardless of whether the conceptual categories were locked in.

**For the NCC piece:** The analytically useful claim is not "Article 6 proves categories are locked in" (too strong) but "Article 6 illustrates what category stability looks like in practice: a decade of negotiation that recycled Kyoto-era concepts without producing new ones." The distinction between category lock-in and political failure is itself informative --- the two reinforce each other in a way that makes the categories self-perpetuating.

## 4. Recommendation: Include, as a single illustrative sentence or clause --- not a paragraph

### Reasoning

**Arguments for inclusion:**
- Article 6 is the most concrete example of Copenhagen-era categories constraining post-Paris governance. The CDM vocabulary (additionality, baseline, crediting period, double counting) migrated directly into Article 6.4 with minimal conceptual innovation.
- It strengthens the "even the negotiations couldn't escape Copenhagen's categories" line of argument.
- NCC readers (climate policy researchers, governance scholars) will immediately recognise Article 6 as a live policy problem and connect it to the bibliometric finding.

**Arguments for exclusion (as a developed section):**
- Article 6 is a moving target: COP29 finalised the rulebook, COP30 wound down the CDM, implementation is ongoing. Any detailed account risks being outdated by the time the piece is published.
- In 2,000 words with 4 figures, there is no room for an Article 6 exposition. The piece must lead with the finding (no structural break), explain the method briefly, show the figures, and deliver the policy implication. Adding Article 6 detail would crowd out the core argument.
- The NCC piece is about *scholarship* (what the corpus shows), not about *negotiations* (what the COPs decided). Article 6 is a negotiation story. Blurring the two weakens the methodological contribution.
- The manuscript already handles Article 6 implicitly: §2.3 discusses CDM vocabulary migration, §3.2 covers the four post-Paris controversies (all fought within crystallization-era categories), and the conclusion traces the trajectory from Article 4.3 through CDM to the NCQG. Adding an Article 6 angle to the NCC piece risks duplicating the Oeconomia paper's narrative.

### Specific recommendation

**Include Article 6 as a single illustrative clause within the policy-implications paragraph.** Do not develop it into its own section or even a full paragraph.

Suggested placement: in the final section of the NCC piece, where the policy implication of category stability is drawn. After establishing that the corpus shows no structural break at Paris, and that the efficiency-accountability divide sharpened but did not restructure:

> Even the formal negotiation process bears the mark of this stability: Article 6 of the Paris Agreement, which replaced the Kyoto Protocol's CDM, was negotiated for a decade using the same conceptual toolkit --- additionality, baseline-and-credit, corresponding adjustments --- that the CDM had introduced, and the first credits under the new mechanism have yet to be issued.

This does the following in one sentence:
- Names Article 6 (recognition for NCC readers)
- Connects it to CDM (the Kyoto-era origin of the categories)
- Notes the decade of negotiation (evidence of difficulty within fixed categories)
- Notes zero credits issued (evidence that the new mechanism hasn't yet generated new practice)
- Does not require explanation of 6.2/6.4/6.8, corresponding adjustments, or COP-by-COP history

**Do not:** devote a paragraph to Article 6 sub-paragraphs, COP outcomes, or implementation status. This would dilute the piece and shift it from bibliometric evidence to policy commentary.

## 6. Summary

Article 6 is genuine evidence that Copenhagen-era categories constrain post-Paris governance. But in a 2,000-word NCC Analysis piece, it is best used as a single illustrative clause --- not a developed argument. The piece's strength is its bibliometric finding; Article 6 is a policy-world corroboration, not the main evidence. One sentence, placed in the implications section, does the work without the cost.
