# Response to Reviewers

**Manuscript:** "Counting Climate Finance. How Economists Made a Governable Object (1990–2025)"
**Target journal:** *Œconomia — History / Methodology / Philosophy*
**Date:** 2026-03-10

We thank the three reviewers for their careful reading and constructive criticism. All three recommend major revision while considering the paper publishable after revision. We have undertaken a substantial revision addressing the convergent diagnoses. Below we describe the changes made, organised by the issues raised.

---

## 1. Theoretical framework integrated into narrative (R1, R3 — HIGH)

**Diagnosis:** Section 4 read as a retrospective gloss rather than structuring the narrative. Theory was "layered on, not integrated" (R1). The paper should "weave theoretical analysis into historical sections" (R3).

**Changes made:**

- **§1.2** now explicitly names the DAC statistical conventions as a pre-existing system of *commensuration* in Desrosières's sense, before the Rio markers are discussed.
- **§2.1** now identifies the Copenhagen $100 billion commitment as *performative in Callon's sense* at the moment it appears in the narrative, rather than deferring the theoretical label to Section 4.
- **§2.2** now frames the policy professionals who built the measurement infrastructure as a case of *economization* (Çalışkan & Callon), naming the concept as the institutional process unfolds.
- **§3.2** now identifies the Oxfam Shadow Reports as *boundary work* (Gieryn) at the point where they enter the story.
- **Section 4** has been condensed from approximately 1,500 words to approximately 800 words. It now serves as a synthetic recapitulation rather than a first exposition. Sections 4.1 and 4.3 (commensuration and economization) have been merged, recognising their substantial overlap. Boundary work and boundary objects are now treated in a single subsection (§4.3). The constitutive-tension argument (§4.4, formerly §4.5) is preserved intact.
- The **road map paragraph** in the introduction has been updated to reflect that Section 4 "draws together the theoretical threads" running through the historical narrative.

**Net effect:** approximately 700 words saved, allowing space for the additions below.

---

## 2. Performativity used imprecisely (R1, R3 — HIGH)

**Diagnosis:** The paper mixed Austin's speech-act theory, Callon's economic performativity, and MacKenzie's Barnesian performativity without distinguishing them (R3). The "speech act" framing in §2.1 was misleading.

**Changes made:**

- **§2.1:** "was, in the language of performativity theory, a speech act that called into being the very object it promised to deliver" has been replaced with "was performative in Callon's sense: a declaration that, by triggering the construction of measurement infrastructure, brought into existence the very object it promised to deliver."
- **§4.2:** now opens with an explicit clarification: "We use the term in Callon's sense: economic categories and devices do not merely describe markets but actively format them. This differs from Austin's linguistic performativity (utterances that constitute institutional facts) and from MacKenzie's Barnesian performativity (models whose adoption makes the world conform to their predictions). The mechanism here is institutional rather than market-based."

---

## 3. "Economists as architects" overstated (R1, R2 — HIGH)

**Diagnosis:** The paper treated developing country negotiators, NGOs, and the Green Climate Fund as reactive rather than constitutive. The *demand* for quantification came from the G77+China, not just from OECD supply (R2).

**Changes made:**

- **§2.4:** the key paragraph now explicitly acknowledges that "the *demand* for quantification came from G77+China negotiators and civil-society organisations who insisted that pledges be measurable and verifiable, and the political targets themselves were set by heads of state." Economists built the instruments, but the mandate came from elsewhere.
- **§3.1:** a new passage notes the MDB joint climate finance tracking methodology (2011+) and the Green Climate Fund's own investment framework and results measurement system as competing commensurations that coexisted with the OECD/DAC framework without displacing it.

---

## 4. "No break at Paris" needs qualification (R2 — MEDIUM)

**Diagnosis:** The paper conflated semantic stability with institutional change. The Enhanced Transparency Framework and the TCFD represent genuine governance shifts (R2).

**Changes made:**

- **§3.4** now opens with an explicit qualification: "A qualification is essential: this finding concerns *vocabulary and measurement categories*, not institutional architecture. The Enhanced Transparency Framework moved climate finance reporting from voluntary to mandatory; the TCFD (2017) extended climate-finance categories into the domain of financial-risk regulation—a genuinely new application not prefigured during crystallization. What persisted was the conceptual infrastructure: the categories through which flows are classified, counted, and contested."

---

## 5. Missing literature (R1, R3 — MEDIUM)

**Diagnosis:** Governance-by-indicators literature absent (R3). Boundary objects (Star & Griesemer) a better fit than Gieryn alone (R3). Additionality review needs updating (R2).

**Changes made:**

Three references added:

- **Star & Griesemer (1989)** on boundary objects, cited in the new §4.3 alongside Gieryn. Climate finance categories are now characterised as boundary objects "shared across communities with different interpretations while maintaining enough identity to coordinate action."
- **Merry (2016)**, *The Seductions of Quantification*, added to the commensuration discussion in §4.1.
- **Weikmans & Roberts (2019)** on the climate finance accounting muddle, added alongside Michaelowa (2007) in §3.2.

We deliberately limited additions to three references to avoid diluting the bibliography (now 50 entries for a ~8,400-word paper). The broader governance-by-indicators literature (Rottenburg et al. 2015, Davis et al. 2012) is relevant but not essential to the argument; we would add it if reviewers specifically request it.

---

## 6. Computational method needs reflexive discussion (R1, R3 — MEDIUM)

**Diagnosis:** The corpus analysis presents algorithmic pattern detection as objective evidence, but from an STS perspective this is itself a commensuration (R3). The paper should either reflexively interrogate the method or articulate a position justifying the combination.

**Changes made:**

- The **introduction** (methodological paragraph) now includes: "A reflexive note is warranted: our own method—transforming 22,000 heterogeneous texts into vector representations and detecting statistical patterns—is itself an act of commensuration in Espeland and Stevens's sense. The embedding space renders comparable what was written in different languages, disciplines, and institutional contexts. We do not treat its outputs as objective descriptions of an independent intellectual landscape but as one more measurement apparatus whose conventions shape what it makes visible."

## Issues deferred

The following suggestions from reviewers have been noted but not addressed in this revision. We would address them in a subsequent round if reviewers consider them essential:

- **Deeper prosopography** of individual economists (R1): reconstructing specific episodes (Rio marker design, AGF deliberations) from primary sources would require archival work beyond the scope of this revision.
- **Francophone literature** (R1): Canfin & Grandjean, Aglietta & Espagne — relevant for a bilingual journal but not essential to the argument.
- **Timeline figure** mapping institutional moments against intellectual interventions (R1).
- **Foucauldian grounding** for "governable object" in the title (R3): we believe the term is used in its ordinary English sense rather than as a technical deployment of Miller & Rose's governmentality framework, but we could make this explicit if requested.
- **Negishi weights genealogy** for Œconomia readers (R1): a brief footnote could be added.
