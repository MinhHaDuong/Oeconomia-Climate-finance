## "Inventing Climate Finance: From Incremental Costs to Strategic Ambiguity"
### Nice, May 26-29, 2026 — 20-minute presentation (~15 slides)

## Slide 1: Title slide

- **Inventing Climate Finance**
- *From Incremental Costs to Strategic Ambiguity*
- Minh Ha-Duong — CNRS / CIRED

- Paper submitted to *Oeconomia*

## Slide 2: Opening provocation — the $300 billion problem

- COP29, Baku, November 2024: developed countries commit to $300 billion/year for climate action by 2035
- No institution holds this sum. No ledger records it. No methodology for counting it commands consensus.
- Same was true of the $100 billion pledged at Copenhagen in 2009
- **Central question:** Before you can argue about whether enough climate finance exists, someone had to make it into a countable object. How was that done, by whom, and with what consequences?

---

## Slide 3: Historiographical positioning

- Existing history of climate economics focuses on *modelling* (Pottier 2016 on cost-benefit, Aykut & Dahan 2015 on IAMs)
- "What should the carbon price be?" — not "does this dollar count as climate finance?"
- These are different kinds of economic work: not optimisation but *commensuration* (Espeland & Stevens 1998)
- Political economy literature documents *what is at stake* in measurement disputes — but takes the categories as given
- This paper asks the prior question: **how were the categories constructed, by whom, through what intellectual operations?**
- Theoretical tools: sociology of quantification (Desrosières, Porter), performativity (Callon, MacKenzie), economization (Calışkan & Callon)

---

## Slide 4: Method — using the corpus to discipline the narrative

- Computational analysis of ~7,500 works on climate finance (1990-2024)
- Six sources: OpenAlex, ISTEX (French archive), grey literature (OECD, World Bank, UNFCCC), university syllabi, bibCNRS, SciSpace
- Eight target languages; 48 detected in the corpus
- Sentence-transformer embeddings (BGE-M3, 1024-dimensional), co-citation analysis, unsupervised clustering
- **The computation corroborates the historical narrative; it does not generate it**
- Reflexive note: our own method is itself an act of commensuration — we render comparable what was written in different languages, disciplines, and institutional contexts

## Slide 5: Three-act periodization [FIGURE: publication volume bar chart]

- **Act I: Prehistory (1990-2006)** — "climate finance" does not yet exist as an economic object; fewer than 50 works/year
- **Act II: Crystallization (2007-2014)** — triggered by the Copenhagen commitment; the object is made
- **Act III: Disputes (2015-2024)** — all subsequent controversies fought within the categories established during crystallization
- Co-citation evidence: three distinct intellectual communities before 2007 with almost no shared edges (modularity = 0.68)
- The field was *assembled* from pre-existing traditions that had remarkably little contact

---

## Slide 6: Act I — Three traditions without intersection

- **Environmental economics** (Nordhaus, Stern): cost-benefit logic, carbon pricing, optimal abatement — but no concept of North-South *flows*
- **Development economics** (OECD DAC): ODA statistical infrastructure, concessionality thresholds, Rio markers (1998) — a readymade measurement apparatus, but designed for aid, not climate
- **International governance** (UNFCCC Art. 4.3, Kyoto): differentiated responsibilities, norm diffusion — but operating at a level of abstraction that made financial flows invisible
- Article 4.3 (1992): precise, transactional, incremental-cost logic — *"agreed full incremental costs"* — "finance" does not even appear
- Climate finance required the *intersection* of these traditions. That intersection was Copenhagen.

---

## Slide 7: The Copenhagen moment — institutional performativity

- December 2009: developed countries commit to "mobilising $100 billion per year" by 2020
- The figure was not derived from any model or needs assessment — it emerged from last-minute diplomatic bargaining
- Callon's performativity, but *institutional* rather than market-based: a declaration that, by triggering measurement infrastructure, brought into existence the very object it promised to deliver
- Before Copenhagen: "climate finance" = loose reference to various climate-related flows
- After Copenhagen: a specific, politically binding quantity that must be defined, measured, tracked, reported
- Decisive rupture from Article 4.3's incremental-cost logic: the pledge specified neither which costs were covered nor what the term meant

---

## Slide 8: Act II — Economists as institutional architects

- Policy professionals embedded in international organisations, not academic theorists (Calışkan & Callon's "economization")
- **Jan Corfee-Morlot (OECD):** redesigned Rio markers from binary tag to three-tier system — the de facto standard for counting bilateral climate finance; ambiguity embedded structurally (no agreed coefficient for "significant" projects)
- **UN Advisory Group on Climate Finance (2010):** Nicholas Stern as member; produces sources/instruments/governance tripartite framework; politically sensitive questions left strategically ambiguous
- **Jean-Charles Hourcade (CIRED):** "financial paradox" framing — public climate finance as correction of financial market failure, not aid
- **OECD Research Collaborative (2013-2015):** small expert group resolves attribution question through committee work; choices become binding statistical conventions

---

## Slide 9: The two vocabularies of crystallization

- **Efficiency pole:** leverage ratios, blended finance, de-risking, crowding-in, mobilised private finance — from OECD working papers, development bank reports
- **Accountability pole:** additionality, double counting, grant-equivalent, new and additional — from civil-society critique (Oxfam 2012), academic political economy (Michaelowa 2007, Roberts & Weikmans 2017)
- The two vocabularies emerged in *direct reaction to each other* — not independent inventions
- By 2014: each pole had its own lexicon, institutional base, and flagship metric
- **Corpus evidence [FIGURE: thematic composition chart]:** all six thematic clusters established by 2013; CDM vocabulary contracts sharply; mobilisation/green finance vocabulary expands

---

## Slide 10: Act III — Disputes within stable categories

- Paris Agreement, Art. 9 (2015): "financial resources" — as if the category required no further definition
- The accounting infrastructure built during crystallization did its work; now the term refers to something everyone can name, even if no one can measure it the same way
- Four recurrent controversies — all anchored in the categories established 2007-2014:
  1. Concessional loans at face value vs. grant-equivalent
  2. Rio markers: self-assessed, no external verification, systematically inflated
  3. Attribution of mobilised private finance — counterfactual reasoning on which public intervention "triggered" private capital
  4. Additionality: is this "new" finance or re-labelled ODA?
- **OECD 2022:** $100 billion target met ($115.9bn). **Oxfam 2022:** climate-specific net assistance ~$21-24 billion. Same data. Different conventions.

---

## Slide 11: Counter-expertise as boundary work

- Oxfam's Shadow Reports (annual since 2016): civil-society economists using the same data sources with different accounting conventions
- This is *boundary work* (Gieryn 1983): not disputing facts but disputing which commensuration is legitimate
- Climate finance categories function as *boundary objects* (Star & Griesemer 1989): shared across communities with different interpretations, yet maintaining enough identity to coordinate action
- Dahan (2010) showed climate models function as boundary objects across science and governance; accounting categories operate the same way
- **The two poles are institutionally embedded:** efficiency pole in finance and energy journals; accountability pole in climate governance and environmental policy venues; *Climate Policy* serves as shared ground

---

## Slide 12: The structural tension is constitutive [FIGURE: thematic composition, post-2015]

- Corpus finding: the efficiency/accountability divide sharpened markedly after 2015 — the field polarised along an axis latent in the crystallization-era categories
- Two distinct concentrations in the publication landscape, not a single shifting mass
- Growth was asymmetric: efficiency pole publications multiplied faster; but the accountability pole did not thin out — it diversified into loss-and-damage, climate debt, just transition
- **The tension is constitutive, not pathological.** If either pole prevailed, climate finance would lose its capacity to sustain both technocratic management and political accountability simultaneously
- The two-pole structure detected computationally is the scholarly trace of a structural feature of the governance object itself

---

## Slide 13: The conclusion — strategic ambiguity

- The economists who built the measurement infrastructure did not simply *fail* to define their object
- They *abandoned* a rigorous definition that was available — the incremental-cost framework of Article 4.3
- Why? Because ambiguity proved more *productive* than precision
- A precise definition would have constrained the countable total, limiting what donors could claim and what recipients could demand
- The deliberately vague category served opposing interests simultaneously:
  - Donors: count concessional loans, guarantees, mobilised private investment
  - Recipients: argue that even these inflated totals fall short
- **Climate finance became governable not despite but through the theoretical incoherence of the category — an incoherence that was not a deficiency to be repaired but a political resource to be exploited**

---

## Slide 14: Contribution and implications for HES

- **Historiographical:** shifts attention from models to accounting conventions; from carbon pricing to commensuration
- **Methodological:** bibliometrics as intellectual history — computational patterns discipline a narrative built on primary sources; each method is reflexively a form of commensuration
- **Theoretical:** institutional performativity (not Barnesian market performativity); economization through bureaucratic procedure rather than market design
- **For the sociology of quantification:** Desrosières' "statistical conventions" are not only stabilised by forgetting their origins — they can be deliberately made ambiguous to maintain political viability
- **Open question:** Will the next generation return to the precision of Article 4.3, or does the productive ambiguity prove durable? The NCQG negotiations at Baku suggest the latter.

---

## Slide 15: Open questions / for discussion

- The developed/developing binary, frozen in 1992, was never updated despite dramatic shifts in global economy — the most consequential yet least examined construction in climate finance governance
- The account of economists as institutional architects rests on published outputs — archival research and oral history remain to be done
- The intellectual history of climate finance as seen from *recipient countries* is largely unwritten
- Loss and damage: genuinely new category (neither mitigation nor adaptation, raises liability) — will it generate its own measurement infrastructure or be absorbed into existing categories?
- **Methodological invitation:** can the bibliometric corpus built for this paper serve the community? Data and code deposited at Zenodo (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19097045), CC BY 4.0
