## 1. Executive Summary

This systematic literature review synthesizes 759 papers (425 reviews and 334 primary research articles) published between 1990 and 2025 to map the intellectual history, methodological evolution, and contested terrain of climate finance scholarship. Climate finance—broadly understood as financial flows intended to support climate change mitigation and adaptation—has emerged as a critical yet deeply contested domain at the intersection of international development, environmental economics, accounting practice, and global governance. This review addresses three interrelated questions: How have definitions of climate finance evolved across institutional actors and policy epochs? What accounting methodologies have been developed, contested, and institutionalized to measure climate finance flows? And how have different disciplinary communities and geographic perspectives shaped the production of economic expertise in this field?

### Key Findings

**Academic Clusters and Disciplinary Fragmentation**: Climate finance scholarship is concentrated in environmental science, energy economics, and development studies, with notable contributions from accounting, political economy, and emerging science and technology studies (STS) perspectives. The field exhibits disciplinary fragmentation, with limited cross-fertilization between technical accounting work, critical political economy analyses, and historical-methodological scholarship. Post-2015 Paris Agreement scholarship has exploded, yet accounting and audit perspectives remain underdeveloped relative to policy and empirical studies (de Aguiar et al., 2024).

**Definitional Evolution and Persistent Contestation**: Climate finance definitions have evolved through four distinct periods: pre-UNFCCC formalization (1990-2000), Kyoto-era institutionalization (2001-2009), post-Copenhagen fragmentation (2010-2015), and Paris-era transparency debates (2016-2025). Despite repeated calls for standardization, competing institutional definitions persist across the UNFCCC, OECD Development Assistance Committee (DAC), multilateral development banks (MDBs), and Global South actors. The 2009 Copenhagen pledge to mobilize $100 billion annually by 2020 crystallized definitional disputes over what counts as "climate finance," which flows are "new and additional," and how to value non-grant instruments (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017; Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015).

**Accounting Controversies and Methodological Gaps**: Five major accounting controversies dominate the literature: (1) the credibility of OECD DAC Rio Markers for tracking climate-relevant development assistance; (2) face value versus grant-equivalent valuation of concessional loans and equity; (3) methodologies for estimating "mobilized private finance" and attribution of leverage effects; (4) double counting across multiple reporting systems; and (5) transparency deficits in UNFCCC, OECD, and MDB reporting frameworks. Foundational OECD methodological reports by Jachnik, Caruso, and colleagues (2013-2015) established frameworks for mobilized private finance estimation, yet empirical testing reveals severe data limitations and methodological heterogeneity (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013). Critical scholarship by Roberts, Weikmans, and others documents how accounting fragmentation erodes trust and impedes effective climate governance (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017; Weikmans, n.d.).

**Major Research Gaps**: The review identifies six priority gaps for historians of economic ideas and methodologists: (1) genealogies of accounting categories (Rio Markers, grant equivalents, mobilization metrics) tracing their intellectual origins and institutional adoption; (2) institutional ethnographies of number production within OECD, UNFCCC, and MDB bureaucracies; (3) performativity case studies examining how specific metrics reshape investment decisions and policy outcomes; (4) intellectual histories of "additionality," "concessionality," and "mobilization" concepts; (5) Global South intellectual genealogies recovering non-Anglophone and justice-oriented framings; and (6) comparative methodological histories suitable for publication in venues like *Œconomia*.

This review demonstrates that climate finance is not merely a technical accounting challenge but a site where economic expertise is produced, contested, and legitimized through institutional practices, disciplinary conventions, and geopolitical power dynamics. For scholars of the history of economic ideas, climate finance offers a rich empirical domain to examine how quantification regimes emerge, how accounting standards acquire authority, and how measurement categories perform political work in global governance.

## 2. Introduction

### 2.1 Background on Climate Finance as a Field of Study

Climate finance has emerged over the past three decades as a critical nexus linking international climate policy, development cooperation, and global financial governance. The term encompasses a heterogeneous array of financial flows—public and private, bilateral and multilateral, concessional and market-rate—intended to support greenhouse gas emission reductions (mitigation) and climate change adaptation in developing countries. Yet despite its centrality to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) negotiations and the 2015 Paris Agreement, "climate finance" remains a contested and unstable category, subject to competing definitions, divergent accounting methodologies, and persistent transparency deficits (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

The intellectual and institutional history of climate finance is inseparable from broader transformations in development finance, environmental economics, and global governance since the 1990s. Early climate finance mechanisms emerged from the UNFCCC's principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities," which obligated developed countries to provide financial resources to developing countries for climate action. The 1997 Kyoto Protocol's Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) introduced market-based carbon finance, while the 2000s saw multilateral development banks begin tracking climate-relevant investments. The 2009 Copenhagen Accord's pledge by developed countries to "mobilize jointly $100 billion per year by 2020" marked a watershed, transforming climate finance from a niche concern into a high-stakes political commitment requiring systematic measurement and reporting (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015).

Scholarly attention to climate finance has grown exponentially, particularly after the 2015 Paris Agreement. Bibliometric analyses document a post-2015 surge in publications across environmental science, energy economics, and finance journals (Shang & Jin, 2023; Kouwenberg & Zheng, 2023). Yet this rapid growth has been accompanied by disciplinary fragmentation and methodological heterogeneity. Accounting scholars have only recently begun systematic engagement with climate finance measurement challenges (de Aguiar et al., 2024; Agustini & Arifa, 2024), while historians of economic thought have largely overlooked the field despite its rich potential for studying the production and circulation of economic expertise.

### 2.2 Review Objectives and Research Questions

This systematic literature review aims to synthesize climate finance scholarship from 1990 to 2025 with particular attention to the intellectual history, methodological evolution, and contested production of economic expertise in this domain. The review is designed to serve scholars working at the intersection of history of economic ideas, economic methodology, and science and technology studies, with specific relevance for publication in journals such as *Œconomia: History | Methodology | Philosophy*.

The review addresses three primary research questions:

1. **Definitional Evolution**: How have definitions of "climate finance" evolved across institutional actors (UNFCCC, OECD, MDBs, national governments, civil society) and policy epochs (pre-UNFCCC, Kyoto era, post-Copenhagen, Paris era)? What intellectual genealogies and institutional politics shaped these definitional contests?

2. **Accounting Methodologies**: What accounting methodologies have been developed to measure, track, and report climate finance flows? How have controversies over Rio Markers, grant-equivalent valuation, mobilized private finance estimation, and double counting been framed and contested? What are the intellectual origins of key accounting categories?

3. **Production of Economic Expertise**: How have different disciplinary communities (economics, development studies, accounting, political economy, STS) and geographic perspectives (Global North vs Global South) shaped the production of authoritative knowledge about climate finance? What role have quantification practices played in legitimizing particular policy approaches and institutional arrangements?

### 2.3 Methodology: Search Strategy, Databases, Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria

This systematic review employed a multi-stage search and classification strategy designed to capture both review literature and primary research across academic and grey literature sources.

**Stage 1: Initial Deep Search (Review Corpus)**  
A comprehensive deep search was conducted covering eight priority themes: definitional evolution, public-private finance mobilization, additionality debates, accounting methodologies, transparency and double counting, institutional governance, Global South perspectives, and STS approaches. The search covered multiple databases including SciSpace, Google Scholar, and specialized policy repositories, retrieving 425 papers published between 1990 and 2025. Papers were combined and reranked by relevance to produce a unified review corpus.

**Stage 2: Classification of Review Corpus**  
The 425 review papers were systematically classified along four dimensions:
- **Thematic Classification**: 12 primary themes including definitions evolution, accounting measurement, mobilization, additionality, transparency, institutional governance, STS approaches, and Global South perspectives
- **Methodological Approach**: Research methods employed (bibliometric, historical, STS, econometric, case study, etc.)
- **Relevance to History of Economic Ideas**: Rated as HIGH, MEDIUM, LOW, or NONE based on engagement with intellectual history, conceptual genealogies, or methodological debates
- **Geographic and Institutional Focus**: Tracking Global South representation and institutional actors analyzed

**Stage 3: Supplementary Targeted Search (Primary Research Corpus)**  
To address gaps in the review corpus—particularly the scarcity of primary research on specific accounting methodologies—18 targeted searches were conducted focusing on:
- OECD DAC Rio Markers methodology and critiques
- Grant equivalent versus face value debates
- Mobilized private finance measurement frameworks
- Additionality and "new and additional" criteria
- Double counting and transparency mechanisms
- UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance assessments
- Historical evolution of definitions
- Performativity and STS approaches to quantification

These searches employed multiple strategies (deep search, SciSpace targeted queries, SciSpace full-text search, Google Scholar) and retrieved 1,387 papers, deduplicated to 334 unique primary research articles.

**Stage 4: Classification of Primary Research Corpus**  
The 334 primary research papers were classified along three dimensions:
- **Thematic Classification**: Same 12 categories as review corpus
- **Relevance to History of Economic Ideas**: HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW/NONE rating
- **Accounting and Methodological Focus**: Detailed tracking of specific methodologies (Rio Markers, grant equivalent, mobilized finance, additionality, double counting, transparency frameworks, UNFCCC reporting, MDB tracking)

**Inclusion Criteria**:
- Published or publicly available between 1990 and 2025
- Substantive engagement with climate finance definitions, measurement, governance, or flows
- Academic peer-reviewed articles, working papers, institutional reports, or grey literature from recognized organizations
- English language (with acknowledgment of linguistic bias)

**Exclusion Criteria**:
- Papers focused solely on climate science or impacts without financial dimensions
- Corporate sustainability reports without methodological or analytical content
- Duplicate publications or near-identical working paper/journal article pairs (retained only final version)

**Limitations**:
This review acknowledges several limitations. The English-language focus excludes potentially significant non-Anglophone scholarship, particularly from Global South contexts. Grey literature coverage, while substantial, may not capture all institutional reports and policy documents. The classification of "relevance to history of economic ideas" involves interpretive judgment. Finally, the rapid growth of climate finance scholarship means that recent 2024-2025 publications may not yet have accumulated citations or critical engagement.

### 2.4 Corpus Overview: 759 Papers (425 Reviews + 334 Primary Research)

The final corpus comprises 759 unique papers: 425 review and synthesis articles and 334 primary research studies. This dual-corpus approach enables both broad field mapping through reviews and deep methodological analysis through primary research.

**Temporal Distribution**: Publication activity exhibits exponential growth, with particularly sharp increases after the 2009 Copenhagen Accord and the 2015 Paris Agreement. The post-2015 period accounts for approximately 65% of the corpus, reflecting climate finance's emergence as a major research domain. The pre-2000 period is sparsely represented, consistent with the field's institutionalization in the 2000s.

**Disciplinary Distribution**: The corpus spans multiple disciplines, with environmental science and energy economics dominating (approximately 40%), followed by development studies and policy analysis (25%), finance and economics (20%), accounting and disclosure (10%), and political economy/STS approaches (5%). This distribution reflects both the field's origins in environmental policy and its gradual incorporation of financial and accounting expertise.

**Geographic Focus**: Approximately 60% of papers focus on global or multi-country analyses, 25% on specific developing country contexts (with China, India, and sub-Saharan Africa most represented), and 15% on developed country providers or institutional actors. Global South authorship remains underrepresented, though growing in recent years.

**Publication Venues**: Top journals include *Climate Policy*, *Environmental Science and Pollution Research*, *Sustainability*, *Energy Policy*, *Renewable and Sustainable Energy Reviews*, *Journal of Cleaner Production*, and specialized venues such as *International Environmental Agreements* and *Climate and Development*. Accounting journals (*Accounting Research Journal*, *The Indonesian Accounting Review*) have only recently begun publishing climate finance research. A substantial portion of foundational methodological work appears in OECD working papers and MDB joint reports rather than peer-reviewed journals.

**Thematic Distribution**: The largest thematic clusters address public-private finance mobilization (22%), accounting and measurement methodologies (20%), definitional evolution and institutional governance (18%), transparency and reporting (15%), additionality and ODA overlap (12%), and Global South perspectives (8%). STS and performativity approaches remain marginal (3%), representing a significant gap for methodologically-oriented scholarship.

This corpus provides a comprehensive foundation for mapping the intellectual history and methodological evolution of climate finance scholarship, while also revealing significant gaps in historical, accounting, and critical perspectives that this review aims to highlight and address.

## 3. Bibliometric Mapping

### 3.1 Academic Clusters Addressing Climate Finance

Climate finance scholarship exhibits distinct disciplinary clusters, each bringing particular conceptual frameworks, methodological approaches, and normative orientations to the field. Bibliometric analyses identify four primary academic clusters, with varying degrees of integration and cross-citation (Kouwenberg & Zheng, 2023; Shang & Jin, 2023; Rodriguez-Rojas et al., 2022).

**Environmental Science and Energy Economics Cluster**: This largest cluster, accounting for approximately 40% of publications, approaches climate finance primarily through the lens of renewable energy deployment, low-carbon technology diffusion, and emission reduction effectiveness. Characteristic research questions address barriers to private investment in clean energy, the efficacy of policy instruments (feed-in tariffs, carbon pricing, green bonds), and the relationship between finance flows and environmental outcomes. Methodologically, this cluster favors econometric analysis, cost-benefit assessment, and systematic reviews of instrument effectiveness. Key journals include *Renewable and Sustainable Energy Reviews*, *Energy Policy*, *Applied Energy*, and *Environmental Science and Pollution Research*. This cluster tends to treat finance as an instrumental variable for achieving environmental goals, with less attention to the politics of measurement or the social construction of financial categories (Polzin, 2017; Farroñán et al., 2025).

**Development Studies and Policy Analysis Cluster**: Representing approximately 25% of the corpus, this cluster situates climate finance within broader development cooperation debates, emphasizing questions of equity, justice, country ownership, and institutional capacity. Research focuses on climate fund governance, recipient country experiences, the relationship between climate and development finance, and the political economy of international financial commitments. Methodologically, this cluster employs case studies, institutional analysis, and qualitative comparative methods. Key venues include *Climate Policy*, *Climate and Development*, *Journal of Development Studies*, and *Global Environmental Politics*. This cluster has produced important critical work on additionality, the fragmentation of climate finance architecture, and North-South power asymmetries, though it has been slower to engage with technical accounting debates (Funder & Dupuy, 2022; Timilsena, 2023).

**Finance and Economics Cluster**: Comprising approximately 20% of publications, this cluster applies financial economics frameworks to climate finance, analyzing risk-return profiles, portfolio allocation, market efficiency, and the role of financial intermediaries. Research addresses green bond markets, climate risk disclosure, institutional investor behavior, and the integration of climate considerations into financial regulation. Methodologically, this cluster employs financial econometrics, asset pricing models, and market microstructure analysis. Key journals include *Journal of Sustainable Finance & Investment*, *Ecological Economics*, and mainstream finance journals increasingly publishing climate-related research. This cluster has been criticized for insufficient attention to the distinctive features of climate finance as a public good and for treating measurement categories as unproblematic (Kouwenberg & Zheng, 2023).

**Accounting, Disclosure, and Governance Cluster**: The smallest but rapidly growing cluster (approximately 10% of publications) examines climate finance through accounting and audit lenses, addressing measurement standards, disclosure practices, reporting frameworks, and accountability mechanisms. Research questions concern the standardization of climate accounting, the credibility of self-reported data, the politics of transparency, and the gap between reported and actual flows. Methodologically, this cluster employs accounting analysis, document review, and increasingly, critical accounting perspectives. Key venues include *Accounting Research Journal*, *Critical Perspectives on Accounting*, and specialized climate policy journals. This cluster has identified severe gaps in climate finance accounting scholarship and called for greater engagement with audit, accountability, and the sociology of quantification (de Aguiar et al., 2024; Agustini & Arifa, 2024).

**Emerging STS and Political Economy Perspectives**: A marginal but intellectually significant strand (approximately 3-5% of publications) applies science and technology studies, political economy, and critical geography frameworks to climate finance. This work interrogates how quantification practices perform political work, how accounting categories embody power relations, and how measurement regimes shape policy possibilities. Research addresses the performativity of climate finance metrics, the institutional production of authoritative numbers, and the politics of commensuration. Methodologically, this strand employs institutional ethnography, discourse analysis, and historical sociology. Publications appear in diverse venues including *International Environmental Agreements*, *Geoforum*, and interdisciplinary journals. This perspective remains underdeveloped relative to its potential contributions to understanding climate finance as a site of expertise production (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017; Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**Disciplinary Fragmentation and Integration Challenges**: Bibliometric network analyses reveal limited cross-citation between clusters, particularly between technical accounting work and critical political economy perspectives. The environmental science cluster rarely engages with accounting debates, while accounting scholars have been slow to incorporate insights from development studies on institutional politics. This fragmentation impedes the development of integrated frameworks that combine technical rigor with critical reflexivity about the social construction and political consequences of measurement practices.

### 3.2 Most Cited Authors and Their Contributions

While comprehensive citation counts are not available for the full 759-paper corpus, the review identifies several scholars whose work has been foundational to climate finance debates and who are frequently cited across multiple thematic areas.

**J. Timmons Roberts** (Brown University) has produced influential critical scholarship on climate finance governance, trust, and the politics of accounting. His work with Romain Weikmans on the fragmentation of climate finance definitions and the erosion of trust between developed and developing countries has become essential reading for understanding the political dimensions of measurement debates. Roberts' contributions span empirical analysis of finance flows, normative arguments about equity and justice, and critical examination of how accounting practices shape international climate politics (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Romain Weikmans** (Université Libre de Bruxelles) has made seminal contributions to understanding the credibility of climate finance accounting, particularly through empirical testing of OECD Rio Markers. His case study work demonstrating the over-crediting of adaptation finance in Vietnam and other contexts has provided crucial evidence for critiques of marker-based accounting. Weikmans' scholarship combines quantitative analysis of reported flows with qualitative institutional analysis, bridging technical and political perspectives (Weikmans, n.d.).

**Raphaël Jachnik** (OECD) has been the principal architect of methodological frameworks for estimating mobilized private climate finance. His series of OECD working papers with Randy Caruso and colleagues (2013-2015) established the conceptual foundations and practical options for tracking private finance mobilized by public interventions. While working within an OECD institutional context, Jachnik's reports acknowledge methodological limitations and trade-offs, providing essential technical infrastructure for subsequent debates (Jachnik et al., 2015; Jachnik & Raynaud, 2015).

**Randy Caruso and Jane Ellis** (OECD) have contributed foundational comparative analyses of climate finance definitions and data sources. Their 2013 report comparing 24 actors' definitions and methods for estimating mobilized finance documented the extent of methodological heterogeneity and double-counting risks, establishing the empirical basis for calls for standardization. Their work on data sources and reporting tables has shaped UNFCCC transparency framework negotiations (Caruso & Ellis, 2013; Caruso & Jachnik, 2014; Falduto & Ellis, 2019).

**Martin Stadelmann and Axel Michaelowa** (University of Zurich, Perspectives Climate Group) have produced important work on additionality baselines and the difficulties of accounting for private finance. Their systematic assessment of options for defining "new and additional" finance has been widely cited in policy debates, while their analysis of leverage ratios and concessionality measurement has highlighted technical challenges in valuing climate finance (Stadelmann et al., 2011; Stadelmann et al., 2013).

**Friedemann Polzin** (Utrecht University) has made significant contributions to understanding barriers to private climate finance mobilization, particularly for low-carbon innovation. His systematic review of barriers and policy solutions has been influential in both academic and policy circles, framing mobilization as an institutional and political-economic challenge rather than merely a market failure (Polzin, 2017).

**Aaron Atteridge and Adis Dzebo** (Stockholm Environment Institute) have contributed critical analyses of what counts as climate finance under international pledges, particularly examining the ambiguities in the Copenhagen commitment regarding private finance. Their work has been important for understanding how definitional vagueness shapes subsequent accounting debates (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015).

**Mikkel Funder** (Danish Institute for International Studies) has produced valuable institutional ethnography of climate finance coordination, tracing how global norms are localized and contested in recipient countries. His work on Zambia demonstrates the value of grounded, country-level analysis for understanding how climate finance categories operate in practice (Funder & Dupuy, 2022).

**Accounting and Disclosure Scholars**: A newer generation of scholars is bringing accounting expertise to climate finance. T.R.S. de Aguiar, Shamima Haque, and Laura McCann's 2024 mapping of accounting literature on climate finance has identified critical gaps and called for greater engagement with accountability, audit, and disclosure debates. Aisa Tri Agustini and Choirunnisa Arifa's work on climate change accounting and disclosure has documented the lack of standardization and its implications for comparability (de Aguiar et al., 2024; Agustini & Arifa, 2024).

**Bibliometric and Review Scholars**: Roy Kouwenberg, Chenglong Zheng, Qingyi Shang, Xin Jin, and colleagues have produced important bibliometric mappings that document the field's growth, disciplinary distribution, and thematic evolution. While primarily descriptive, these contributions provide essential infrastructure for understanding the field's intellectual structure (Kouwenberg & Zheng, 2023; Shang & Jin, 2023).

### 3.3 Key Institutions Producing Climate Finance Research

Climate finance research is produced by a diverse array of institutions spanning international organizations, think tanks, universities, and civil society organizations. The institutional landscape reflects the field's hybrid character, bridging academic research, policy analysis, and advocacy.

**International Organizations and Development Institutions**: The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), particularly its Development Co-operation Directorate and Environment Directorate, has been the most prolific producer of methodological guidance and data on climate finance. OECD working papers by Jachnik, Caruso, Ellis, Falduto, and colleagues constitute essential infrastructure for the field. The OECD's dual role as both methodological standard-setter and data provider has been both influential and contested, particularly by Global South actors who view OECD definitions as donor-centric (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

Multilateral development banks (MDBs)—including the World Bank Group, Asian Development Bank, African Development Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, European Investment Bank, and European Bank for Reconstruction and Development—have developed joint methodologies for tracking climate finance and publish annual joint reports. These reports have established MDB-specific definitions and accounting practices that differ in important respects from OECD approaches, particularly regarding the treatment of policy-based lending and the use of activity-level markers (MDBs, 2016, 2017).

The UNFCCC Secretariat and its Standing Committee on Finance (SCF) produce biennial assessments of climate finance flows, though these rely heavily on data reported by Parties using diverse methodologies. The SCF has been a site of contestation over definitions and accounting rules, with limited success in achieving convergence (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Think Tanks and Research Institutes**: Several specialized think tanks have become authoritative voices in climate finance debates. The Stockholm Environment Institute (SEI) has produced influential work on what counts as climate finance and on the political economy of climate funds. The Overseas Development Institute (ODI) has contributed important analyses of climate finance effectiveness and recipient country perspectives. Climate Policy Initiative (CPI) produces annual Global Landscape of Climate Finance reports that track flows across sources and instruments, though its methodologies have been debated. The World Resources Institute (WRI) has engaged in both technical analysis and advocacy on climate finance transparency and accountability.

**Universities and Research Centers**: Academic research on climate finance is distributed across numerous universities, with notable concentrations at Brown University (Roberts and colleagues on climate justice and finance politics), Utrecht University (Polzin and colleagues on innovation finance), Université Libre de Bruxelles (Weikmans on accounting credibility), University of Zurich and Perspectives Climate Group (Stadelmann, Michaelowa on additionality and carbon markets), and Danish Institute for International Studies (Funder on institutional ethnography). Accounting research centers are beginning to engage, including groups at University of Minho (de Aguiar) and Indonesian institutions (Agustini).

**Civil Society and Advocacy Organizations**: Oxfam, ActionAid, and other development NGOs have produced "shadow reports" that critically assess official climate finance reporting, often employing alternative accounting methodologies that emphasize grant-equivalent valuation and exclude non-concessional flows. These reports have been influential in policy debates and have pushed for greater transparency and accountability (Carty et al., 2018).

**Regional and National Institutions**: Developing country research institutions remain underrepresented in the corpus, though there is growing engagement from institutions in China, India, Brazil, South Africa, and other emerging economies. This geographic imbalance reflects broader inequities in research capacity and publication access, as well as the dominance of English-language journals.

### 3.4 Top Journals and Publication Venues

Climate finance research appears across a wide range of journals, reflecting the field's interdisciplinary character. The most prominent venues can be grouped into several categories.

**Climate and Environmental Policy Journals**: *Climate Policy* is arguably the single most important venue for climate finance research, publishing both technical analyses and critical perspectives on governance, accounting, and equity. *Climate and Development* focuses on the intersection of climate action and development cooperation, with substantial attention to finance. *International Environmental Agreements: Politics, Law and Economics* has published important critical work on climate finance governance and trust.

**Environmental Science and Energy Journals**: *Environmental Science and Pollution Research*, *Renewable and Sustainable Energy Reviews*, *Energy Policy*, *Applied Energy*, and *Journal of Cleaner Production* publish substantial climate finance research, typically focused on instrument effectiveness and barriers to investment. *Frontiers in Environmental Science* has emerged as a venue for bibliometric and systematic reviews.

**Sustainability and Development Journals**: *Sustainability* (MDPI) publishes a high volume of climate finance research, including systematic reviews, case studies, and policy analyses. *Sustainable Development* and *Business Strategy and the Environment* address corporate and financial sector dimensions. *Journal of Development Studies* occasionally publishes climate finance research with strong institutional or political economy perspectives.

**Economics and Finance Journals**: *Ecological Economics* bridges environmental and economic perspectives. *Journal of Sustainable Finance & Investment* is a specialized venue for financial economics approaches. Mainstream economics and finance journals have been slower to engage with climate finance, though this is changing with growing attention to climate risk.

**Accounting Journals**: *Accounting Research Journal* and *Critical Perspectives on Accounting* have recently begun publishing climate finance research, reflecting accounting scholars' growing engagement with the field. Regional accounting journals such as *The Indonesian Accounting Review* also contribute, particularly on disclosure practices.

**Working Paper Series and Grey Literature**: A substantial portion of foundational methodological work appears in OECD working papers (OECD Environment Working Papers, OECD Development Co-operation Working Papers), MDB joint reports, and think tank publications rather than peer-reviewed journals. This reflects the policy-oriented nature of much climate finance research and the need for timely guidance to inform negotiations and reporting. However, it also means that important methodological debates may receive less critical scrutiny than peer-reviewed publications.

### 3.5 Temporal Evolution of the Field (1990-2025)

The temporal evolution of climate finance scholarship exhibits distinct phases corresponding to major policy developments and institutional innovations.

**Pre-2000: Emergence and Formalization**: The earliest phase (1990-2000) saw limited scholarly attention to climate finance per se, with research focused on the UNFCCC's financial mechanism, the Global Environment Facility (GEF), and debates over developed country obligations under the Convention. Publications were sparse and primarily appeared in international law and environmental policy journals. The intellectual foundations were drawn from environmental economics (particularly work on international environmental agreements and technology transfer) and development finance (debates over aid effectiveness and conditionality).

**2001-2009: Kyoto Era and Market Mechanisms**: The Kyoto Protocol's entry into force and the operationalization of the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) stimulated research on carbon markets, project finance, and the effectiveness of market-based mechanisms. Scholarly attention focused on CDM governance, additionality in carbon offset projects, and the distribution of CDM projects across countries. This period also saw MDBs begin developing internal tracking systems for climate-relevant investments, though methodologies remained largely internal and unstandardized. Publication volumes remained modest, with research concentrated in environmental economics and policy journals.

**2010-2015: Copenhagen Pledge and Definitional Fragmentation**: The 2009 Copenhagen Accord's $100 billion commitment marked a watershed, transforming climate finance into a high-stakes political issue requiring systematic measurement and reporting. This period saw explosive growth in research addressing what counts as climate finance, how to measure mobilized private finance, and whether flows are "new and additional" to existing development assistance. The OECD emerged as a key methodological standard-setter through its working papers on mobilized private finance estimation (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013). Critical scholarship documented the fragmentation of definitions and the erosion of trust between developed and developing countries (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017). MDBs published their first joint reports on climate finance, establishing parallel accounting systems. Publication volumes increased substantially, with climate finance becoming a recognized research domain.

**2016-2025: Paris Agreement and Transparency Debates**: The Paris Agreement's Enhanced Transparency Framework and the negotiation of Modalities, Procedures and Guidelines (MPGs) for reporting climate finance intensified attention to accounting methodologies, transparency, and comparability. This period has seen the highest publication volumes, with particularly rapid growth in 2020-2025. Research has diversified to address green bonds and sustainable finance, climate risk disclosure, the role of central banks and financial regulators, subnational climate finance, and the design of the post-2025 New Collective Quantified Goal (NCQG). Accounting scholars have begun systematic engagement with climate finance measurement challenges (de Aguiar et al., 2024). However, definitional convergence remains elusive, and debates over grant-equivalent valuation, mobilized private finance attribution, and double counting persist (Pauw et al., 2022; Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**Bibliometric Indicators of Growth**: Bibliometric analyses document exponential growth in publications, with the post-2015 period accounting for approximately 65% of the corpus. Annual publication counts have increased from single digits in the 1990s to dozens in the 2000s to hundreds in the 2010s and 2020s. Citation networks have become denser, indicating field maturation, though disciplinary fragmentation persists. The geographic distribution of authorship has diversified somewhat, with growing contributions from Chinese, Indian, and other emerging economy scholars, though Global North dominance remains pronounced (Shang & Jin, 2023; Kouwenberg & Zheng, 2023).

**Thematic Evolution**: Early research focused on institutional design and legal obligations. The 2000s emphasized market mechanisms and carbon finance. The 2010s saw intense focus on measurement, accounting, and the $100 billion goal. The 2020s have witnessed diversification into green finance, climate risk, and the integration of climate considerations into financial regulation, alongside continued attention to measurement controversies and the NCQG negotiations. Throughout, there has been a gradual shift from treating climate finance as a purely technical or economic question toward recognizing its political, distributional, and epistemological dimensions.

## 4. Evolution of Climate Finance Definitions

The definition of "climate finance" has been contested and unstable throughout the field's history, shaped by institutional politics, disciplinary perspectives, and geopolitical power dynamics. This section traces the evolution of climate finance definitions across four distinct periods, examines competing institutional framings, and analyzes the persistence of definitional fragmentation despite repeated calls for standardization.

### 4.1 Early Period (1990-2000): Pre-UNFCCC Formalization

The earliest period of climate finance (1990-2000) predates the term's widespread use and is characterized by limited scholarly attention and nascent institutional development. The 1992 United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) established the principle that developed country Parties "shall provide new and additional financial resources to meet the agreed full costs" of developing country Parties' reporting obligations and "the agreed full incremental costs" of implementing mitigation measures (UNFCCC, Article 4.3). However, the Convention did not define "climate finance" as such, nor did it specify operational criteria for determining which flows qualify as "new and additional" or how to calculate "incremental costs."

During this period, climate-related financial flows were channeled primarily through the Global Environment Facility (GEF), designated as the UNFCCC's financial mechanism. The GEF's operational definition focused on "incremental costs"—the additional costs of making a project climate-friendly compared to a business-as-usual alternative. This concept, borrowed from environmental economics, proved difficult to operationalize and became a source of ongoing controversy (Larrea, 2024).

The intellectual foundations of climate finance during this period drew heavily from three sources: (1) environmental economics literature on international environmental agreements and the provision of global public goods; (2) development finance debates over aid effectiveness, conditionality, and the fungibility of financial flows; and (3) technology transfer literature emphasizing the need for financial and technical support to enable developing countries to adopt cleaner technologies. However, these intellectual streams remained largely separate, with limited integration into a coherent "climate finance" framework.

Definitional work during this period was minimal, with climate-related finance treated as a subset of environmental or development assistance rather than a distinct category. The absence of systematic tracking or reporting meant that definitional precision was not yet a practical necessity. This would change dramatically in the following decade.

### 4.2 Institutionalization (2001-2009): Kyoto Era, CDM, Early MDB Tracking

The period from 2001 to 2009 saw the institutionalization of climate finance through the Kyoto Protocol's market mechanisms and the emergence of multilateral development bank (MDB) tracking systems. The Kyoto Protocol's Clean Development Mechanism (CDM), which entered into force in 2005, introduced a market-based approach to climate finance, allowing developed countries to meet emission reduction commitments by purchasing carbon credits from projects in developing countries. The CDM's operational definition of climate finance centered on "additional" emission reductions—projects that would not have occurred in the absence of carbon finance. This additionality criterion, while conceptually clear, proved notoriously difficult to verify in practice and became a major source of controversy in carbon market scholarship (Zhang et al., 2022).

Parallel to the CDM, multilateral development banks began developing internal systems to track climate-relevant investments within their broader development portfolios. The World Bank and regional development banks recognized that substantial portions of their lending supported climate objectives (renewable energy, energy efficiency, sustainable transport, climate-resilient infrastructure), but lacked standardized methodologies to identify and quantify these flows. During the mid-2000s, MDBs began experimenting with activity-level markers to tag projects with climate co-benefits, drawing on approaches developed for tracking aid to other cross-cutting themes such as gender equality and environmental sustainability.

This period also saw the establishment of dedicated climate funds under the UNFCCC, including the Least Developed Countries Fund (LDCF) and the Special Climate Change Fund (SCCF), alongside the expansion of the GEF's climate change focal area. Each fund developed its own operational definitions and eligibility criteria, contributing to an increasingly fragmented institutional landscape (Larrea, 2024).

Scholarly attention during this period focused primarily on the effectiveness of the CDM and other Kyoto mechanisms, with less attention to definitional questions per se. However, debates over CDM additionality and the appropriate baseline for calculating emission reductions prefigured later controversies over what counts as climate finance and how to establish baselines for "new and additional" flows. The intellectual framework remained dominated by environmental economics, with limited engagement from accounting, development studies, or critical perspectives.

### 4.3 Post-Copenhagen (2010-2015): $100bn Pledge, Fragmentation of Definitions

The 2009 Copenhagen Accord marked a decisive turning point, transforming climate finance from a niche concern into a central pillar of international climate policy. Developed countries committed to "a goal of mobilizing jointly USD 100 billion dollars a year by 2020 to address the needs of developing countries" from "a wide variety of sources, public and private, bilateral and multilateral, including alternative sources of finance" (UNFCCC, 2009). This commitment, formalized in subsequent UNFCCC decisions, created an urgent need for systematic measurement and reporting of climate finance flows.

However, the Copenhagen commitment was deliberately vague on crucial definitional questions: What activities count as "climate" finance? Which flows are "new and additional" to existing development assistance? How should non-grant instruments (loans, equity, guarantees) be valued? How should "mobilized" private finance be attributed to public interventions? The absence of agreed definitions reflected both genuine technical complexity and strategic ambiguity that allowed diverse actors to claim progress toward the goal while employing divergent accounting methodologies (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015; Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

This period saw the proliferation of competing institutional definitions and accounting approaches:

**OECD DAC Approach**: The OECD Development Assistance Committee (DAC) adapted its existing "Rio Markers" system—originally developed to track aid relevant to the Rio Conventions (UNFCCC, Convention on Biological Diversity, UN Convention to Combat Desertification)—to quantify climate finance. The Rio Marker system uses activity-level markers to tag development projects as having climate change mitigation or adaptation as a "principal" or "significant" objective. For "principal" projects, 100% of the commitment is counted as climate finance; for "significant" projects, various methodologies have been used, with many providers counting 40-50% of the commitment. This approach has been criticized for over-crediting projects with only tangential climate relevance and for lacking empirical validation of the marker assignments (Weikmans, n.d.; Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**MDB Joint Methodology**: Multilateral development banks developed a joint methodology for tracking climate finance, published in annual joint reports beginning in 2011. The MDB approach uses activity-level lists of eligible project types (e.g., renewable energy, public transport, climate-resilient infrastructure) and tracks the full value of projects meeting eligibility criteria. Unlike the OECD approach, MDBs do not use a "significant" category or partial attribution. The MDB methodology also includes policy-based lending that supports climate-relevant reforms. This approach has been praised for transparency but criticized for potentially over-counting projects with mixed objectives (MDBs, 2016, 2017).

**UNFCCC Reporting**: Under the UNFCCC, developed country Parties submit Biennial Reports (BRs) describing their climate finance provision, using diverse methodologies and definitions. The UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance (SCF) produces biennial assessments synthesizing reported information, but the lack of standardized reporting formats and definitions severely limits comparability and aggregation. Developing countries have consistently criticized the opacity and inconsistency of developed country reporting (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Bilateral Provider Approaches**: Individual developed countries employ diverse approaches, often combining elements of OECD, MDB, and national methodologies. Some providers report only grant-equivalent values of loans, while others report face values. Some include export credits and other non-concessional flows, while others exclude them. This heterogeneity makes it impossible to aggregate reported figures without extensive adjustments and assumptions (Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

The post-Copenhagen period also saw intense scholarly and policy debate over three definitional controversies that remain unresolved:

1. **Climate Specificity**: What degree of climate relevance is required for a financial flow to count as climate finance? Should only projects with climate change as the primary objective qualify, or should projects with significant but secondary climate co-benefits be included? How should multi-objective projects (e.g., sustainable agriculture that supports both development and adaptation) be treated?

2. **Additionality**: Are climate finance flows "new and additional" to existing development assistance, or do they represent relabeling of ODA that would have been provided anyway? What baseline should be used to assess additionality—historical ODA levels, projected ODA trends, or some other reference point? (Stadelmann et al., 2011).

3. **Instrument Valuation**: Should non-grant instruments be counted at face value or grant-equivalent value? Face value counting inflates reported figures by including the full value of loans that will be repaid, while grant-equivalent counting requires complex calculations of concessionality that depend on discount rate assumptions and comparator interest rates (Bachus & Bécault, 2017).

These definitional controversies were not merely technical disputes but reflected deeper political tensions over burden-sharing, accountability, and trust between developed and developing countries. The fragmentation of definitions served the interests of some actors (allowing flexibility in reporting and claims of progress) while frustrating others (particularly developing countries seeking clarity on the scale and nature of support they could expect) (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

### 4.4 Paris Era (2016-2025): Transparency Frameworks, NCQG Debates

The 2015 Paris Agreement and its Enhanced Transparency Framework (ETF) aimed to address some of the definitional and reporting challenges that had plagued the post-Copenhagen period. Article 9 of the Paris Agreement requires developed countries to provide financial resources to assist developing countries and encourages voluntary contributions from other Parties. The Agreement's transparency provisions, elaborated in the Katowice Modalities, Procedures and Guidelines (MPGs) adopted at COP24 in 2018, establish Common Tabular Formats (CTFs) for reporting climate finance provided and mobilized (Falduto & Ellis, 2019).

However, the Paris era has not resolved fundamental definitional questions. The MPGs provide reporting formats but do not mandate specific definitions of what counts as climate finance, how to value non-grant instruments, or how to attribute mobilized private finance. Parties retain flexibility to use diverse methodologies, though they are required to provide information on underlying assumptions and methodologies. This "flexibility with transparency" approach reflects political compromises but perpetuates comparability challenges (Bergsvik et al., 2024).

The Paris era has seen several important developments in definitional debates:

**Grant Equivalent vs Face Value**: The debate over instrument valuation has intensified, with developing countries and civil society organizations arguing that only grant-equivalent values should count toward the $100 billion goal and future targets. The OECD DAC has undertaken a "modernization" of its statistical system to better capture the donor effort involved in non-grant instruments, introducing grant-equivalent reporting alongside face value reporting. However, many providers continue to report face values, and there is no consensus on appropriate discount rates or concessionality thresholds (Pauw et al., 2022; Bachus & Bécault, 2017).

**Mobilized Private Finance**: The attribution of private finance "mobilized" by public interventions has become a central definitional battleground. The OECD has developed methodological guidance for estimating mobilized private finance, but implementation reveals severe data limitations and methodological sensitivities. Key controversies include: (1) which public interventions can claim to have "mobilized" private finance (direct co-financing, guarantees, policy reforms?); (2) how to attribute mobilization when multiple public actors are involved; (3) whether to count only direct mobilization or also indirect effects; and (4) how to avoid double counting when the same private investment is claimed by multiple public actors (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

**Adaptation Finance**: The definition and tracking of adaptation finance has proven particularly challenging. Unlike mitigation, which can often be quantified through emission reductions, adaptation outcomes are context-specific and difficult to measure. The OECD adaptation Rio Marker has been criticized for over-crediting projects with limited adaptation benefits, while MDB methodologies for identifying adaptation projects vary. The Paris Agreement's call for a "balance" between mitigation and adaptation finance has heightened attention to adaptation tracking, but definitional clarity remains elusive (Weikmans, n.d.; Savvidou et al., 2021).

**New Collective Quantified Goal (NCQG)**: The Paris Agreement mandates setting a New Collective Quantified Goal on climate finance by 2024, to succeed the $100 billion goal. NCQG negotiations have reopened fundamental definitional questions: Should the goal cover only public finance or also mobilized private finance? Should it be expressed in grant-equivalent or face value terms? Should it include domestic climate finance by developing countries or only international flows? Should it cover only mitigation and adaptation or also loss and damage? These debates demonstrate that definitional controversies are far from resolved and remain deeply political (Pauw et al., 2022).

**Transparency and Verification**: The Paris era has seen growing attention to transparency mechanisms and the verification of reported climate finance. The MPGs establish review processes for reported information, but these reviews focus on procedural compliance rather than substantive verification of whether reported flows meet climate finance criteria. Civil society "shadow reports" have played an important role in critically assessing official reporting and proposing alternative accounting methodologies, though their influence on official processes remains limited (Carty et al., 2018; Bergsvik et al., 2024).

### 4.5 Competing Institutional Definitions

The persistence of competing institutional definitions reflects both technical complexity and divergent institutional interests and perspectives. This section compares the principal institutional approaches to defining climate finance.

**UNFCCC Perspective**: The UNFCCC framework emphasizes financial flows from developed to developing countries to support mitigation and adaptation, grounded in the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities. UNFCCC decisions refer to "new and additional" finance but do not operationalize this criterion. The Standing Committee on Finance's biennial assessments attempt to synthesize diverse reported information but acknowledge severe comparability limitations. Developing country negotiators have consistently pushed for narrow definitions that emphasize public, grant-based, additional finance, while developed countries have advocated for broader definitions that include mobilized private finance and non-concessional instruments (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**OECD DAC Perspective**: The OECD DAC approach treats climate finance as a subset of development finance, using Rio Markers to identify climate-relevant ODA and Other Official Flows (OOF). This approach prioritizes comparability across donors and integration with existing development finance statistics. The DAC perspective emphasizes donor effort and includes the full face value of loans and other non-grant instruments. Critics argue that this approach over-counts climate finance by including projects with only tangential climate relevance and by not adjusting for the non-concessional nature of many flows (Weikmans, n.d.; Carty et al., 2018).

**MDB Perspective**: Multilateral development banks define climate finance as financing for projects and programs that contribute to climate change mitigation or adaptation, using activity-based eligibility lists. The MDB approach emphasizes project-level tracking and the integration of climate considerations into development lending. MDBs report the full value of climate-relevant projects, arguing that this reflects the scale of investment being directed toward climate objectives. The MDB joint methodology has achieved a degree of standardization across institutions, though differences remain in how policy-based lending and financial intermediary lending are treated (MDBs, 2016, 2017).

**Global South Perspectives**: Developing country governments, civil society organizations, and scholars from the Global South have articulated alternative framings that emphasize equity, justice, and the adequacy of finance relative to needs. These perspectives often advocate for narrow definitions that count only public, concessional, additional finance and that value non-grant instruments at grant-equivalent rather than face value. Global South critiques highlight how broad definitions allow developed countries to claim progress toward commitments while providing limited actual resources for climate action. There is also emphasis on the quality of finance (grants vs loans, country ownership, alignment with national priorities) rather than just quantity (Timilsena, 2023; Sarfo-Adu & Kokofu, 2023).

**Private Sector and Financial Industry Perspectives**: The financial industry has developed its own definitions and taxonomies, particularly around "green finance" and "sustainable finance." These definitions often include a broader range of activities than official climate finance definitions and focus on environmental sustainability more broadly rather than climate change specifically. Green bond principles, sustainable finance taxonomies (e.g., EU Taxonomy), and climate risk disclosure frameworks (e.g., TCFD) represent private sector efforts to define and standardize climate-relevant finance, though these operate largely in parallel to official climate finance definitions (Farroñán et al., 2025; Rodriguez-Rojas et al., 2022).

### 4.6 Stabilization and Persistent Fragmentation

Despite three decades of institutional development and scholarly attention, climate finance definitions have not stabilized into a single authoritative framework. Instead, the field is characterized by persistent fragmentation, with multiple competing definitions coexisting and serving different institutional and political purposes.

Several factors explain this persistent fragmentation:

**Technical Complexity**: Defining climate finance involves genuinely difficult technical questions about attribution, additionality, and the valuation of diverse financial instruments. Reasonable experts can disagree on appropriate methodologies, and different approaches involve trade-offs between accuracy, practicality, and incentive effects (Jachnik et al., 2015).

**Institutional Path Dependence**: Established institutions (OECD, MDBs, UNFCCC) have invested in particular definitional and accounting approaches, and changing these systems involves significant costs and political challenges. Path dependence creates resistance to convergence even when the benefits of standardization are recognized (Funder & Dupuy, 2022).

**Divergent Interests**: Different actors have divergent interests in how climate finance is defined. Developed countries benefit from broad definitions that allow them to report larger figures and claim progress toward commitments. Developing countries benefit from narrow definitions that emphasize the inadequacy of current flows and the need for scaled-up support. MDBs benefit from definitions that recognize their climate-relevant lending. These divergent interests make consensus difficult to achieve (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Political Sensitivity**: Climate finance is deeply intertwined with broader North-South tensions over responsibility, equity, and burden-sharing in addressing climate change. Definitional debates are not merely technical but reflect fundamental disagreements about the nature and scale of developed country obligations. The political sensitivity of these issues makes it difficult to achieve the trust and goodwill necessary for definitional convergence (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Epistemological Pluralism**: Different disciplinary and institutional communities bring different epistemological assumptions and methodological preferences to climate finance. Economists emphasize efficiency and incentive effects, accountants emphasize accuracy and auditability, development practitioners emphasize country ownership and alignment with national priorities, and civil society emphasizes equity and justice. These diverse perspectives are not easily reconciled into a single definitional framework.

The persistence of definitional fragmentation has significant consequences. It undermines accountability by making it impossible to definitively assess whether commitments have been met. It erodes trust between developed and developing countries. It complicates efforts to track progress toward climate goals. And it creates opportunities for strategic reporting that inflates apparent progress while delivering limited actual resources. Yet fragmentation also reflects genuine pluralism in perspectives and interests, and efforts to impose a single definition may be neither feasible nor desirable. The challenge for future research and policy is to find ways to improve transparency and comparability while acknowledging legitimate diversity in approaches and perspectives.

## 5. Thematic Synthesis: Priority Debates

This section provides in-depth analysis of seven priority thematic debates in climate finance scholarship, examining conceptual frameworks, key papers, methodological approaches, controversies, and research gaps. Each subsection synthesizes evidence from both the review corpus and primary research corpus, with particular attention to contributions relevant to the history of economic ideas and economic methodology.

### 5.A Quantification and Performativity

**Conceptual Overview**

The quantification and performativity debate examines how climate finance numbers are produced, circulated, and used, and how measurement practices themselves shape the phenomena being measured. Drawing on science and technology studies (STS), sociology of quantification, and performativity theory, this perspective treats climate finance metrics not as neutral representations of pre-existing flows but as sociotechnical accomplishments that perform political and economic work. Quantification practices establish commensurability across diverse activities, create accountability relationships, and shape investment decisions and policy priorities. The production of authoritative climate finance numbers involves choices about boundaries, categories, valuation methods, and aggregation rules—choices that embody particular interests, assumptions, and power relations.

Despite the rich potential of performativity perspectives for understanding climate finance, explicit engagement with this theoretical framework remains limited in the corpus. Most research treats quantification as a technical challenge (how to measure accurately) rather than as a social practice worthy of critical examination (how measurement practices constitute and transform their objects). This represents a significant gap, as climate finance offers an ideal empirical domain for studying the performativity of economics and the politics of quantification.

**Key Papers and Contributions**

**de Aguiar et al. (2024)** provide the most direct call for critical engagement with climate finance quantification. Their systematic review of accounting literature on climate finance identifies severe gaps in research on accountability, audit, and the social construction of climate finance numbers. They argue that accounting scholars have largely treated climate finance measurement as a technical problem of improving disclosure standards, with insufficient attention to how accounting categories shape what counts as climate finance and how measurement practices perform legitimation work. Their review calls for research drawing on critical accounting perspectives, including studies of how quantification regimes emerge, how numbers acquire authority, and how measurement practices distribute resources and responsibilities (de Aguiar et al., 2024).

**Roberts and Weikmans (2017)** offer a political economy analysis that, while not explicitly employing performativity theory, demonstrates how accounting fragmentation performs political work. They argue that the absence of agreed accounting modalities is not merely a technical failure but serves the interests of developed countries by allowing flexibility in reporting and claims of progress while obscuring the inadequacy of actual resource flows. Their analysis shows how the proliferation of competing definitions and methodologies fragments trust and impedes accountability, with consequences for the effectiveness of climate governance. This work demonstrates the political stakes of seemingly technical accounting choices (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Bergsvik et al. (2024)** examine whether transparency reforms under the Paris Agreement's Enhanced Transparency Framework are furthering clarity or perpetuating opacity in climate finance governance. Their analysis suggests that transparency mechanisms may perform symbolic functions (demonstrating commitment to accountability) without necessarily improving substantive comparability or verification. This work raises important questions about the relationship between transparency as a procedural norm and accountability as a substantive outcome, relevant to broader debates about the performativity of governance metrics (Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**Agustini and Arifa (2024)** document the lack of standardization in climate change accounting and disclosure practices, showing how the absence of mandatory standards creates space for diverse and potentially strategic reporting choices. Their analysis of disclosure motivations (legitimacy, stakeholder pressure, competitive advantage) suggests that climate accounting performs multiple functions beyond simple information provision. However, their analysis remains largely descriptive, with limited engagement with theoretical frameworks for understanding the performative effects of disclosure practices (Agustini & Arifa, 2024).

**Zhang et al. (2022)** trace the evolution of green finance and carbon emission reduction metrics, documenting how particular measurement categories (carbon intensity, emission reductions, green investment) have become institutionalized and how they shape policy and investment decisions. While not explicitly framed in performativity terms, their bibliometric analysis reveals how measurement categories diffuse across research communities and policy contexts, suggesting processes of performative stabilization (Zhang et al., 2022).

**Methodological Approaches**

Research on quantification and performativity in climate finance employs diverse methodological approaches, though explicit performativity studies remain rare:

- **Critical accounting analysis**: Examination of how accounting standards, disclosure requirements, and reporting frameworks shape what gets measured and how (de Aguiar et al., 2024; Agustini & Arifa, 2024)
- **Institutional analysis**: Tracing how particular measurement categories emerge, become institutionalized, and acquire authority across organizations and policy contexts (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017; Funder & Dupuy, 2022)
- **Document analysis**: Systematic review of reporting documents, methodological guidance, and policy debates to reveal underlying assumptions and choices in quantification practices (Bergsvik et al., 2024)
- **Bibliometric analysis**: Mapping the diffusion of measurement concepts and categories across research communities (Zhang et al., 2022; Shang & Jin, 2023)

**Controversies and Debates**

Several controversies animate the quantification debate, though they are often implicit rather than explicitly theorized:

1. **Commensurability**: Can diverse climate finance flows (grants, loans, equity, guarantees, carbon credits, mobilized private finance) be meaningfully aggregated into a single metric? What is lost in the process of commensuration, and whose interests are served by particular aggregation rules?

2. **Boundary Work**: How are boundaries drawn between climate finance and other financial flows (development finance, humanitarian assistance, private investment)? Who has authority to define these boundaries, and how do boundary disputes reflect deeper political tensions?

3. **Valuation**: How should non-grant instruments be valued—at face value, grant-equivalent value, or some other basis? What assumptions about time preference, risk, and opportunity cost are embedded in different valuation approaches, and how do these assumptions shape reported figures?

4. **Attribution**: How should mobilized private finance be attributed to public interventions? What causal claims are implicit in attribution methodologies, and how can these claims be empirically validated?

5. **Performative Effects**: How do climate finance metrics shape investment decisions, policy priorities, and institutional behavior? Do measurement practices create incentives for particular types of interventions or reporting strategies? How do metrics become targets, and with what consequences?

**Research Gaps**

The quantification and performativity dimension of climate finance remains severely underdeveloped relative to its importance. Priority research gaps include:

- **Performativity case studies**: Detailed empirical studies of how specific climate finance metrics (e.g., mobilized private finance ratios, Rio Marker percentages, green bond volumes) have shaped investment decisions, policy choices, or institutional practices. Such studies could employ mixed methods combining quantitative analysis of behavioral responses with qualitative analysis of decision-making processes.

- **Genealogies of measurement categories**: Historical research tracing the origins and evolution of key climate finance measurement categories (additionality, mobilization, concessionality, climate specificity). Such genealogies could draw on archival research, oral histories, and document analysis to reconstruct how particular categories emerged, who championed them, and how they became institutionalized.

- **Sociology of quantification**: Ethnographic or interview-based research examining how climate finance numbers are produced within organizations (OECD, MDBs, national agencies). Such research could reveal the practical judgments, negotiations, and compromises involved in generating authoritative numbers, illuminating the social construction of quantitative authority.

- **Comparative commensuration studies**: Analysis of how different actors and institutions establish commensurability across diverse climate finance flows, and what political and epistemological work is performed by particular commensuration practices.

- **Metric fixation and gaming**: Research on how climate finance metrics become targets and how actors respond strategically to measurement regimes, potentially distorting behavior in ways that undermine substantive climate objectives.

For scholars of the history of economic ideas, climate finance quantification offers a contemporary case study of how economic categories emerge, stabilize, and perform work in policy contexts—a process that can illuminate broader questions about the relationship between economic theory, measurement practices, and governance.

### 5.B Development Finance vs Climate Finance

**Conceptual Overview**

The relationship between development finance and climate finance constitutes one of the most persistent and politically charged debates in the field. At stake are fundamental questions about the nature of developed country obligations, the adequacy of climate finance relative to developing country needs, and the risk that climate finance represents relabeling of existing development assistance rather than genuinely additional resources. The UNFCCC's requirement that climate finance be "new and additional" to existing development assistance reflects developing countries' insistence that climate finance should not come at the expense of other development priorities. However, operationalizing "additionality" has proven extraordinarily difficult, and the debate remains unresolved three decades after the Convention's adoption.

The development-climate finance boundary is not merely a technical accounting question but reflects deeper tensions about the relationship between climate change and development, the appropriate division of labor between climate and development institutions, and the extent to which climate action should be integrated into or separated from broader development cooperation. These tensions play out in debates over ODA eligibility, the role of climate funds versus mainstreaming in development banks, and the appropriate balance between climate-specific and development-oriented objectives in project design.

**Key Papers and Contributions**

**Stadelmann et al. (2011)** provide the most systematic analysis of additionality baselines, assessing eight options for defining "new and additional" climate finance. Their analysis evaluates each baseline option against criteria including novelty to existing pledges, additionality to development assistance, environmental effectiveness, distributional consequences, and institutional and political feasibility. They conclude that only two options perform well across criteria: "new sources only" (counting only finance from sources not previously used for development assistance, such as carbon pricing revenues or financial transaction taxes) and "above pre-defined business as usual level of development assistance" (establishing a baseline trajectory for ODA and counting only climate finance above this level). However, they acknowledge that both options face significant political and practical challenges. This work remains the most rigorous attempt to operationalize additionality, yet its recommendations have not been adopted in practice (Stadelmann et al., 2011).

**Stadelmann et al. (2013)** extend this analysis by examining difficulties in accounting for private finance and the relationship between additionality and leverage. They argue that the inclusion of mobilized private finance in climate finance accounting creates additional additionality challenges: Is private finance that would have occurred anyway truly "additional" simply because a public intervention was present? How can counterfactual private investment be estimated? Their analysis highlights the tension between wanting to credit public interventions for mobilizing private finance and ensuring that reported figures represent genuinely additional resources (Stadelmann et al., 2013).

**Roberts and Weikmans (2017)** situate additionality debates within broader political economy of climate finance, arguing that the failure to operationalize additionality reflects developed countries' reluctance to commit to genuinely new resources. They document how the OECD Rio Marker system, by counting climate-relevant ODA as climate finance without adjusting for additionality, allows developed countries to report large climate finance figures while ODA levels stagnate or decline. This "relabeling" of development assistance as climate finance, they argue, erodes trust and undermines the political bargain underlying the UNFCCC's financial provisions (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Funder and Dupuy (2022)** provide valuable institutional ethnography of how climate finance coordination norms interact with development finance institutions and practices in Zambia. Their analysis shows how the World Bank's early influence in establishing climate finance coordination mechanisms shaped institutional arrangements in ways that privileged particular actors and approaches. They document tensions between donor preferences for climate-specific interventions and recipient country preferences for integrated development approaches that address climate change alongside other priorities. This work demonstrates how the climate-development boundary is negotiated and contested in practice, with consequences for country ownership and institutional effectiveness (Funder & Dupuy, 2022).

**Timilsena (2023)** synthesizes evidence on climate finance practices in developing countries, highlighting governance challenges, reporting difficulties, and the gap between finance received and finance needed. The review emphasizes recipient country perspectives on the quality of climate finance, including concerns about the predominance of loans over grants, the transaction costs of accessing climate funds, and the misalignment between donor priorities and national development plans. This work underscores that the development-climate finance boundary matters not just for accounting purposes but for the effectiveness and equity of climate action (Timilsena, 2023).

**Carty et al. (2018)** provide civil society perspective through Oxfam's "shadow report" on progress toward the $100 billion goal. The report argues that much of what developed countries count as climate finance is actually ODA that would have been provided anyway, repackaged with climate labels. Using grant-equivalent valuation and excluding non-concessional flows, the shadow report estimates that "true" climate finance is substantially lower than officially reported figures. While the report's methodology is contested, it has been influential in policy debates and represents an important alternative accounting perspective (Carty et al., 2018).

**Methodological Approaches**

Research on the development-climate finance boundary employs several methodological approaches:

- **Baseline analysis**: Systematic assessment of options for defining additionality baselines, evaluating trade-offs between different approaches (Stadelmann et al., 2011)
- **Comparative accounting**: Analysis of how different actors and methodologies treat the development-climate boundary, revealing divergent assumptions and interests (Carty et al., 2018; Roberts & Weikmans, 2017)
- **Institutional ethnography**: Grounded analysis of how climate finance is coordinated with development finance in specific country contexts, revealing practical negotiations and tensions (Funder & Dupuy, 2022)
- **Policy analysis**: Examination of institutional mandates, eligibility criteria, and operational guidelines to understand how climate and development objectives are balanced in practice (Larrea, 2024; Timilsena, 2023)

**Controversies and Debates**

Several interrelated controversies structure the development-climate finance debate:

1. **Additionality Operationalization**: Despite the UNFCCC's "new and additional" requirement, no agreed methodology exists for assessing additionality. Developed countries have resisted establishing baselines or adjustment mechanisms, while developing countries view this as a fundamental breach of commitments.

2. **ODA Overlap**: A large portion of reported climate finance consists of ODA that is climate-relevant. Is this overlap appropriate (climate change is a development challenge) or problematic (climate finance should be additional to ODA)? Should there be limits on how much ODA can count as climate finance?

3. **Crowding Out**: Does the emphasis on climate finance crowd out other development priorities, such as health, education, or poverty reduction? Or does climate action support broader development objectives through co-benefits?

4. **Institutional Separation vs Integration**: Should climate finance be channeled through dedicated climate funds (ensuring climate specificity and additionality) or mainstreamed through development institutions (ensuring integration and efficiency)? The proliferation of climate funds has been criticized for fragmentation, while mainstreaming has been criticized for diluting climate focus.

5. **Quality vs Quantity**: Should the focus be on the quantity of climate finance (meeting the $100 billion goal) or the quality (grants vs loans, country ownership, alignment with national priorities)? Developing countries emphasize that the form and governance of finance matter as much as the amount.

**Research Gaps**

Despite extensive policy debate, several research gaps persist:

- **Empirical additionality assessment**: Rigorous empirical studies attempting to estimate what portion of reported climate finance is genuinely additional to what would have been provided as development assistance anyway. Such studies could employ econometric methods to model counterfactual ODA trajectories or case study methods to trace decision-making processes.

- **Crowding out analysis**: Quantitative analysis of whether increased climate finance is associated with decreased non-climate ODA, either in aggregate or for specific sectors or recipients. Such analysis could help adjudicate competing claims about crowding out.

- **Institutional histories of boundary-making**: Historical research tracing how the climate-development boundary has been constructed and contested across institutions and policy epochs. Such histories could illuminate the political and intellectual work involved in establishing and maintaining categorical boundaries.

- **Recipient country perspectives**: More research centering developing country perspectives on the climate-development relationship, including how recipient governments navigate competing donor priorities and how climate finance affects national development planning and budgeting.

- **Co-benefits and trade-offs**: Systematic analysis of synergies and trade-offs between climate and development objectives in specific sectors (e.g., energy, agriculture, infrastructure), informing debates about integration vs separation.

For historians of economic ideas, the development-climate finance boundary offers insights into how economic categories are constructed and contested in international policy contexts, how boundary disputes reflect underlying political tensions, and how institutional path dependence shapes the evolution of policy frameworks.

### 5.C Accounting Categories and Methodologies

**Conceptual Overview**

Accounting categories and methodologies constitute the technical infrastructure of climate finance governance, translating abstract commitments into measurable flows and enabling (or impeding) accountability. The development of climate finance accounting has involved adapting existing development finance tracking systems (particularly the OECD DAC statistical system), creating new climate-specific categories and markers, and negotiating methodological choices about valuation, attribution, and aggregation. These seemingly technical accounting decisions embody substantive political and normative judgments about what counts as climate finance, how different instruments should be valued, and how to balance accuracy, practicality, and incentive effects.

The intellectual history of climate finance accounting remains largely unwritten, yet it offers rich material for understanding how accounting categories emerge, how they acquire authority, and how they shape policy and practice. Key accounting innovations—Rio Markers, grant-equivalent valuation, mobilized private finance estimation, Common Tabular Formats—have specific origins in particular institutional contexts and reflect particular problem framings and solution logics. Tracing these genealogies can illuminate broader questions about the relationship between accounting practice and economic governance.

**Key Papers and Contributions**

**Rio Markers and OECD DAC Methodology**

The OECD DAC Rio Marker system is the most widely used methodology for tracking climate-relevant development finance, yet it has been subject to sustained criticism regarding its credibility and over-crediting tendencies.

**Roberts and Weikmans (2017)** provide the most comprehensive critique of the Rio Marker system, arguing that it serves neither contributors nor recipients well. They document how the marker system's reliance on self-reporting by donors, its use of broad "significant" categories that allow counting 40-100% of project values, and its lack of empirical validation create systematic over-crediting. They argue that the marker system's deficiencies are not merely technical but reflect institutional interests: the OECD DAC serves donor countries, and a permissive accounting system allows donors to report large climate finance figures without committing genuinely additional resources (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Weikmans (n.d.)** provides empirical validation of Rio Marker credibility concerns through a detailed case study of adaptation finance to Vietnam. By examining project documents for a sample of projects marked as having adaptation as a "principal" or "significant" objective, Weikmans demonstrates that many projects have only tangential adaptation relevance. The study finds systematic over-crediting, with projects receiving adaptation markers despite limited adaptation content. This work provides crucial empirical evidence for critiques of marker-based accounting and demonstrates the value of ground-truthing reported figures against project-level evidence (Weikmans, n.d.).

**Falduto and Ellis (2019)** analyze options for Common Tabular Formats (CTFs) for reporting climate finance under the Paris Agreement's Enhanced Transparency Framework. Their analysis examines how CTFs can facilitate comparability while accommodating methodological diversity, including the treatment of Rio Markers in UNFCCC reporting. They discuss options for reporting climate-specific vs total project values, for disaggregating by marker category (principal vs significant), and for providing methodological transparency. While primarily technical, this work reveals the political negotiations involved in designing reporting formats and the trade-offs between standardization and flexibility (Falduto & Ellis, 2019).

**Grant Equivalent vs Face Value Debates**

The valuation of non-grant instruments (loans, equity, guarantees) is one of the most contentious accounting issues, with major implications for reported climate finance volumes and assessments of progress toward commitments.

**Bachus and Bécault (2017)** provide the most comprehensive analysis of methods for valuing equity investments in climate finance. They review approaches including face value, grant-equivalent value based on expected returns, and various risk-adjusted methods. They highlight that equity valuation is particularly challenging because returns are uncertain and may not materialize for many years. Their analysis situates equity valuation debates within the broader OECD DAC modernization process, which aims to better capture donor effort in providing non-grant instruments. However, they acknowledge that no valuation method is perfect, and choices involve trade-offs between accuracy, practicality, and incentive effects (Bachus & Bécault, 2017).

**Pauw et al. (2022)** examine implications of face value vs grant-equivalent accounting for the post-2025 New Collective Quantified Goal (NCQG). They argue that the persistence of face value accounting in the $100 billion goal has inflated reported figures and obscured the limited concessionality of many flows. They advocate for grant-equivalent accounting in the NCQG to ensure that the goal reflects actual resource transfers rather than loan volumes. However, they acknowledge political challenges: developed countries prefer face value accounting because it allows reporting larger figures, while grant-equivalent calculations require agreement on discount rates and concessionality thresholds that are themselves contested (Pauw et al., 2022).

**Carty et al. (2018)** apply grant-equivalent methodology in their shadow report, estimating that the grant-equivalent value of reported climate finance is substantially lower than face value figures. Their analysis uses discount rates and concessionality thresholds to adjust loan values, revealing that much of what is counted as climate finance represents loans that will be repaid with interest. While their specific methodological choices are debatable, the shadow report has been influential in highlighting the implications of valuation choices for assessments of progress and adequacy (Carty et al., 2018).

**Mobilized Private Finance Methodologies**

The estimation of private finance mobilized by public interventions has emerged as a critical accounting challenge, with major implications for the $100 billion goal and future targets.

**Jachnik et al. (2015)** provide the foundational methodological framework for estimating mobilized private climate finance. Their OECD report outlines key decision points including: (1) which public interventions can claim to mobilize private finance; (2) how to attribute mobilization when multiple public actors are involved; (3) whether to count direct mobilization only or also indirect effects; (4) what data sources and estimation methods to use. They assess trade-offs between different approaches in terms of accuracy, standardization potential, incentive effects, and data requirements. The report recommends pragmatic short-term approaches while acknowledging the need for improved data systems and methodological convergence over time. This work has been foundational for subsequent OECD guidance and MDB reporting on mobilized private finance (Jachnik et al., 2015).

**Caruso and Ellis (2013)** provide crucial comparative analysis of how 24 different actors define and estimate mobilized private finance. They document wide methodological heterogeneity and identify specific double-counting channels that arise when multiple actors claim credit for mobilizing the same private investment. Their analysis reveals that mobilization estimates are highly sensitive to methodological choices and that aggregating across actors without adjustments risks severe over-counting. This work established the empirical basis for concerns about mobilized private finance accounting and the need for standardization (Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

**Jachnik and Raynaud (2015)** test two practical approaches for estimating mobilized private finance at the sector level, using renewable energy as a case study. The first approach analyzes investment databases to identify projects with public and private co-financing; the second extrapolates from leverage ratios reported by public finance providers. Both approaches reveal severe limitations: investment databases have incomplete coverage and inconsistent classification, while leverage ratios are highly sensitive to calculation methods and vary widely across providers and contexts. The study demonstrates that current data systems are inadequate for robust mobilization estimation and that reported figures should be interpreted with great caution (Jachnik & Raynaud, 2015).

**Atteridge and Dzebo (2015)** examine the conceptual question of when private finance should count toward international climate finance pledges. They argue that the Copenhagen commitment's inclusion of private finance was deliberately vague, leaving unresolved whether all private climate investment in developing countries counts, or only private finance that can be causally linked to public interventions, or only private finance that would not have occurred without public support. They highlight that different interpretations have radically different implications for assessing progress and for the scale of public finance required (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015).

**Concessionality, Double Counting, and Transparency**

**Savvidou et al. (2021)** provide detailed analysis of international public finance for climate change adaptation in Africa, examining both volumes and concessionality. Their analysis reveals that much adaptation finance is provided as loans rather than grants, raising questions about appropriateness for adaptation (which generates limited revenue for repayment) and about debt sustainability. They also document inconsistencies in how different providers report adaptation finance, complicating aggregation and comparison (Savvidou et al., 2021).

**Bergsvik et al. (2024)** assess whether transparency reforms under the Paris Agreement are improving clarity in climate finance governance. Their analysis suggests that transparency mechanisms may increase the volume of reported information without necessarily improving comparability or enabling verification. They argue that transparency is a necessary but insufficient condition for accountability, and that substantive accounting standards are needed alongside procedural transparency requirements (Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**MDB Joint Reports (2016, 2017)** document the multilateral development banks' joint methodology for tracking climate finance. These reports describe activity-based eligibility criteria, the treatment of policy-based lending, and approaches to estimating mobilized private finance. The MDB methodology differs from OECD approaches in important respects, contributing to the fragmentation of accounting systems. However, the joint reports represent an important effort at standardization across MDBs and provide transparency about methodological choices (MDBs, 2016, 2017).

**Methodological Approaches**

Research on accounting methodologies employs several approaches:

- **Methodological development**: Creation of frameworks, decision trees, and guidance for estimating climate finance, particularly mobilized private finance (Jachnik et al., 2015)
- **Comparative methodology analysis**: Systematic comparison of how different actors define and measure climate finance, revealing heterogeneity and trade-offs (Caruso & Ellis, 2013)
- **Empirical testing**: Application of methodologies to real data to assess feasibility, data requirements, and sensitivity to assumptions (Jachnik & Raynaud, 2015)
- **Credibility assessment**: Ground-truthing of reported figures against project-level evidence to assess over-crediting (Weikmans, n.d.)
- **Critical accounting analysis**: Examination of how accounting choices embody political and normative judgments and serve particular interests (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017)

**Controversies and Debates**

Key accounting controversies include:

1. **Marker Credibility**: Are Rio Markers a reliable indicator of climate relevance, or do they systematically over-credit projects with limited climate content? Should markers be validated through independent review?

2. **Valuation Basis**: Should non-grant instruments be valued at face value (reflecting investment volumes) or grant-equivalent value (reflecting resource transfers)? What discount rates and concessionality thresholds should be used?

3. **Mobilization Attribution**: How should mobilized private finance be attributed when multiple public actors are involved? Should attribution be proportional to public finance shares, or should all actors claim full credit, or some other approach?

4. **Causality and Additionality**: How can we determine whether private finance was genuinely mobilized by public interventions or would have occurred anyway? What counterfactual baselines are appropriate?

5. **Standardization vs Flexibility**: Should accounting methodologies be standardized across all actors (improving comparability) or should flexibility be allowed to accommodate diverse institutional contexts and mandates?

**Research Gaps**

Despite substantial methodological development work, significant gaps remain:

- **Genealogies of accounting categories**: Historical research tracing the origins and evolution of Rio Markers, grant-equivalent concepts, and mobilization metrics. When and why were these categories developed? Who championed them? How did they become institutionalized?

- **Validation studies**: More empirical research ground-truthing reported climate finance against project-level evidence, extending Weikmans' approach to other contexts and finance types.

- **Incentive effects**: Analysis of how accounting methodologies shape provider behavior. Do permissive accounting rules encourage over-reporting? Do mobilization metrics incentivize particular types of interventions?

- **Comparative institutional analysis**: Research on how different institutions (OECD, MDBs, UNFCCC) produce climate finance numbers, including the organizational processes, data systems, and quality control mechanisms involved.

- **Alternative accounting frameworks**: Development and testing of alternative accounting approaches, such as needs-based assessment, outcome-based accounting, or recipient-centric methodologies.

For historians of economic ideas and methodologists, climate finance accounting offers a rich case study of how measurement categories emerge, how accounting standards acquire authority, and how technical choices embody political and normative judgments.

### 5.D Public vs Private Finance and Mobilization

**Conceptual Overview**

The relationship between public and private climate finance, and particularly the question of how to measure private finance "mobilized" by public interventions, has become central to climate finance debates. The Copenhagen commitment's inclusion of private finance alongside public finance reflected recognition that public resources alone would be insufficient to finance the low-carbon transition and that public finance should be used strategically to leverage private investment. However, this framing has proven deeply controversial, with debates over whether private finance should count toward international commitments, how to attribute mobilization to public interventions, and whether emphasis on mobilization distracts from the need for scaled-up public finance.

The mobilization debate intersects with broader questions about the appropriate roles of public and private actors in climate finance, the effectiveness of different instruments for leveraging private investment, and the political economy of blended finance. It also raises methodological challenges about causality, attribution, and counterfactual estimation that have proven difficult to resolve.

**Key Papers and Contributions**

**Polzin (2017)** provides the most comprehensive systematic review of barriers to private finance for low-carbon innovation and policy solutions for mobilization. His analysis identifies barriers across multiple dimensions: financial (risk-return profiles, information asymmetries, transaction costs), institutional (regulatory uncertainty, policy instability), and systemic (lock-in to carbon-intensive infrastructure, network effects). He argues that mobilizing private finance requires addressing these barriers through policy interventions including carbon pricing, regulatory frameworks, public co-investment, and risk mitigation instruments. Importantly, Polzin frames mobilization as a political-economic and institutional challenge rather than simply a market failure, emphasizing the need for coordinated policy packages rather than isolated financial instruments. This work has been highly influential in shaping both academic and policy discussions of private finance mobilization (Polzin, 2017).

**Jachnik et al. (2015)** provide the foundational methodological framework for estimating mobilized private climate finance, as discussed in section 5.C. Their framework outlines decision points for defining which public interventions can claim to mobilize private finance, how to attribute mobilization, and what data and methods to use. They emphasize trade-offs between accuracy and practicality, and between providing incentives for mobilization and avoiding over-counting. The report acknowledges that mobilization estimation is technically complex and that current data systems are inadequate, recommending transparency about limitations alongside efforts to improve methods and data over time (Jachnik et al., 2015).

**Caruso and Ellis (2013)** document the wide heterogeneity in how different actors define and estimate mobilized private finance, revealing that estimates are highly sensitive to methodological choices. Their comparison of 24 actors identifies specific double-counting risks when multiple public actors claim credit for mobilizing the same private investment. This work established the empirical basis for concerns about mobilization accounting and the need for standardization (Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

**Jachnik and Raynaud (2015)** test practical approaches for estimating mobilized private finance in the renewable energy sector, revealing severe data limitations. Their analysis of investment databases shows incomplete coverage and inconsistent classification, while their analysis of leverage ratios shows high sensitivity to calculation methods and wide variation across providers and contexts. They conclude that current data systems cannot support robust mobilization estimates and that reported figures should be interpreted with great caution (Jachnik & Raynaud, 2015).

**Atteridge and Dzebo (2015)** examine the conceptual question of when private finance should count toward international climate finance pledges. They highlight the ambiguity in the Copenhagen commitment about whether all private climate investment counts, only private finance linked to public interventions, or only private finance that would not have occurred without public support. They argue that this ambiguity has allowed diverse interpretations and claims of progress while obscuring fundamental questions about the scale of public finance required (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015).

**Stadelmann et al. (2013)** analyze difficulties in accounting for private finance, particularly the challenge of assessing additionality. They argue that including mobilized private finance in climate finance accounting creates additional additionality challenges: Is private finance that would have occurred anyway truly "additional" simply because a public intervention was present? They highlight the tension between wanting to credit public interventions for mobilizing private finance and ensuring that reported figures represent genuinely additional resources (Stadelmann et al., 2013).

**Farroñán et al. (2025)** provide a recent systematic review of economic instruments for renewable energy deployment in emerging economies, with attention to the role of green bonds and other private finance instruments. They report that green bonds have mobilized approximately $500 billion globally but note that effectiveness depends on robust regulatory oversight and liquid markets, which are often absent in emerging economies. Their analysis emphasizes that instrument effectiveness is contingent on institutional capacity, challenging one-size-fits-all prescriptions for private finance mobilization (Farroñán et al., 2025).

**Desalegn and Tangl (2022)** analyze the green finance gap and barriers to private investment in developing countries. They emphasize regulatory and institutional constraints that limit private finance mobilization, including policy uncertainty, weak enforcement, and limited domestic financial market development. Their analysis suggests that mobilization strategies must be tailored to country contexts and that substantial public investment in institutional capacity is a prerequisite for effective private finance mobilization (Desalegn & Tangl, 2022).

**Methodological Approaches**

Research on public-private finance and mobilization employs diverse methods:

- **Systematic reviews**: Synthesis of evidence on barriers to private investment and effectiveness of mobilization instruments (Polzin, 2017; Farroñán et al., 2025)
- **Methodological development**: Creation of frameworks for estimating mobilized private finance (Jachnik et al., 2015)
- **Comparative analysis**: Examination of how different actors define and measure mobilization (Caruso & Ellis, 2013)
- **Empirical testing**: Application of estimation methods to real data to assess feasibility and limitations (Jachnik & Raynaud, 2015)
- **Institutional analysis**: Examination of regulatory, policy, and market conditions that enable or constrain private finance mobilization (Desalegn & Tangl, 2022)
- **Conceptual analysis**: Clarification of what "mobilization" means and when private finance should count toward commitments (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015)

**Controversies and Debates**

Several controversies structure the public-private finance debate:

1. **Counting Private Finance**: Should private finance count toward the $100 billion goal and future targets? Developing countries argue that the commitment was for public finance and that including private finance dilutes developed country obligations. Developed countries argue that the commitment explicitly includes private finance and that leveraging private investment is essential for achieving climate goals.

2. **Attribution and Causality**: How can we determine whether private finance was genuinely mobilized by public interventions or would have occurred anyway? What evidence is required to establish causal links? Current methodologies rely largely on co-financing relationships rather than rigorous causal inference.

3. **Leverage Ratios**: What leverage ratios (private finance mobilized per unit of public finance) are achievable and appropriate? Reported ratios vary widely, from less than 1:1 to over 10:1, depending on sector, instrument, and calculation method. Are high leverage ratios always desirable, or do they indicate that public finance is supporting projects that would have been commercially viable anyway?

4. **Instrument Effectiveness**: Which public finance instruments (grants, concessional loans, guarantees, equity) are most effective for mobilizing private finance in different contexts? Evidence is mixed, with effectiveness depending on sector, country context, and project characteristics.

5. **Blended Finance Governance**: How should blended finance (combining public and private finance) be governed to ensure development effectiveness, climate impact, and appropriate risk-sharing? Concerns have been raised about blended finance benefiting private investors at public expense and about mission drift toward commercially viable projects.

**Research Gaps**

Despite substantial attention, significant gaps remain:

- **Causal inference studies**: Rigorous empirical research using quasi-experimental or experimental methods to estimate the causal effect of public interventions on private investment. Such studies could employ difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, or randomized controlled trial designs to identify mobilization effects.

- **Leverage ratio determinants**: Systematic analysis of what factors (sector, instrument, country context, project characteristics) determine leverage ratios, informing realistic expectations and appropriate benchmarks.

- **Additionality assessment**: Methods for assessing whether mobilized private finance is additional to what would have occurred without public intervention, potentially using counterfactual modeling or comparison group approaches.

- **Distributional analysis**: Research on who benefits from mobilized private finance—which investors, which countries, which sectors—and whether mobilization strategies are consistent with equity and development objectives.

- **Institutional histories**: Historical research tracing how the concept of "mobilized private finance" emerged, who championed it, and how it became central to climate finance discourse and accounting.

- **Alternative mobilization strategies**: Comparative analysis of different approaches to mobilizing private finance (de-risking, concessional co-financing, policy reforms, capacity building) and their relative effectiveness and appropriateness in different contexts.

For historians of economic ideas, the mobilization debate offers insights into how concepts like "leverage," "crowding in," and "blended finance" have been adapted from financial economics to climate policy contexts, and how these concepts shape policy priorities and resource allocation.

### 5.E Transparency and Double Counting

**Conceptual Overview**

Transparency and the prevention of double counting have emerged as critical governance challenges in climate finance. Transparency—the availability of clear, comparable, and verifiable information about climate finance flows—is essential for accountability, trust-building, and effective resource allocation. However, achieving transparency is complicated by the diversity of actors and instruments involved in climate finance, the absence of standardized definitions and methodologies, and the political sensitivities surrounding reporting. Double counting—the risk that the same financial flow is counted multiple times by different actors—is a particular concern given the involvement of multiple public providers, the attribution of mobilized private finance, and the use of financial intermediaries.

The Paris Agreement's Enhanced Transparency Framework represents the most ambitious effort to date to improve climate finance transparency, establishing Common Tabular Formats and review processes for reported information. However, questions remain about whether transparency reforms are sufficient to enable meaningful accountability and whether they address underlying definitional and methodological challenges or merely make diverse approaches more visible.

**Key Papers and Contributions**

**Forstater (2012)** provides an early and influential analysis of climate finance transparency challenges and potential solutions. The report outlines principles for transparent climate finance reporting including clarity (clear definitions and methodologies), comparability (standardized formats and categories), comprehensiveness (coverage of all relevant flows), and verifiability (ability to check reported information against underlying data). Forstater identifies specific transparency gaps including the lack of project-level reporting, inconsistent treatment of non-grant instruments, and inadequate information on mobilized private finance. The report recommends developing common reporting formats, establishing independent verification mechanisms, and improving data systems. While published over a decade ago, many of the transparency challenges Forstater identified remain unresolved (Forstater, 2012).

**Falduto and Ellis (2019)** analyze options for Common Tabular Formats (CTFs) for reporting climate finance under the Paris Agreement's Enhanced Transparency Framework. Their analysis examines how CTFs can facilitate comparability while accommodating methodological diversity. They discuss options for reporting climate-specific vs total project values, disaggregating by instrument type and marker category, and providing methodological transparency. The report highlights trade-offs between standardization (which improves comparability) and flexibility (which accommodates diverse institutional contexts). Their work has been influential in UNFCCC negotiations on CTF design (Falduto & Ellis, 2019).

**Bergsvik et al. (2024)** assess whether transparency reforms under the Paris Agreement are furthering clarity in climate finance governance. Their analysis suggests that transparency mechanisms may increase the volume of reported information without necessarily improving comparability or enabling verification. They argue that transparency is a necessary but insufficient condition for accountability, and that substantive accounting standards are needed alongside procedural transparency requirements. The paper raises important questions about the relationship between transparency as disclosure and transparency as meaningful accountability (Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**Caruso and Ellis (2013)** identify specific double-counting channels in mobilized private finance reporting. When multiple public actors are involved in a project (e.g., bilateral provider, MDB, climate fund), each may claim credit for mobilizing the same private investment. Similarly, when public finance flows through financial intermediaries, both the provider to the intermediary and the intermediary to the final project may count the same flow. The report documents that double-counting risks are substantial and that aggregating reported figures without adjustments could severely over-count mobilized private finance (Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

**Caruso and Jachnik (2014)** examine data sources for tracking private climate finance, assessing their coverage, classification systems, and transparency. They find that commercial investment databases have incomplete coverage and inconsistent climate-relevant classification, while public data sources often lack project-level detail. The analysis reveals that data infrastructure limitations are a major constraint on transparency and that improving transparency requires investment in data systems alongside reporting requirements (Caruso & Jachnik, 2014).

**Carty et al. (2018)** provide civil society perspective on transparency through Oxfam's shadow report. The report criticizes official climate finance reporting for lack of transparency about methodologies, over-counting through face value reporting of loans, and inclusion of non-concessional flows. The shadow report applies alternative methodologies (grant-equivalent valuation, exclusion of non-concessional flows) to estimate "true" climate finance, finding it substantially lower than official figures. While the report's methodology is contested, it has been influential in highlighting transparency deficits and the implications of methodological choices (Carty et al., 2018).

**Roberts and Weikmans (2017)** situate transparency debates within the broader political economy of climate finance, arguing that the lack of agreed accounting modalities is not merely a technical failure but serves developed country interests by allowing flexibility in reporting. They argue that transparency reforms that focus on procedural requirements without addressing substantive definitional and methodological issues may increase the volume of reported information without improving accountability or trust (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Agustini and Arifa (2024)** document the lack of standardization in climate change accounting and disclosure practices at the corporate level, showing how the absence of mandatory standards creates transparency deficits. Their analysis reveals that disclosure practices vary widely across companies and countries, limiting comparability and creating opportunities for selective or strategic disclosure. They call for standardization of disclosure requirements to improve transparency and accountability (Agustini & Arifa, 2024).

**Methodological Approaches**

Research on transparency and double counting employs several approaches:

- **Transparency framework analysis**: Examination of reporting requirements, formats, and review processes under UNFCCC and other frameworks (Falduto & Ellis, 2019; Bergsvik et al., 2024)
- **Double-counting identification**: Systematic analysis of reporting systems to identify specific channels through which double counting can occur (Caruso & Ellis, 2013)
- **Data source assessment**: Evaluation of available data sources for tracking climate finance, assessing coverage, quality, and transparency (Caruso & Jachnik, 2014)
- **Alternative accounting**: Application of alternative methodologies to official data to reveal implications of methodological choices (Carty et al., 2018)
- **Disclosure analysis**: Examination of corporate and institutional disclosure practices to assess transparency and comparability (Agustini & Arifa, 2024)

**Controversies and Debates**

Key controversies include:

1. **Transparency vs Flexibility**: Should reporting requirements be highly standardized (improving comparability but reducing flexibility) or should flexibility be allowed to accommodate diverse contexts (preserving flexibility but limiting comparability)?

2. **Verification**: Should reported climate finance be subject to independent verification, or is self-reporting with methodological transparency sufficient? What would verification entail, and who would conduct it?

3. **Project-Level Disclosure**: Should climate finance reporting include project-level information (enabling detailed scrutiny but raising confidentiality concerns) or only aggregate figures (protecting confidentiality but limiting transparency)?

4. **Double-Counting Prevention**: What mechanisms are needed to prevent double counting? Should there be a central registry of climate finance flows? Should attribution rules be standardized?

5. **Transparency and Trust**: Can transparency reforms rebuild trust between developed and developing countries, or are deeper reforms to definitions and methodologies required?

**Research Gaps**

Despite growing attention to transparency, significant gaps remain:

- **Empirical double-counting assessment**: Systematic analysis attempting to quantify the extent of double counting in reported climate finance, using data reconciliation and matching methods.

- **Transparency effectiveness**: Research on whether transparency reforms actually improve accountability and decision-making, or whether they primarily serve symbolic functions.

- **Verification mechanisms**: Analysis of options for independent verification of climate finance reporting, including feasibility, costs, and institutional arrangements.

- **Data infrastructure**: Research on the data systems and institutional capacities required to support transparent climate finance reporting, particularly in developing countries.

- **Comparative transparency analysis**: Cross-national or cross-institutional comparison of transparency practices, identifying best practices and common challenges.

- **Political economy of transparency**: Analysis of how transparency reforms are negotiated and implemented, including which actors support or resist transparency and why.

For scholars of economic governance, climate finance transparency offers insights into the challenges of creating accountability in complex, multi-actor systems and the relationship between procedural transparency and substantive accountability.

### 5.F Institutional Production of Numbers

**Conceptual Overview**

Climate finance numbers—the quantitative representations of flows, commitments, and needs that populate policy debates and international negotiations—are not simply discovered but are produced through institutional processes involving data collection, classification, calculation, and reporting. Understanding how institutions produce authoritative climate finance numbers requires examining the organizational practices, data systems, methodological choices, and quality control mechanisms through which numbers are generated. It also requires attention to how institutional mandates, interests, and cultures shape what gets measured and how, and how numbers acquire authority and legitimacy.

The institutional production of climate finance numbers involves multiple actors: the OECD DAC compiles and publishes data on climate-relevant development finance; multilateral development banks track and report their climate finance using joint methodologies; the UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance synthesizes information from Party reports; national governments produce climate finance reports for international and domestic audiences; and civil society organizations generate alternative estimates and critiques. These diverse institutional producers employ different methodologies, serve different constituencies, and produce numbers that are often incommensurable.

Despite the importance of institutional number production for understanding climate finance governance, explicit research on this topic remains limited. Most scholarship treats reported numbers as data to be analyzed rather than as accomplishments to be explained. Institutional ethnographies, organizational studies, and science and technology studies perspectives that could illuminate number production processes are largely absent from the climate finance literature.

**Key Papers and Contributions**

**Funder and Dupuy (2022)** provide valuable institutional ethnography of climate finance coordination in Zambia, tracing how international norms and practices are enacted and adapted in a specific country context. While not focused specifically on number production, their analysis reveals how institutional arrangements shape what gets counted and reported. They document the World Bank's early influence in establishing coordination mechanisms and how this shaped which actors and activities were recognized as part of the climate finance landscape. Their work demonstrates the value of grounded, country-level analysis for understanding how climate finance categories operate in practice and how institutional politics shape measurement and reporting (Funder & Dupuy, 2022).

**Larrea (2024)** examines climate funds under the UNFCCC, analyzing their institutional design, governance, and operational practices. The chapter discusses how fund mandates and eligibility criteria shape what activities receive financing and how funds report their activities. While primarily focused on fund effectiveness and development impacts, the analysis reveals how institutional design choices affect measurement and reporting practices. Different funds employ different definitions of climate finance, eligibility criteria, and reporting formats, contributing to the fragmentation of the climate finance landscape (Larrea, 2024).

**MDB Joint Reports (2016, 2017)** document the multilateral development banks' joint methodology for tracking climate finance. These reports describe the institutional processes through which MDBs identify, classify, and aggregate climate-relevant investments. They reveal that MDB tracking involves activity-based eligibility lists, project-level tagging, and aggregation across diverse portfolios. The joint reports represent an important effort at standardization across MDBs, but they also reveal institutional differences in how policy-based lending, financial intermediary lending, and mobilized private finance are treated. The reports provide transparency about methodological choices but limited insight into the organizational processes through which numbers are produced (MDBs, 2016, 2017).

**Jachnik et al. (2015)** and **Caruso and Ellis (2013)**, while primarily focused on methodological development, provide insights into OECD institutional practices for producing climate finance numbers. Their reports describe data collection processes, the role of the OECD DAC Creditor Reporting System, and the challenges of compiling data from diverse providers using diverse methodologies. They reveal that OECD climate finance numbers are produced through a process of data submission by providers, quality checks by the OECD Secretariat, and aggregation using standardized formats. However, the reports provide limited insight into the organizational dynamics, negotiations, and judgments involved in this process (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

**Roberts and Weikmans (2017)** offer critical political economy analysis of how institutional interests shape climate finance accounting. They argue that the OECD DAC's role as both methodological standard-setter and data provider creates conflicts of interest, as the DAC serves donor countries who benefit from permissive accounting rules. They suggest that the fragmentation of climate finance accounting reflects not just technical complexity but also institutional interests in maintaining flexibility and avoiding accountability. Their analysis highlights the need to examine how institutional mandates and constituencies shape number production (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Bergsvik et al. (2024)** examine transparency mechanisms under the Paris Agreement, including the institutional arrangements for reporting and review. Their analysis suggests that transparency reforms focus on procedural requirements (what information to report, in what format) without addressing the institutional processes through which numbers are produced or the substantive methodological choices involved. They argue that understanding transparency requires examining not just what information is disclosed but how it is produced and by whom (Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**Methodological Approaches**

Research on institutional production of numbers employs limited methodological approaches, reflecting the underdevelopment of this research area:

- **Institutional analysis**: Examination of organizational mandates, governance structures, and operational practices (Larrea, 2024; Funder & Dupuy, 2022)
- **Document analysis**: Review of methodological guidance, reporting formats, and institutional reports to infer production processes (MDBs, 2016, 2017; Jachnik et al., 2015)
- **Critical political economy**: Analysis of how institutional interests shape accounting practices and number production (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017)

**Controversies and Debates**

Several issues animate debates about institutional number production:

1. **Institutional Conflicts of Interest**: Does the OECD DAC's dual role as methodological standard-setter and data provider create conflicts of interest? Should these functions be separated?

2. **Standardization vs Institutional Diversity**: Should all institutions use the same methodologies for tracking climate finance (improving comparability) or should methodologies reflect diverse institutional mandates and contexts (preserving institutional autonomy)?

3. **Quality Control**: What quality control mechanisms exist to ensure accuracy and consistency of reported climate finance? Are self-reporting and peer review sufficient, or is independent verification needed?

4. **Institutional Capacity**: Do all institutions have the capacity to produce high-quality climate finance data? What support is needed to strengthen institutional capacity, particularly in developing countries?

5. **Legitimacy and Authority**: How do particular institutions and their numbers acquire authority in climate finance debates? What makes some numbers more influential than others?

**Research Gaps**

The institutional production of climate finance numbers is severely understudied, representing a major gap for scholars of economic expertise and governance:

- **Institutional ethnographies**: Detailed ethnographic or interview-based research examining how climate finance numbers are produced within specific organizations (OECD, MDBs, national agencies, climate funds). Such research could reveal the practical judgments, negotiations, and compromises involved in generating authoritative numbers.

- **Comparative institutional analysis**: Systematic comparison of how different institutions produce climate finance numbers, examining differences in data systems, methodological choices, quality control mechanisms, and organizational cultures.

- **Historical institutionalism**: Research tracing the historical development of institutional capacities and practices for tracking climate finance, examining path dependence and critical junctures.

- **Sociology of quantification**: Application of sociology of quantification frameworks to examine how climate finance numbers acquire authority, how they circulate across institutional contexts, and how they shape policy and practice.

- **Organizational studies**: Research on the organizational dynamics of number production, including coordination across units, management of methodological uncertainty, and responses to external critique.

- **Capacity assessment**: Systematic assessment of institutional capacities for climate finance tracking and reporting, particularly in developing countries, identifying gaps and support needs.

For historians of economic ideas and scholars of expertise, the institutional production of climate finance numbers offers a rich empirical domain for examining how quantitative authority is constructed and maintained in contemporary governance contexts.

### 5.G Global South Critiques and Perspectives

**Conceptual Overview**

Global South perspectives on climate finance emphasize equity, justice, historical responsibility, and the adequacy of finance relative to needs. These perspectives challenge dominant framings that treat climate finance primarily as a technical or economic question, instead foregrounding its political, ethical, and distributional dimensions. Global South critiques highlight how climate finance definitions, accounting methodologies, and governance arrangements reflect and reproduce North-South power asymmetries, and how they often fail to address developing countries' priorities and circumstances.

Key themes in Global South perspectives include: (1) the inadequacy of climate finance relative to developing countries' needs and developed countries' historical responsibility; (2) the problematic predominance of loans over grants, raising debt sustainability concerns; (3) the lack of country ownership and alignment with national priorities; (4) the transaction costs and complexity of accessing climate finance; (5) the over-counting of climate finance through permissive accounting methodologies; and (6) the need for loss and damage finance alongside mitigation and adaptation support.

Despite the importance of Global South perspectives, they remain underrepresented in the climate finance literature. The corpus is dominated by Global North authors and institutions, with limited engagement with scholarship from developing countries or with non-Anglophone literature. This geographic and linguistic bias represents a significant limitation and a priority area for future research.

**Key Papers and Contributions**

**Timilsena (2023)** provides a systematic literature review of climate finance practices in developing countries, synthesizing evidence on governance challenges, reporting difficulties, and the gap between finance received and needs. The review emphasizes recipient country perspectives on the quality of climate finance, including concerns about the predominance of loans over grants, the transaction costs of accessing climate funds, and the misalignment between donor priorities and national development plans. Timilsena highlights that developing countries face multiple challenges in accessing and utilizing climate finance, including limited institutional capacity, complex application procedures, and inadequate technical support. The review calls for reforms to improve accessibility, country ownership, and alignment with national priorities (Timilsena, 2023).

**Sarfo-Adu and Kokofu (2023)** link climate finance to justice frameworks, proposing a "SAVE" framework (Sustainability, Adaptation, Vulnerability, and Equity) for optimizing climate interventions. Their analysis emphasizes that climate finance must address the disproportionate vulnerability of developing countries and communities to climate impacts, and that finance allocation should be guided by equity principles rather than purely economic criteria. They argue that current climate finance governance inadequately addresses justice concerns and that reforms are needed to center equity and vulnerability in finance allocation and governance (Sarfo-Adu & Kokofu, 2023).

**Funder and Dupuy (2022)** provide institutional ethnography of climate finance coordination in Zambia, documenting how international norms and donor preferences interact with domestic priorities and institutional arrangements. Their analysis reveals tensions between donor preferences for climate-specific interventions and recipient country preferences for integrated development approaches. They show how the World Bank's early influence in establishing coordination mechanisms shaped institutional arrangements in ways that privileged particular actors and approaches, with implications for country ownership. The study demonstrates the value of grounded, country-level analysis for understanding how climate finance operates in practice and how power dynamics shape outcomes (Funder & Dupuy, 2022).

**Desalegn and Tangl (2022)** analyze the green finance gap and barriers to inclusive green growth in developing countries. Their analysis emphasizes regulatory and institutional constraints that limit both public and private climate finance, including policy uncertainty, weak enforcement, limited financial market development, and inadequate institutional capacity. They argue that addressing the green finance gap requires not just scaled-up finance but also substantial investment in institutional capacity, regulatory frameworks, and enabling environments. Their analysis highlights that finance alone is insufficient without the institutional infrastructure to deploy it effectively (Desalegn & Tangl, 2022).

**Savvidou et al. (2021)** quantify international public finance for climate change adaptation in Africa, revealing that much adaptation finance is provided as loans rather than grants. Their analysis raises concerns about the appropriateness of loans for adaptation (which generates limited revenue for repayment) and about debt sustainability. They also document that adaptation finance to Africa is inadequate relative to assessed needs and that finance is unevenly distributed across countries and regions. The study provides important empirical evidence for Global South critiques of climate finance quality and adequacy (Savvidou et al., 2021).

**Larrea (2024)** examines climate funds under the UNFCCC through case studies that highlight recipient country experiences and development impacts. The analysis reveals that fund governance and operational practices significantly affect whether finance reaches intended beneficiaries and achieves development co-benefits. Larrea emphasizes the importance of country ownership, participatory processes, and alignment with national development priorities for fund effectiveness. The chapter provides evidence that governance arrangements matter as much as finance volumes for achieving equitable and effective climate action (Larrea, 2024).

**Carty et al. (2018)** provide civil society perspective through Oxfam's shadow report, which applies alternative accounting methodologies that emphasize grant-equivalent valuation and exclude non-concessional flows. The shadow report argues that much of what developed countries count as climate finance is actually loans that will be repaid, and that "true" climate finance is substantially lower than officially reported figures. While produced by a Global North NGO, the shadow report amplifies Global South concerns about over-counting and the predominance of loans. It has been influential in policy debates and represents an important alternative accounting perspective (Carty et al., 2018).

**Farroñán et al. (2025)** provide a systematic review focused on emerging economies, emphasizing that instrument effectiveness depends on institutional capacity and that one-size-fits-all prescriptions are inappropriate. Their analysis highlights geographic gaps in the literature, with sub-Saharan Africa representing only 16% of studies despite acute financing barriers. They call for research prioritizing underrepresented regions and for policy approaches tailored to institutional realities rather than assuming universal applicability of instruments developed in high-income contexts (Farroñán et al., 2025).

**Methodological Approaches**

Research centering Global South perspectives employs several approaches:

- **Systematic reviews**: Synthesis of evidence on climate finance practices and challenges in developing countries (Timilsena, 2023; Farroñán et al., 2025)
- **Case studies**: Detailed analysis of climate finance in specific country contexts, revealing implementation challenges and local perspectives (Funder & Dupuy, 2022; Larrea, 2024)
- **Quantitative analysis**: Empirical assessment of climate finance flows to developing countries, revealing gaps and inequities (Savvidou et al., 2021)
- **Normative analysis**: Articulation of justice frameworks and equity principles for climate finance (Sarfo-Adu & Kokofu, 2023)
- **Alternative accounting**: Application of methodologies that reflect developing country priorities (grant-equivalent valuation, needs-based assessment) (Carty et al., 2018)

**Controversies and Debates**

Key issues in Global South perspectives include:

1. **Adequacy**: Is climate finance adequate relative to developing countries' needs and developed countries' historical responsibility? Official estimates of needs (hundreds of billions to trillions annually) far exceed current flows.

2. **Quality**: Should climate finance be primarily grants or are loans acceptable? Developing countries argue that loans create debt burdens and that adaptation finance in particular should be grant-based.

3. **Additionality**: Is climate finance genuinely additional to development assistance, or does it represent relabeling? Developing countries view additionality as a fundamental commitment that has not been honored.

4. **Access**: Are climate finance access procedures too complex and burdensome? Developing countries, particularly least developed countries and small island states, report significant barriers to accessing climate funds.

5. **Ownership**: Do climate finance governance arrangements ensure country ownership and alignment with national priorities, or do they reflect donor preferences and conditionalities?

6. **Loss and Damage**: Should climate finance cover loss and damage from climate impacts, not just mitigation and adaptation? Developing countries have long advocated for loss and damage finance, which was formally recognized at COP27 (2022) but remains contentious.

**Research Gaps**

Global South perspectives remain underrepresented in climate finance scholarship, creating several priority research gaps:

- **Developing country authorship**: More research authored by scholars based in developing countries, bringing local knowledge and perspectives. This requires addressing structural barriers including research funding, publication access, and language.

- **Non-Anglophone literature**: Systematic engagement with climate finance scholarship in languages other than English, particularly Spanish, French, Portuguese, Arabic, and Chinese. This requires translation efforts and recognition of diverse knowledge traditions.

- **Recipient country perspectives**: More research centering recipient country governments', civil society organizations', and communities' perspectives on climate finance, including through participatory research methods.

- **Justice and equity frameworks**: Theoretical and empirical work developing and applying justice and equity frameworks to climate finance, moving beyond efficiency-focused economic analysis.

- **Needs assessment**: Rigorous, country-driven assessment of climate finance needs, moving beyond top-down estimates to bottom-up, participatory approaches.

- **South-South cooperation**: Research on South-South climate finance and cooperation, which operates outside traditional North-South frameworks and may offer alternative models.

- **Intellectual genealogies**: Historical research tracing the development of Global South critiques and alternative framings of climate finance, recovering intellectual traditions that have been marginalized in dominant narratives.

For historians of economic ideas, Global South perspectives offer crucial insights into how economic categories and frameworks are contested from positions of relative powerlessness, how alternative knowledge traditions challenge dominant paradigms, and how geography and geopolitics shape the production and circulation of economic expertise.

## 6. Disciplinary Contributions

Climate finance scholarship draws on multiple disciplinary traditions, each bringing distinctive conceptual frameworks, methodological approaches, and normative orientations. This section distinguishes between theoretical contributions, methodological and accounting papers, policy-oriented institutional reports, and historical and STS approaches, examining how different disciplinary communities have shaped the field.

### 6.1 Theoretical Contributions

Theoretical contributions to climate finance scholarship develop conceptual frameworks, normative theories, and analytical models for understanding climate finance phenomena. These contributions span multiple disciplines including environmental economics, development economics, political economy, international relations, and increasingly, critical theory and science and technology studies.

**Environmental Economics Frameworks**: Environmental economics has provided foundational theoretical frameworks for climate finance, particularly concepts of global public goods, externalities, and market failures. The framing of climate change as a global public good requiring international cooperation and financial transfers draws on environmental economics literature on international environmental agreements. Concepts of incremental costs, additionality, and cost-effectiveness in climate finance reflect environmental economics approaches to valuing environmental interventions. However, environmental economics frameworks have been criticized for insufficient attention to equity, justice, and power relations, and for treating institutional and political factors as exogenous constraints rather than central analytical concerns (Polzin, 2017).

**Development Economics and Finance**: Development economics perspectives emphasize the relationship between climate finance and broader development objectives, the role of institutions and governance, and the political economy of aid and development cooperation. Concepts of aid effectiveness, country ownership, and alignment with national priorities have been adapted from development economics to climate finance contexts. Development finance literature on concessional lending, debt sustainability, and the role of multilateral development banks informs climate finance debates about instrument choice and institutional arrangements. However, development economics approaches have sometimes struggled to incorporate the distinctive features of climate finance, particularly its global public good character and the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities (Timilsena, 2023; Funder & Dupuy, 2022).

**Political Economy and International Relations**: Political economy and international relations theories provide frameworks for understanding climate finance as a site of North-South contestation, institutional politics, and power relations. Concepts of regime effectiveness, institutional design, and compliance mechanisms from international relations inform analysis of climate finance governance. Political economy perspectives on the distributional consequences of policy choices, the role of interests and ideas, and the dynamics of international negotiations illuminate climate finance debates. Critical political economy approaches examine how climate finance reflects and reproduces global inequalities and how accounting practices serve particular interests (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017; Purdon, n.d.).

**Justice and Equity Theories**: Normative theories of justice and equity have been increasingly applied to climate finance, particularly by scholars and advocates from the Global South. These frameworks emphasize historical responsibility, the polluter pays principle, capacity to pay, and the rights of vulnerable populations. Justice perspectives critique efficiency-focused economic frameworks for inadequate attention to distributional concerns and for treating equity as a constraint rather than a central objective. They argue for needs-based rather than market-based approaches to climate finance allocation and for centering the perspectives and priorities of affected communities (Sarfo-Adu & Kokofu, 2023).

**Science and Technology Studies**: STS perspectives, while still marginal in climate finance scholarship, offer theoretical frameworks for understanding quantification, commensuration, and the performativity of metrics. STS concepts of boundary work, standardization, and the co-production of knowledge and social order illuminate how climate finance categories are constructed and contested. Performativity theory examines how measurement practices shape the phenomena being measured. Sociology of quantification frameworks analyze how numbers acquire authority and how quantification regimes distribute power and resources. These perspectives remain underdeveloped in climate finance but offer significant potential for future theoretical work (de Aguiar et al., 2024; Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**Theoretical Gaps**: Despite diverse disciplinary engagement, several theoretical gaps persist. There is limited integration across disciplinary frameworks, with environmental economics, development economics, and political economy perspectives often operating in parallel rather than dialogue. Theories of institutional change and path dependence could illuminate the persistence of fragmented accounting systems. Theories of expertise and authority could explain how particular institutions and methodologies acquire legitimacy. Feminist and postcolonial theories could provide critical perspectives on power relations and knowledge production in climate finance. These theoretical gaps represent opportunities for future scholarship.

### 6.2 Methodological and Accounting Papers

Methodological and accounting papers develop measurement frameworks, technical standards, and practical guidance for tracking and reporting climate finance. This category includes OECD working papers on mobilized private finance estimation, MDB joint reports on climate finance tracking, analyses of Rio Markers and grant-equivalent valuation, and proposals for Common Tabular Formats under the Paris Agreement. These papers are often produced by international organizations and think tanks rather than academic researchers, reflecting the policy-oriented nature of methodological development work.

**OECD Methodological Guidance**: The OECD has been the most prolific producer of methodological guidance, particularly on mobilized private finance estimation. The series of working papers by Jachnik, Caruso, Ellis, and colleagues (2013-2015) established conceptual frameworks, outlined decision points, compared existing approaches, and tested practical methods. These papers combine technical rigor with pragmatic attention to data availability and institutional feasibility. They acknowledge methodological limitations and trade-offs while providing actionable guidance for providers and policymakers. The OECD's methodological work has been influential in shaping both provider practices and UNFCCC negotiations, though it has also been criticized for reflecting donor perspectives and interests (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013; Caruso & Jachnik, 2014; Falduto & Ellis, 2019).

**MDB Joint Methodologies**: Multilateral development banks have developed joint methodologies for tracking climate finance, documented in annual joint reports. These methodologies use activity-based eligibility criteria, project-level tagging, and standardized aggregation approaches. The MDB joint reports provide transparency about methodological choices and represent an important effort at standardization across institutions. However, the MDB approach differs from OECD approaches in important respects (treatment of policy-based lending, use of eligibility lists rather than markers, inclusion of full project values), contributing to the fragmentation of accounting systems. The MDB methodologies reflect the distinctive mandates and operational contexts of development banks (MDBs, 2016, 2017).

**Rio Marker Analysis**: Several papers analyze the OECD DAC Rio Marker system, examining its credibility, limitations, and implications for climate finance accounting. Weikmans' empirical testing of adaptation markers in Vietnam provides crucial evidence of over-crediting. Roberts and Weikmans' critique situates marker limitations within broader political economy of climate finance accounting. These analyses combine technical assessment of marker methodology with critical examination of its institutional context and political consequences (Weikmans, n.d.; Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Grant-Equivalent Valuation**: Papers on grant-equivalent valuation examine methods for valuing non-grant instruments, particularly loans and equity. Bachus and Bécault's analysis of equity valuation methods is the most comprehensive treatment, reviewing approaches and situating debates within OECD DAC modernization. Pauw et al. examine implications of face value vs grant-equivalent accounting for the post-2025 NCQG. These papers combine technical accounting analysis with attention to political and policy implications (Bachus & Bécault, 2017; Pauw et al., 2022).

**Transparency and Reporting Frameworks**: Papers on transparency examine reporting requirements, Common Tabular Formats, and review mechanisms under the Paris Agreement. Falduto and Ellis' analysis of CTF options is the most detailed technical treatment. Forstater's earlier work on transparency principles established a framework that has influenced subsequent debates. Bergsvik et al. provide critical assessment of whether transparency reforms are achieving their objectives (Forstater, 2012; Falduto & Ellis, 2019; Bergsvik et al., 2024).

**Methodological Gaps**: Despite substantial methodological development work, several gaps remain. There is limited empirical validation of methodologies, with most papers proposing frameworks rather than testing them rigorously. Comparative assessment of different methodologies' accuracy, incentive effects, and practical feasibility is limited. Methods for assessing additionality and causality in mobilization remain underdeveloped. Alternative methodologies reflecting recipient country perspectives or justice frameworks are scarce. These gaps represent priorities for future methodological research.

### 6.3 Policy-Oriented Institutional Reports

Policy-oriented institutional reports constitute a substantial portion of the climate finance corpus, produced by international organizations (OECD, UNFCCC, MDBs), think tanks (SEI, ODI, CPI, WRI), and civil society organizations (Oxfam, ActionAid). These reports serve multiple functions: providing data and analysis to inform policy debates, offering recommendations for institutional reforms, and advocating for particular positions in negotiations. While often not peer-reviewed, institutional reports are influential in policy circles and provide essential empirical and analytical infrastructure for the field.

**UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance**: The SCF produces biennial assessments of climate finance flows, synthesizing information from Party reports and other sources. These assessments document the fragmentation of reporting and the challenges of aggregation, while attempting to provide comprehensive overviews of global climate finance. The SCF also produces technical papers on specific issues (e.g., definitions, methodologies, needs assessment) that inform UNFCCC negotiations. SCF reports reflect the political dynamics of the UNFCCC process, with developing and developed countries often disagreeing on key findings and recommendations.

**OECD Reports**: Beyond methodological guidance, the OECD produces regular reports on climate finance provided and mobilized by developed countries, using DAC data and methodologies. These reports are the primary source of aggregate data on climate-relevant development finance and mobilized private finance. The OECD also produces thematic reports on specific issues (e.g., subnational climate finance, climate finance for adaptation). OECD reports are influential but also contested, particularly by developing countries and civil society who view OECD methodologies as over-counting climate finance.

**MDB Reports**: In addition to joint reports on climate finance tracking, individual MDBs produce reports on their climate finance activities, strategies, and impacts. These reports provide transparency about MDB operations and demonstrate institutional commitment to climate objectives. However, they also serve institutional interests in demonstrating relevance and impact, and may present optimistic assessments of effectiveness.

**Think Tank Reports**: Think tanks produce diverse reports including data compilations (CPI's Global Landscape of Climate Finance), policy analyses (SEI, ODI reports on specific issues), and advocacy-oriented assessments. Think tank reports often bridge academic research and policy practice, synthesizing evidence and offering actionable recommendations. They play important roles in agenda-setting and in providing independent analysis, though they also reflect the perspectives and funding sources of their organizations.

**Civil Society Shadow Reports**: Civil society organizations, particularly development and environmental NGOs, produce "shadow reports" that critically assess official climate finance reporting and advocate for reforms. Oxfam's shadow reports are the most prominent, applying alternative accounting methodologies and arguing that official figures over-count climate finance. These reports serve advocacy functions but also provide important alternative perspectives and methodological innovations. They have been influential in policy debates despite (or because of) their critical stance.

**Strengths and Limitations**: Policy-oriented institutional reports provide essential data, analysis, and recommendations that inform climate finance governance. They are often more timely and policy-relevant than academic publications. However, they also have limitations: they may reflect institutional interests and perspectives; they are not always subject to rigorous peer review; and they may prioritize policy relevance over analytical depth. For academic researchers, institutional reports are essential sources but require critical engagement with their methodologies, assumptions, and potential biases.

### 6.4 Historical and STS Approaches

Historical and science and technology studies (STS) approaches to climate finance remain underdeveloped relative to their potential contributions. These approaches examine how climate finance categories, institutions, and practices have evolved over time; how quantification regimes emerge and acquire authority; and how measurement practices perform political and economic work. They bring distinctive methodological tools including archival research, oral history, institutional ethnography, and discourse analysis.

**Historical Approaches**: Historical research on climate finance is limited, with most scholarship focused on contemporary policy debates rather than historical evolution. However, several papers provide historical context for current debates. Bibliometric analyses document the temporal evolution of scholarship, identifying growth periods and thematic shifts (Kouwenberg & Zheng, 2023; Shang & Jin, 2023). Some papers trace the evolution of specific concepts or institutions (e.g., CDM, climate funds, Rio Markers), though often as background rather than primary focus. Comprehensive intellectual histories of climate finance concepts, accounting categories, or institutional arrangements are largely absent from the corpus.

**Potential Historical Research**: Several historical research agendas would make valuable contributions. Intellectual histories could trace the genealogies of key concepts (additionality, mobilization, concessionality, climate specificity), examining their origins, evolution, and contestation. Institutional histories could examine the development of climate finance tracking systems within the OECD, MDBs, and UNFCCC, using archival research and oral histories to reconstruct decision-making processes and path dependencies. Comparative histories could examine how different countries or institutions have approached climate finance over time, revealing diverse trajectories and alternative possibilities. Histories of quantification could examine how climate finance measurement practices emerged and evolved, situating them within broader histories of economic statistics and development indicators.

**STS Approaches**: Science and technology studies perspectives on climate finance are rare but potentially transformative. STS frameworks examine how scientific and technical knowledge is produced, how it acquires authority, and how it shapes social and political outcomes. Applied to climate finance, STS approaches could examine: how climate finance categories are constructed and stabilized; how quantification practices perform political work; how accounting standards acquire legitimacy; how measurement regimes shape investment decisions and policy priorities; and how expertise is produced and contested in climate finance governance.

**Existing STS-Informed Work**: While explicit STS frameworks are rare, some papers employ STS-adjacent concepts. Roberts and Weikmans' analysis of accounting fragmentation and trust draws on political economy but resonates with STS concerns about standardization and commensuration. Bergsvik et al.'s examination of transparency and clarity engages with questions about the relationship between procedural and substantive accountability. de Aguiar et al.'s call for critical accounting perspectives on climate finance aligns with STS interests in the social construction of quantitative authority. However, none of these papers fully develops STS theoretical frameworks or employs characteristic STS methods such as ethnography or discourse analysis (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017; Bergsvik et al., 2024; de Aguiar et al., 2024).

**Methodological Opportunities**: STS and historical approaches offer distinctive methodological opportunities for climate finance research. Archival research could access institutional records, meeting minutes, and internal documents that reveal how climate finance categories and methodologies were developed. Oral histories could capture the perspectives of key actors involved in institutional development and methodological negotiations. Institutional ethnography could observe how climate finance numbers are produced within organizations, revealing practical judgments and negotiations. Discourse analysis could examine how climate finance is framed and contested in policy documents, negotiations, and public debates. These methods could generate insights unavailable through conventional policy analysis or econometric approaches.

**Disciplinary Integration**: The underdevelopment of historical and STS approaches represents a significant gap in climate finance scholarship. These approaches could provide crucial insights into how climate finance governance has evolved, how measurement practices shape outcomes, and how expertise is produced and contested. They could also facilitate dialogue between technical and critical perspectives, between policy-oriented and theoretically-informed research, and between Global North and Global South knowledge traditions. Developing historical and STS research agendas should be a priority for the field, particularly for scholars interested in the history of economic ideas and economic methodology.

## 7. Major Authors, Institutions, and Schools of Thought

### 7.1 Leading Scholars and Their Intellectual Contributions

Climate finance scholarship has been shaped by a relatively small number of highly productive and influential scholars whose work spans multiple themes and methodological approaches. This section profiles leading scholars and their distinctive intellectual contributions, with attention to how their work has shaped debates and opened new research directions.

**J. Timmons Roberts** (Brown University) stands out as perhaps the most influential critical voice in climate finance scholarship. Roberts' work combines empirical analysis of finance flows with normative arguments about equity and justice, and critical examination of how accounting practices shape international climate politics. His collaboration with Romain Weikmans has produced seminal analyses of the fragmentation of climate finance definitions and the erosion of trust between developed and developing countries. Roberts' intellectual contribution lies in demonstrating that climate finance accounting is not merely a technical challenge but a political arena where power relations are enacted and contested. His work bridges quantitative analysis, institutional analysis, and normative theory, and has been influential in both academic and policy circles. Roberts represents a critical political economy school of thought that interrogates how climate finance governance reflects and reproduces global inequalities (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Romain Weikmans** (Université Libre de Bruxelles) has made foundational contributions to understanding the credibility of climate finance accounting, particularly through empirical testing of OECD Rio Markers. Weikmans' case study work demonstrating the over-crediting of adaptation finance in Vietnam and other contexts has provided crucial evidence for critiques of marker-based accounting. His intellectual contribution lies in combining quantitative analysis of reported flows with qualitative institutional analysis and ground-truthing against project-level evidence. Weikmans' work exemplifies rigorous empirical research in service of normative concerns about accountability and equity. His scholarship has been influential in policy debates over accounting methodologies and has established empirical validation as an essential complement to methodological development (Weikmans, n.d.).

**Raphaël Jachnik** (OECD) has been the principal architect of methodological frameworks for estimating mobilized private climate finance. His series of OECD working papers with Randy Caruso and colleagues (2013-2015) established the conceptual foundations and practical options for tracking private finance mobilized by public interventions. Jachnik's intellectual contribution lies in translating complex methodological questions into structured decision frameworks that acknowledge trade-offs and limitations while providing actionable guidance. His work exemplifies policy-oriented methodological research that combines technical rigor with pragmatic attention to institutional feasibility and data constraints. While working within an OECD institutional context, Jachnik's reports acknowledge methodological limitations and have provided essential technical infrastructure for subsequent debates. His work represents a technocratic problem-solving approach that seeks to improve measurement while recognizing that perfect measurement is unattainable (Jachnik et al., 2015; Jachnik & Raynaud, 2015).

**Randy Caruso and Jane Ellis** (OECD) have contributed foundational comparative analyses of climate finance definitions, methodologies, and data sources. Their 2013 report comparing 24 actors' definitions and methods for estimating mobilized finance documented the extent of methodological heterogeneity and double-counting risks, establishing the empirical basis for calls for standardization. Their work on data sources and reporting tables has shaped UNFCCC transparency framework negotiations. Caruso and Ellis' intellectual contribution lies in systematic comparative analysis that reveals the implications of methodological diversity for aggregation and accountability. Their work exemplifies empirical institutional analysis that informs both methodological development and policy negotiations (Caruso & Ellis, 2013; Caruso & Jachnik, 2014; Falduto & Ellis, 2019).

**Martin Stadelmann and Axel Michaelowa** (University of Zurich, Perspectives Climate Group) have produced important work on additionality baselines and the difficulties of accounting for private finance. Their systematic assessment of options for defining "new and additional" finance has been widely cited in policy debates, while their analysis of leverage ratios and concessionality measurement has highlighted technical challenges in valuing climate finance. Stadelmann and Michaelowa's intellectual contribution lies in rigorous assessment of methodological options against multiple criteria (feasibility, equity, environmental effectiveness), providing structured analysis to inform policy choices. Their work bridges academic research and policy consulting, and represents a pragmatic analytical approach that seeks to identify viable solutions to contested issues (Stadelmann et al., 2011; Stadelmann et al., 2013).

**Friedemann Polzin** (Utrecht University) has made significant contributions to understanding barriers to private climate finance mobilization, particularly for low-carbon innovation. His systematic review of barriers and policy solutions has been influential in both academic and policy circles, framing mobilization as an institutional and political-economic challenge rather than merely a market failure. Polzin's intellectual contribution lies in integrating insights from innovation studies, institutional economics, and policy analysis to develop comprehensive frameworks for understanding and addressing mobilization barriers. His work exemplifies interdisciplinary synthesis that generates actionable insights for policy and practice (Polzin, 2017).

**Aaron Atteridge and Adis Dzebo** (Stockholm Environment Institute) have contributed critical analyses of what counts as climate finance under international pledges, particularly examining the ambiguities in the Copenhagen commitment regarding private finance. Their work has been important for understanding how definitional vagueness shapes subsequent accounting debates and for highlighting the political stakes of seemingly technical definitional choices. Atteridge and Dzebo's intellectual contribution lies in conceptual clarification that reveals the implications of definitional choices for accountability and equity. Their work represents a critical analytical approach that interrogates the assumptions and interests embedded in policy frameworks (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015).

**Mikkel Funder** (Danish Institute for International Studies) has produced valuable institutional ethnography of climate finance coordination, tracing how global norms are localized and contested in recipient countries. His work on Zambia demonstrates the value of grounded, country-level analysis for understanding how climate finance categories operate in practice and how institutional politics shape outcomes. Funder's intellectual contribution lies in bringing ethnographic sensibilities to climate finance research, revealing the gap between global policy frameworks and local realities. His work exemplifies qualitative institutional analysis that centers recipient country perspectives and experiences (Funder & Dupuy, 2022).

**Accounting and Disclosure Scholars**: A newer generation of scholars is bringing accounting expertise to climate finance. T.R.S. de Aguiar, Shamima Haque, and Laura McCann's 2024 mapping of accounting literature on climate finance has identified critical gaps and called for greater engagement with accountability, audit, and disclosure debates. Their intellectual contribution lies in bringing critical accounting perspectives to a field that has been dominated by economists and policy analysts. Aisa Tri Agustini and Choirunnisa Arifa's work on climate change accounting and disclosure has documented the lack of standardization and its implications for comparability. These scholars represent an emerging accounting school of thought that emphasizes the social construction of climate finance numbers and the need for critical reflexivity about measurement practices (de Aguiar et al., 2024; Agustini & Arifa, 2024).

### 7.2 Key Research Institutions and Think Tanks

Climate finance research is produced by a diverse array of institutions spanning international organizations, think tanks, universities, and civil society organizations. This section profiles key institutions and their distinctive contributions to the field.

**Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD)**: The OECD, particularly its Development Co-operation Directorate and Environment Directorate, has been the most prolific and influential producer of climate finance research and guidance. The OECD's contributions include: development of Rio Markers for tracking climate-relevant development finance; methodological frameworks for estimating mobilized private finance; comparative analyses of definitions and data sources; and guidance on reporting formats and transparency. The OECD's institutional contribution lies in providing technical infrastructure and standardization efforts that enable (imperfect) comparability across diverse providers. However, the OECD's role has also been contested, with critics arguing that its methodologies reflect donor perspectives and interests and that its dual role as standard-setter and data provider creates conflicts of interest. The OECD represents a technocratic, standardization-oriented approach to climate finance governance (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013; Falduto & Ellis, 2019).

**Multilateral Development Banks (MDBs)**: The World Bank Group, Asian Development Bank, African Development Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, European Investment Bank, and European Bank for Reconstruction and Development have collectively developed joint methodologies for tracking climate finance and publish annual joint reports. The MDBs' institutional contribution lies in mainstreaming climate considerations into development finance and in demonstrating that substantial climate-relevant investment can be achieved through existing development institutions. The MDB joint methodology represents an important effort at standardization across institutions, though it differs from OECD approaches in important respects. MDBs represent an institutional integration approach that embeds climate finance within broader development mandates (MDBs, 2016, 2017).

**UNFCCC Secretariat and Standing Committee on Finance**: The UNFCCC Secretariat and its Standing Committee on Finance produce biennial assessments of climate finance flows and technical papers on specific issues. The SCF's institutional contribution lies in providing a multilateral forum for discussing climate finance issues and in attempting to synthesize diverse information sources into comprehensive assessments. However, the SCF's work is constrained by the political dynamics of the UNFCCC process and by the lack of standardized reporting from Parties. The UNFCCC represents a multilateral, consensus-based approach to climate finance governance that reflects the diversity of Party perspectives and interests.

**Stockholm Environment Institute (SEI)**: SEI has produced influential research on what counts as climate finance, the political economy of climate funds, and recipient country perspectives. SEI's institutional contribution lies in combining rigorous analysis with attention to equity and justice concerns, and in centering developing country perspectives. SEI represents a critical, equity-oriented research approach that interrogates dominant framings and advocates for reforms (Atteridge & Dzebo, 2015).

**Overseas Development Institute (ODI)**: ODI has contributed important analyses of climate finance effectiveness, recipient country experiences, and the relationship between climate and development finance. ODI's institutional contribution lies in bridging academic research and policy practice, and in providing evidence-based analysis to inform policy debates. ODI represents a policy-engaged research approach that combines empirical rigor with practical relevance.

**Climate Policy Initiative (CPI)**: CPI produces annual Global Landscape of Climate Finance reports that track flows across sources, instruments, and sectors. CPI's institutional contribution lies in providing comprehensive data on global climate finance, though its methodologies and definitions have been debated. CPI represents a data-driven, tracking-oriented approach that emphasizes comprehensive coverage and temporal trends.

**Universities**: Academic research on climate finance is distributed across numerous universities, with notable concentrations at Brown University (critical political economy), Utrecht University (innovation finance), Université Libre de Bruxelles (accounting credibility), University of Zurich and Perspectives Climate Group (additionality and carbon markets), and Danish Institute for International Studies (institutional ethnography). Universities' institutional contribution lies in producing theoretically-informed, peer-reviewed research that is less constrained by policy timelines and institutional interests than think tank or international organization research. Universities represent diverse research approaches and schools of thought.

**Civil Society Organizations**: Oxfam, ActionAid, and other development and environmental NGOs have produced "shadow reports" and advocacy-oriented analyses that critically assess official climate finance reporting. Civil society's institutional contribution lies in providing alternative perspectives and methodologies, in advocating for reforms that center equity and justice, and in holding governments and institutions accountable. Civil society represents a critical, advocacy-oriented approach that challenges dominant framings and power relations (Carty et al., 2018).

### 7.3 Schools of Thought and Paradigmatic Debates

Climate finance scholarship exhibits several distinct schools of thought, each with characteristic assumptions, methodological preferences, and normative orientations. These schools of thought are not rigidly bounded but represent recognizable intellectual traditions and communities.

**Technocratic Problem-Solving School**: This school, exemplified by OECD methodological work, approaches climate finance as a technical challenge requiring improved measurement, standardization, and data systems. It emphasizes pragmatic solutions that balance accuracy, feasibility, and incentive effects. It tends to take existing institutional arrangements and political constraints as given and to focus on incremental improvements within these constraints. Methodologically, it favors structured frameworks, comparative analysis, and empirical testing. Normatively, it emphasizes efficiency, comparability, and accountability to donors and international commitments. This school has been influential in shaping methodological guidance and reporting frameworks, but has been criticized for insufficient attention to power relations, equity, and the political stakes of methodological choices (Jachnik et al., 2015; Caruso & Ellis, 2013).

**Critical Political Economy School**: This school, exemplified by Roberts and Weikmans, approaches climate finance as a political arena where power relations are enacted and contested. It emphasizes how accounting practices reflect and reproduce global inequalities, how definitional fragmentation serves particular interests, and how measurement regimes shape policy possibilities. It tends to interrogate existing institutional arrangements and to highlight their political and distributional consequences. Methodologically, it combines quantitative analysis, institutional analysis, and normative critique. Normatively, it emphasizes equity, justice, and accountability to developing countries and affected communities. This school has been influential in critical scholarship and advocacy, but has been criticized by some for insufficient attention to practical constraints and feasible reforms (Roberts & Weikmans, 2017).

**Development Effectiveness School**: This school, exemplified by ODI and some MDB research, approaches climate finance through the lens of development effectiveness, emphasizing country ownership, alignment with national priorities, and institutional capacity. It tends to situate climate finance within broader development cooperation debates and to emphasize the importance of governance, institutions, and local context. Methodologically, it favors case studies, institutional analysis, and qualitative comparative methods. Normatively, it emphasizes effectiveness, country ownership, and development co-benefits. This school has been influential in shaping debates about climate fund governance and the relationship between climate and development finance, but has sometimes struggled to incorporate the distinctive features of climate finance as a global public good (Funder & Dupuy, 2022; Timilsena, 2023).

**Financial Economics School**: This school, less prominent in the corpus but growing, approaches climate finance through financial economics frameworks, emphasizing risk-return profiles, market efficiency, and the role of financial intermediaries. It tends to focus on green bonds, institutional investors, and the integration of climate considerations into financial regulation. Methodologically, it favors financial econometrics, asset pricing models, and market analysis. Normatively, it emphasizes efficiency, market-based solutions, and the mobilization of private capital. This school has been influential in green finance and sustainable finance debates, but has been criticized for insufficient attention to equity, the public good character of climate finance, and the limits of market-based approaches (Farroñán et al., 2025).

**Accounting and Disclosure School**: This emerging school, exemplified by de Aguiar et al. and Agustini and Arifa, approaches climate finance through accounting and audit lenses, emphasizing measurement standards, disclosure practices, and accountability mechanisms. It tends to focus on the social construction of climate finance numbers, the politics of transparency, and the gap between reported and actual flows. Methodologically, it favors accounting analysis, document review, and increasingly, critical accounting perspectives. Normatively, it emphasizes accuracy, transparency, and accountability. This school is still developing but has potential to make important contributions by bringing accounting expertise and critical reflexivity about measurement practices to climate finance (de Aguiar et al., 2024; Agustini & Arifa, 2024).

**Justice and Equity School**: This school, exemplified by some Global South scholars and civil society organizations, approaches climate finance through justice and equity frameworks, emphasizing historical responsibility, the rights of vulnerable populations, and needs-based allocation. It tends to critique efficiency-focused economic frameworks for inadequate attention to distributional concerns and to advocate for reforms that center equity and justice. Methodologically, it employs normative analysis, participatory methods, and alternative accounting approaches. Normatively, it emphasizes justice, equity, and the rights and priorities of developing countries and affected communities. This school has been influential in advocacy and in shaping normative debates, but remains underrepresented in mainstream academic literature (Sarfo-Adu & Kokofu, 2023).

**Paradigmatic Debates**: Several paradigmatic debates structure climate finance scholarship. The efficiency vs equity debate concerns whether climate finance should be allocated primarily according to economic efficiency criteria (cost-effectiveness, leverage ratios) or equity criteria (needs, vulnerability, historical responsibility). The standardization vs flexibility debate concerns whether accounting methodologies should be highly standardized (improving comparability) or flexible (accommodating diverse contexts). The integration vs separation debate concerns whether climate finance should be mainstreamed through development institutions or channeled through dedicated climate funds. The public vs private debate concerns the appropriate balance between public and private finance and whether private finance should count toward international commitments. These debates reflect deeper disagreements about the nature of climate finance, the appropriate roles of different actors, and the relationship between climate action and broader development objectives.

### 7.4 Networks and Collaborations

Climate finance research is characterized by several collaborative networks and communities of practice that span institutions and disciplines. These networks facilitate knowledge exchange, methodological development, and collective advocacy.

**OECD-Centered Network**: A network of researchers and practitioners centered on the OECD has been influential in methodological development, particularly on mobilized private finance estimation. This network includes OECD staff (Jachnik, Caruso, Ellis, Falduto), consultants and collaborators from think tanks and universities, and representatives from bilateral providers and MDBs. This network has produced the bulk of methodological guidance and has shaped UNFCCC transparency framework negotiations. It represents a technocratic, standardization-oriented community of practice.

**Critical Scholars Network**: A network of critical scholars, including Roberts, Weikmans, Atteridge, and others, has produced influential critiques of climate finance governance and accounting. This network spans universities (Brown, Université Libre de Bruxelles) and think tanks (SEI), and collaborates with civil society organizations. This network represents a critical, equity-oriented community that interrogates power relations and advocates for reforms.

**MDB Community of Practice**: Multilateral development banks have developed a community of practice around climate finance tracking and reporting, producing joint methodologies and annual reports. This community includes climate finance specialists from individual MDBs who collaborate on methodological development and data compilation. This community represents an institutional integration approach that mainstreams climate finance within development banking.

**Accounting Scholars Network**: An emerging network of accounting scholars is beginning to engage with climate finance, bringing accounting expertise and critical perspectives. This network includes scholars from accounting departments and research centers who are applying accounting frameworks to climate finance questions. This network represents an emerging disciplinary community that could make important contributions to understanding the social construction and governance of climate finance numbers.

**Global South Networks**: Networks of Global South scholars, practitioners, and civil society organizations are developing, though they remain less visible in the Anglophone academic literature. These networks include regional research institutions, developing country negotiators, and civil society organizations that collaborate on research, capacity building, and advocacy. These networks represent diverse perspectives and knowledge traditions that challenge dominant framings and advocate for equity and justice.

**Interdisciplinary Collaborations**: Some of the most innovative climate finance research emerges from interdisciplinary collaborations that bring together economists, political scientists, accountants, geographers, and STS scholars. These collaborations are still relatively rare but have potential to generate integrated frameworks that combine technical rigor with critical reflexivity. Fostering such collaborations should be a priority for the field.

## 8. Research Gaps and Future Directions

This section identifies priority research gaps and future directions for climate finance scholarship, with particular emphasis on opportunities for historians of economic ideas, methodologists, and scholars interested in the production of economic expertise. The gaps identified here are organized around six themes that align with the intellectual interests of journals such as *Œconomia: History | Methodology | Philosophy*.

### 8.1 Accounting and Audit Genealogies

**The Gap**: The intellectual history of climate finance accounting categories and methodologies remains largely unwritten. We lack systematic genealogies tracing the origins, evolution, and institutionalization of key accounting concepts such as Rio Markers, grant-equivalent valuation, mobilized private finance estimation, and additionality baselines. We do not know who first proposed these categories, in what institutional contexts, drawing on what intellectual traditions, and through what processes they became standardized (or failed to standardize). We lack understanding of how accounting practices migrated across institutions and policy domains, and how they were adapted and contested in the process.

**Why It Matters**: Accounting categories are not neutral technical tools but embody particular problem framings, assumptions, and interests. Understanding their genealogies can reveal the intellectual and political work involved in constructing climate finance as a measurable and governable domain. It can illuminate how particular approaches became dominant while alternatives were marginalized. And it can inform contemporary debates by revealing the contingency of current practices and the possibility of alternatives.

**Research Opportunities**:

- **Rio Marker Genealogy**: Trace the development of the OECD DAC Rio Marker system from its origins in the 1990s through its adaptation to climate finance in the 2000s. Examine who championed markers, what problems they were designed to solve, what alternatives were considered, and how markers became institutionalized despite credibility concerns. Use archival research in OECD records, interviews with key actors, and analysis of policy documents.

- **Grant-Equivalent Concept History**: Trace the intellectual history of grant-equivalent valuation from its origins in development finance to its contested application in climate finance. Examine how concepts of concessionality, donor effort, and resource transfer have been operationalized and debated. Analyze the OECD DAC modernization process and the political economy of valuation debates.

- **Mobilization Metrics Genealogy**: Trace the emergence of "mobilized private finance" as a key climate finance metric. Examine when and why the concept emerged, how it was operationalized, and how it became central to the $100 billion goal despite methodological challenges. Analyze the intellectual lineages (leverage ratios from finance, additionality from environmental economics) and the institutional politics of mobilization accounting.

- **Additionality Concept History**: Trace the genealogy of "new and additional" finance from the UNFCCC's original formulation through decades of failed operationalization attempts. Examine why additionality has proven so difficult to define and measure, and what this reveals about the politics of climate finance.

**Methodological Approaches**: Archival research in institutional records (OECD, UNFCCC, MDBs); oral histories with key actors involved in methodological development; discourse analysis of policy documents, negotiation texts, and methodological guidance; comparative institutional analysis of how different organizations have approached accounting challenges.

**Potential Outlets**: *Œconomia*, *History of Political Economy*, *Journal of the History of Economic Thought*, *Accounting, Organizations and Society*, *Critical Perspectives on Accounting*.

### 8.2 Intellectual Histories of Key Concepts

**The Gap**: Beyond accounting categories, several key climate finance concepts lack intellectual histories. We do not have systematic accounts of how concepts such as "climate finance" itself, "climate specificity," "country ownership," "blended finance," or "climate risk" emerged, evolved, and became institutionalized. We lack understanding of the intellectual lineages of these concepts—what disciplines, theories, and policy contexts they drew from—and how they have been contested and reinterpreted over time.

**Why It Matters**: Concepts shape how problems are framed and what solutions are considered. Understanding their intellectual histories can reveal the assumptions and interests embedded in dominant framings and can open space for alternative conceptualizations. It can also illuminate how economic ideas circulate across disciplines and policy domains, and how they are adapted and transformed in the process.

**Research Opportunities**:

- **"Climate Finance" Concept History**: Trace the emergence of "climate finance" as a distinct category from the 1990s to the present. Examine when the term first appeared, how it was initially defined, and how its meaning has evolved. Analyze the relationship between "climate finance" and related concepts such as "environmental finance," "green finance," and "sustainable finance." Examine how different actors and institutions have defined climate finance and what these definitional choices reveal about their interests and perspectives.

- **"Climate Specificity" Debates**: Trace debates over what degree of climate relevance is required for a financial flow to count as climate finance. Examine how the boundary between climate and non-climate finance has been constructed and contested, and what this reveals about the politics of categorization.

- **"Blended Finance" Concept History**: Trace the emergence of blended finance as a key climate finance strategy. Examine its intellectual lineages (public-private partnerships, development finance institutions, impact investing) and how it has been operationalized and contested in climate contexts.

- **"Climate Risk" Concept History**: Trace the emergence of climate risk as a financial concept, from early work on climate impacts to contemporary climate risk disclosure frameworks. Examine how climate risk has been conceptualized, measured, and integrated into financial decision-making.

**Methodological Approaches**: Conceptual history methods; discourse analysis; bibliometric analysis to trace concept diffusion; interviews with key actors; comparative analysis of how different disciplines and institutions have conceptualized key terms.

**Potential Outlets**: *Œconomia*, *History of Political Economy*, *Economy and Society*, *Journal of Cultural Economy*, *Global Environmental Politics*.

### 8.3 Institutional Ethnographies of Number Production

**The Gap**: We lack detailed understanding of how climate finance numbers are actually produced within organizations. We do not know the practical processes, organizational routines, data systems, and quality control mechanisms through which institutions generate authoritative climate finance statistics. We lack insight into the judgments, negotiations, and compromises involved in producing numbers, and how organizational cultures and institutional interests shape measurement practices.

**Why It Matters**: Understanding number production processes can reveal the social construction of quantitative authority and the gap between the apparent objectivity of numbers and the messy realities of their production. It can illuminate how institutional interests and constraints shape what gets measured and how, and how numbers acquire legitimacy despite methodological limitations. It can also inform efforts to improve measurement by revealing practical challenges and organizational barriers.

**Research Opportunities**:

- **OECD Number Production Ethnography**: Conduct ethnographic or interview-based research on how the OECD DAC produces climate finance statistics. Examine data collection processes, quality control mechanisms, interactions with data providers, and how methodological decisions are made. Analyze how the OECD's institutional mandate and culture shape its measurement practices.

- **MDB Tracking System Ethnography**: Conduct research on how multilateral development banks identify, classify, and track climate-relevant investments. Examine the organizational processes involved, including project appraisal, marker assignment, data aggregation, and quality assurance. Analyze how MDB mandates and operational contexts shape tracking practices.

- **National Reporting Ethnography**: Conduct research on how national governments produce climate finance reports for UNFCCC and other international forums. Examine data collection challenges, coordination across government agencies, methodological choices, and political pressures. Analyze how domestic politics and international commitments shape reporting practices.

- **Climate Fund Operations Ethnography**: Conduct research on how climate funds (GCF, GEF, Adaptation Fund) operationalize eligibility criteria, assess project proposals, and track disbursements. Examine how fund mandates and governance arrangements shape what gets funded and how it is reported.

**Methodological Approaches**: Institutional ethnography; semi-structured interviews with staff involved in number production; document analysis of internal methodological guidance and quality control procedures; participant observation where feasible; comparative institutional analysis.

**Potential Outlets**: *Accounting, Organizations and Society*, *Organization Studies*, *Science, Technology & Human Values*, *Social Studies of Science*, *Critical Perspectives on Accounting*.

### 8.4 Quantification Performativity Case Studies

**The Gap**: We lack empirical studies of how climate finance metrics shape behavior, decisions, and outcomes—the performative effects of quantification. We do not know whether and how metrics such as mobilized private finance ratios, Rio Marker percentages, or green bond volumes influence investment decisions, policy priorities, or institutional strategies. We lack understanding of whether metrics become targets (Goodhart's Law) and with what consequences, or how measurement regimes create incentives for particular types of interventions or reporting strategies.

**Why It Matters**: If measurement practices shape the phenomena being measured, then understanding performative effects is essential for assessing the consequences of accounting choices and for designing measurement systems that incentivize desired outcomes. Performativity studies can reveal unintended consequences of metrics and can inform debates about appropriate measurement approaches.

**Research Opportunities**:

- **Mobilization Metrics and Investment Decisions**: Examine whether and how mobilized private finance metrics influence public finance providers' investment decisions. Do providers favor projects with high leverage ratios? Does emphasis on mobilization lead to neglect of projects that are important for climate objectives but have limited private finance potential? Use mixed methods combining quantitative analysis of investment patterns with qualitative analysis of decision-making processes.

- **Rio Markers and Project Design**: Examine whether and how Rio Marker requirements influence project design. Do providers design projects to maximize marker eligibility? Does marker-based accounting create incentives for over-stating climate relevance? Use case studies and interviews with project designers and appraisers.

- **Green Bonds and Corporate Behavior**: Examine whether green bond issuance influences corporate investment and disclosure behavior. Do green bonds lead to additional green investment, or do they primarily finance projects that would have occurred anyway? Use econometric methods to estimate treatment effects alongside qualitative analysis of corporate decision-making.

- **Transparency Requirements and Reporting Behavior**: Examine how transparency requirements under the Paris Agreement influence reporting behavior. Do enhanced transparency requirements improve reporting quality, or do they primarily increase reporting volume without improving comparability? Use comparative analysis of reporting before and after transparency reforms.

**Methodological Approaches**: Mixed methods combining quantitative analysis (econometrics, quasi-experimental designs) with qualitative analysis (case studies, interviews, document analysis); comparative analysis across contexts; longitudinal analysis to trace behavioral changes over time.

**Potential Outlets**: *Organization Science*, *Administrative Science Quarterly*, *Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization*, *Accounting, Organizations and Society*, *Research Policy*.

### 8.5 Global South Intellectual Genealogies

**The Gap**: Climate finance scholarship is dominated by Global North authors, institutions, and perspectives. We lack systematic engagement with Global South intellectual traditions, non-Anglophone scholarship, and alternative framings of climate finance that center equity, justice, and developing country priorities. We do not have intellectual histories that trace the development of Global South critiques and alternative proposals, or that recover marginalized knowledge traditions.

**Why It Matters**: The dominance of Global North perspectives reproduces epistemic injustices and limits the field's ability to address equity and justice concerns. Recovering Global South intellectual genealogies can reveal alternative framings and possibilities that have been marginalized in dominant narratives. It can also inform more equitable and effective climate finance governance by centering the perspectives and priorities of those most affected by climate change and most dependent on climate finance.

**Research Opportunities**:

- **Global South Critiques History**: Trace the development of Global South critiques of climate finance from the 1990s to the present. Examine how developing country negotiators, scholars, and civil society organizations have framed climate finance issues, what alternative proposals they have advanced, and how their perspectives have been received or marginalized. Use archival research in UNFCCC records, analysis of developing country submissions and statements, and interviews with key actors.

- **Non-Anglophone Scholarship Review**: Conduct systematic reviews of climate finance scholarship in languages other than English, particularly Spanish, French, Portuguese, Arabic, and Chinese. Examine how climate finance is conceptualized and debated in different linguistic and regional contexts, and what insights are missed by Anglophone-only scholarship.

- **Justice Frameworks Genealogy**: Trace the development of justice and equity frameworks for climate finance, examining their intellectual lineages (environmental justice, climate justice, development ethics) and how they have been applied to climate finance debates. Analyze how justice frameworks challenge dominant economic framings.

- **Alternative Accounting Proposals**: Examine alternative accounting proposals advanced by Global South actors, such as needs-based assessment, recipient-centric methodologies, or justice-informed allocation criteria. Analyze their intellectual foundations and practical implications.

**Methodological Approaches**: Intellectual history methods; systematic review of non-Anglophone literature; discourse analysis of developing country statements and submissions; interviews with Global South scholars, negotiators, and practitioners; participatory research methods that center Global South perspectives.

**Potential Outlets**: *Global Environmental Politics*, *Third World Quarterly*, *Development and Change*, *Geoforum*, *Œconomia*, *Review of International Political Economy*.

### 8.6 Methodological Histories for Œconomia

**The Gap**: Climate finance offers rich material for methodological histories that examine how economic measurement practices emerge, evolve, and acquire authority—precisely the kind of scholarship that *Œconomia: History | Methodology | Philosophy* seeks to publish. Yet such methodological histories are largely absent from the climate finance literature. We lack systematic accounts of how climate finance measurement practices relate to broader histories of economic statistics, development indicators, and environmental accounting.

**Why It Matters**: Methodological histories can illuminate how economic categories and measurement practices are constructed, how they reflect particular theoretical commitments and political interests, and how they shape economic governance. Climate finance provides a contemporary case study of how measurement practices are constructed in real time, offering opportunities to study processes that are often only accessible through historical reconstruction.

**Research Opportunities**:

- **Climate Finance Measurement in the History of Economic Statistics**: Situate climate finance measurement within broader histories of economic statistics and national accounting. Examine how climate finance tracking relates to earlier efforts to measure environmental externalities, development assistance, or cross-border financial flows. Analyze what is distinctive about climate finance measurement and what it shares with other measurement domains.

- **Commensuration and Standardization**: Examine climate finance as a case study in commensuration—the process of making diverse phenomena comparable through common metrics. Analyze how diverse financial flows (grants, loans, equity, guarantees, carbon credits) are made commensurable, what is gained and lost in the process, and how standardization efforts succeed or fail.

- **Expertise and Authority**: Examine how particular institutions and methodologies acquire authority in climate finance measurement. Analyze the role of international organizations (OECD, UNFCCC), the relationship between technical expertise and political legitimacy, and how measurement authority is constructed and contested.

- **Measurement and Governance**: Examine the relationship between measurement practices and governance in climate finance. Analyze how measurement enables (or impedes) accountability, how metrics shape policy priorities, and how quantification regimes distribute power and resources.

**Methodological Approaches**: Historical methods combining archival research, oral history, and document analysis; comparative analysis across measurement domains; theoretical engagement with literatures on commensuration, standardization, expertise, and governance; reflexive attention to the relationship between measurement and the phenomena being measured.

**Potential Outlets**: *Œconomia*, *History of Political Economy*, *Journal of the History of Economic Thought*, *Economy and Society*, *Social Studies of Science*.

**Concrete Research Projects for Œconomia**:

1. **"The Invention of Mobilized Private Finance: A Methodological History"**: Trace the emergence of mobilized private finance as a climate finance metric from the Copenhagen Accord through OECD methodological development to contemporary debates. Examine intellectual lineages, institutional politics, and methodological controversies. Analyze what this case reveals about how economic categories are constructed in international policy contexts.

2. **"Commensuration and Its Discontents: The Politics of Climate Finance Accounting"**: Examine climate finance as a case study in commensuration, analyzing how diverse financial flows are made comparable and what political and epistemological work is performed by particular commensuration practices. Engage with STS and economic sociology literatures on commensuration.

3. **"From Incremental Costs to Rio Markers: The Evolution of Climate Finance Measurement, 1992-2025"**: Trace the evolution of climate finance measurement practices from the UNFCCC's original "incremental costs" concept through Rio Markers to contemporary debates. Analyze how measurement practices have co-evolved with institutional arrangements and policy commitments.

4. **"The OECD and the Production of Climate Finance Numbers: Institutional Authority and Methodological Controversy"**: Examine the OECD's role in climate finance measurement, analyzing how it acquired authority, how its methodologies have been contested, and what this reveals about the relationship between technical expertise and political legitimacy in international economic governance.

5. **"Grant Equivalents and the Valuation of Climate Finance: An Intellectual History"**: Trace the intellectual history of grant-equivalent valuation from development finance to climate finance, examining how concepts of concessionality and donor effort have been operationalized and contested. Analyze what this reveals about the politics of valuation in international finance.

## 9. Conclusions

This systematic literature review of 759 papers published between 1990 and 2025 has mapped the intellectual landscape of climate finance scholarship, tracing the evolution of definitions, accounting methodologies, and the production of economic expertise in this contested domain. The review reveals a field characterized by rapid growth, disciplinary fragmentation, persistent definitional controversies, and significant geographic and methodological gaps.

**Key Findings Synthesis**

Climate finance has evolved from a niche concern in the 1990s to a central pillar of international climate policy, with scholarship growing exponentially, particularly after the 2009 Copenhagen Accord and the 2015 Paris Agreement. Yet this growth has been accompanied by persistent fragmentation. Competing institutional definitions coexist across the UNFCCC, OECD, MDBs, and national governments, reflecting not merely technical complexity but divergent interests and perspectives. The 2009 Copenhagen pledge to mobilize $100 billion annually crystallized definitional disputes that remain unresolved: what counts as climate finance, which flows are "new and additional," how to value non-grant instruments, and how to attribute mobilized private finance.

Five major accounting controversies dominate the literature: (1) the credibility of OECD DAC Rio Markers, with empirical evidence of systematic over-crediting; (2) face value versus grant-equivalent valuation, with major implications for reported volumes and assessments of progress; (3) methodologies for estimating mobilized private finance, plagued by data limitations and attribution challenges; (4) double counting across multiple reporting systems; and (5) transparency deficits despite Paris Agreement reforms. Foundational OECD methodological work by Jachnik, Caruso, and colleagues established frameworks that have shaped practice, while critical scholarship by Roberts, Weikmans, and others has documented how accounting fragmentation erodes trust and impedes accountability.

The review identifies six priority research gaps for historians of economic ideas and methodologists: (1) genealogies of accounting categories tracing their intellectual origins and institutional adoption; (2) intellectual histories of key concepts such as additionality, mobilization, and concessionality; (3) institutional ethnographies of number production within OECD, UNFCCC, and MDB bureaucracies; (4) performativity case studies examining how metrics reshape investment and policy; (5) Global South intellectual genealogies recovering marginalized knowledge traditions; and (6) methodological histories suitable for *Œconomia* examining how measurement practices emerge and acquire authority.

**Implications for Future Research**

For scholars of the history of economic ideas, climate finance offers a rich empirical domain for examining how economic categories are constructed in real time, how measurement practices perform political work, and how expertise is produced and contested in global governance. Unlike many historical topics that require archival reconstruction, climate finance allows observation of ongoing processes of category formation, standardization efforts, and methodological controversies. This contemporaneity creates opportunities for mixed methods combining historical analysis with ethnography, interviews, and document analysis.

Climate finance also offers opportunities to examine the circulation of economic ideas across disciplines and policy domains. Concepts such as additionality, leverage, and concessionality have migrated from environmental economics, financial economics, and development finance to climate policy contexts, where they have been adapted and contested. Tracing these migrations can illuminate how economic ideas travel and transform, and how they are shaped by institutional contexts and political pressures.

The field's geographic imbalances—with Global North dominance in authorship, institutions, and perspectives—create both challenges and opportunities. The challenge is to correct these imbalances through greater engagement with Global South scholarship, non-Anglophone literature, and alternative knowledge traditions. The opportunity is to examine how geography and geopolitics shape the production and circulation of economic expertise, and how marginalized perspectives challenge dominant paradigms.

**Recommendations for Scholars Working on History of Economic Ideas**

For scholars interested in contributing to climate finance research from a history of economic ideas perspective, several recommendations emerge:

1. **Engage with Primary Sources**: Move beyond secondary literature to engage with primary sources including OECD working papers, MDB reports, UNFCCC documents, and institutional archives. These sources reveal the practical processes and political negotiations through which accounting categories and methodologies were developed.

2. **Employ Mixed Methods**: Combine historical methods (archival research, document analysis) with contemporary methods (interviews, ethnography) to study ongoing processes of category formation and standardization. The contemporaneity of climate finance allows methodological approaches unavailable for purely historical topics.

3. **Center Methodological Questions**: Focus on how measurement practices emerge, how they acquire authority, and how they shape governance. Climate finance offers ideal material for methodological histories examining the relationship between quantification and economic governance.

4. **Engage with Multiple Disciplines**: Draw on insights from accounting, STS, economic sociology, and political economy alongside history of economic thought. Climate finance requires interdisciplinary engagement to understand its technical, political, and epistemological dimensions.

5. **Attend to Geography and Power**: Examine how geographic location and geopolitical power shape the production of climate finance knowledge. Engage with Global South perspectives and non-Anglophone scholarship to develop more inclusive and equitable intellectual histories.

6. **Connect to Broader Debates**: Situate climate finance research within broader debates about the performativity of economics, the politics of quantification, the sociology of expertise, and the governance of global public goods. Climate finance offers a contemporary case study that can inform these broader theoretical conversations.

7. **Publish Strategically**: Target journals that value historical and methodological work, including *Œconomia*, *History of Political Economy*, *Journal of the History of Economic Thought*, *Accounting, Organizations and Society*, and interdisciplinary venues such as *Economy and Society* and *Social Studies of Science*.

**Final Reflections**

Climate finance is not merely a technical accounting challenge but a site where economic expertise is produced, contested, and legitimized through institutional practices, disciplinary conventions, and geopolitical power dynamics. The categories and methodologies that structure climate finance governance—Rio Markers, grant equivalents, mobilized private finance, additionality baselines—are not neutral technical tools but embody particular problem framings, assumptions, and interests. Understanding their genealogies, examining their performative effects, and recovering marginalized alternatives are essential tasks for historians of economic ideas.

The persistent fragmentation of climate finance definitions and accounting methodologies, despite three decades of institutional development and repeated calls for standardization, reveals the deeply political nature of measurement in international economic governance. Definitional disputes are not merely technical disagreements but reflect fundamental tensions over responsibility, equity, and burden-sharing in addressing climate change. The failure to achieve convergence is not simply a technical failure but reflects divergent interests and the absence of trust necessary for consensus.

For the climate finance field to mature, it requires not just improved measurement techniques but critical reflexivity about how measurement practices shape what is measured, how accounting categories embody political choices, and how quantification regimes distribute power and resources. Historians of economic ideas, with their attention to intellectual genealogies, methodological controversies, and the social construction of economic categories, have essential contributions to make to this critical reflexivity.

The research gaps identified in this review—particularly genealogies of accounting categories, institutional ethnographies of number production, performativity case studies, and Global South intellectual histories—represent not just opportunities for individual scholars but a collective agenda for developing more historically-informed, methodologically-sophisticated, and politically-aware climate finance scholarship. Pursuing this agenda can contribute not only to academic understanding but also to more equitable and effective climate finance governance.
