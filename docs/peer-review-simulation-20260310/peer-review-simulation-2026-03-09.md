# Simulated Peer Review — Referee Reports

**Manuscript:** "Counting Climate Finance. How Economists Made a Governable Object (1990–2025)"
**Target journal:** *Œconomia — History / Methodology / Philosophy*
**Date:** 2026-03-09
**Method:** Three simulated reviewers (Claude Opus agents), each with a distinct disciplinary lens

---

## Reviewer 1 — History of Economic Thought

**Recommendation: Major revision**

### Strengths
- Addresses a genuine gap: the historiography of climate economics is dominated by the modelling tradition (Nordhaus, Stern, IAMs); accounting infrastructure has been neglected.
- The core insight — "what should the carbon price be?" vs "how do we count the $100 billion?" as categorically different economic work — is sharp and productive.
- Three-act periodization is sensible; four controversies are well-chosen analytical anchors.

### Major Concerns

**1. Thin prosopography of "economists as architects."** Corfee-Morlot, Stern, Hourcade are named but intellectual biographies remain gestural. For HET, readers expect: What was Corfee-Morlot's training? What alternatives were considered and rejected for Rio markers? The AGF deliberations are mentioned but not analysed. **Recommendation:** Reconstruct at least one episode (Rio marker design or AGF methodology) in substantially greater detail, using primary sources.

**2. Theoretical framework is layered on, not integrated.** Section 4 applies four lenses sequentially — reads as post-hoc gloss rather than structuring the narrative. Does climate finance confirm, complicate, or extend Porter's "technology of distance"? The paper doesn't say. **Recommendation:** Either integrate theory into historical sections or reduce to 1-2 frameworks applied with real analytical force.

**3. Computational corpus analysis is underspecified methodologically.** What is the corpus boundary? What embedding model? What analytical operations? The relationship between computational evidence and historical argument is unclear — does the corpus discover, confirm, or merely illustrate the periodization?

**4. "Two communities" claim needs stronger evidence.** The efficiency/accountability bimodality risks reproducing a stylised opposition practitioners wouldn't recognise. Is it visible in citation patterns, institutional affiliations, methodological choices?

### Minor Concerns
- Negishi weights genealogy needs explanation for Œconomia readers.
- Missing literature on history of DAC conventions (Hynes & Scott 2013, Severino & Ray 2009, Führer 1996).
- Kaul (2003, 2017) on global public goods deserves more than parenthetical citation.
- Francophone contributions underexplored (Canfin-Grandjean, Aglietta & Espagne) — notable gap for a bilingual journal.
- Would benefit from a timeline figure mapping institutional moments against intellectual interventions.
- "Governable object" in title invokes Foucauldian governmentality (Miller & Rose) without deploying it — either adjust title or make connection explicit.

### Questions for Author
1. Can you identify a specific moment where an accounting convention *changed the behaviour* of actors it was measuring (clear performativity loop)?
2. What is the evidentiary basis for the periodization beyond corpus analysis? Archival/interview evidence?
3. How do you position relative to Gupta et al. (2024) and recent reviews of climate finance governance?
4. Has the accounting infrastructure made climate finance more or less *governable* in practice?

## Reviewer 2 — Climate Finance Policy & International Development

**Recommendation: Major revision**

### Strengths
- Core insight is original and important.
- Treatment of four controversies is well-informed and accurately characterised.
- Institutional detail on OECD Research Collaborative and AGF is genuine strength.
- Author clearly knows the empirical literature.

### Major Concerns

**1. "Economists as architects" overstates the case.** The $100bn figure emerged from diplomats and heads of state. Key missing actors:
- **Developing country negotiators** (G77+China, AOSIS) — instrumental in demanding quantification, additionality, accountability. Paper treats their agency as reactive rather than constitutive.
- **NGOs** (Oxfam, Germanwatch, WRI) — actively shaped what counted, not merely "counter-expertise."
- **Green Climate Fund** — entirely absent; developed its own investment framework and results measurement system independent of OECD/DAC categories.

**2. "No break at Paris" is too strong.** Confuses semantic stability with institutional change:
- Enhanced Transparency Framework = shift from voluntary to mandatory reporting.
- Biennial reporting, technical expert review, facilitative multilateral consideration = different governance architecture.
- Climate risk disclosure (TCFD 2017), central bank stress testing = genuinely new domain not prefigured in crystallization.
- **Qualification needed:** vocabulary/measurement categories show continuity, but institutional architecture changed significantly.

**3. Missing institutions weaken empirical coverage:**
- Green Climate Fund and Adaptation Fund (dedicated multilateral climate funds with own accounting).
- MDBs' joint climate finance tracking methodology (2011+) — project-level activity-based vs DAC's policy-marker approach.
- International Development Finance Club (IDFC) — yet another methodology since 2012.
- Climate Policy Initiative role understated — created a third measurement regime (neither OECD nor UNFCCC).

**4. Efficiency/accountability binary oversimplifies.** From within the policy community, the landscape is more multidimensional:
- Mitigation/adaptation split at least as consequential.
- Domestic/international distinction.
- Public/private boundary.
- Grant/loan/equity instrument spectrum.

### Minor Concerns
- The "Sustainability" journal appearing as 3rd most-cited venue (Table 1) is surprising and may reflect MDPI's publication model more than intellectual influence — deserves comment.
- Stadelmann et al. (2011) on additionality baselines is well cited, but the more recent Weikmans & Roberts (2019) systematic review of additionality would strengthen the discussion.
- The CDM narrative in Section 1.3 could note the CDM Executive Board's role in creating the additionality assessment framework.
- Loss-and-damage section (3.3) understates the conceptual novelty — it challenges the mitigation/adaptation binary, not just the ODA categories.

### Questions for Author
1. How do you account for MDB joint reporting methodology as a parallel (and competing) commensuration system?
2. Does the GCF's investment framework represent a break from crystallization-era categories or a continuation?
3. The paper focuses on OECD-centric actors. How would the narrative change if told from the perspective of the G77 finance negotiators?

## Reviewer 3 — STS & Sociology of Quantification

**Recommendation: Major revision**

### Strengths
- Excellent empirical history — the story of DAC infrastructure repurposing, OECD "infrastructural incumbency," and the $100bn target preceding its measurement apparatus are analytically sharp.
- Genuine contribution: redirecting attention from climate modelling to climate accounting.
- Well-suited to Œconomia's interdisciplinary readership.

### Major Concerns

**1. Separation of theory from narrative is the paper's most significant structural weakness.** Sections 1–3 are largely atheoretical; Section 4 reads as retrospective labelling. An STS reader recognises commensuration in Section 2 (face-value vs grant-equivalent = textbook Espeland & Stevens) but the paper doesn't name it there. **Recommendation:** Weave theoretical analysis into historical sections; use 2-3 well-developed concepts rather than 4 underdeveloped ones.

**2. "Performativity" is used imprecisely, conflating three traditions:**
- Austin's speech-act theory (performative utterance creates institutional fact).
- Callon's programme (economics formats markets).
- MacKenzie's Barnesian performativity (model makes world conform to model).
- The paper's "speech act that called into being the object" mixes all three. Clarify: is the claim institutional creation (Austin), market formatting (Callon), or feedback loop (MacKenzie)? Each has different evidentiary requirements.

**3. Epistemological tension between computational analysis and STS constructivism is unacknowledged.** The corpus analysis (sentence-transformer embeddings, structural breaks, GMM, ΔBIC) presents algorithmic pattern detection as objective evidence — but from STS perspective, this is itself a commensuration (transforming 22,000 texts into vectors). The paper should either (a) reflexively interrogate the method as an act of commensuration, or (b) articulate a position justifying the combination. Risk: reproducing the objectification the paper critiques.

**4. "Economization" doesn't carry sufficient analytical weight** distinct from commensuration and performativity. Section 4.3 essentially restates what 4.1 established, adding only the sociological observation about *who* did the commensuration. Without tracing micro-processes of economization (specific meetings, committee negotiations, moments when non-economic framing was displaced), it becomes a synonym for "economists got involved."

### Minor Concerns
- **Missing governance-by-indicators literature:** Merry (*The Seductions of Quantification*, 2016); Rottenburg et al. (*The World of Indicators*, 2015); Davis et al. (*Governance by Indicators*, 2012) — directly relevant, conspicuously absent.
- **Boundary work vs boundary objects:** Section 4.4 uses Gieryn, but the evidence suggests climate finance categories function as boundary objects (Star & Griesemer 1989) — shared across communities with different interpretations while maintaining enough identity to be recognisable. Paper hints at this in 4.5 but doesn't develop it.
- **"Constitutive, not convergent" claim** (Section 4.5) is asserted rather than demonstrated. What mechanism explains why the two poles sustain rather than erode each other? Could draw on Abbott's "linked ecologies" or Bourdieu's field theory.
- **Power (1997) on audit failure** is invoked too briefly — the Rio marker story is a rich case of "rituals of verification" deserving more than a parenthetical citation.
- **"Economists didn't apply theory but built categories"** needs qualification — the categories encode substantial theoretical commitments (market-failure logic, crowding-in assumptions). STS would recognise categories *as* theoretical commitments in material form (cf. Latour & Woolgar on inscription devices).
- **Consider Latour's "centres of calculation"** as potentially more parsimonious than the four-lens approach — OECD and UNFCCC function precisely as centres where heterogeneous information is accumulated and rendered comparable.

### Questions for Author
1. If you integrated theory into narrative, which concepts would you foreground where — and would any of the four lenses prove dispensable?
2. Can you specify which sense of performativity you intend? What are the specific "economic devices" (Muniesa, Millo, Callon) through which the $100bn target performed its reality?
3. Would you accept that the corpus analysis is itself an act of commensuration — and if so, doesn't this require reflexive discussion?
4. Given the 8.6% non-English share and the OECD-centric narrative — does the paper risk reproducing the perspective it analyses?
