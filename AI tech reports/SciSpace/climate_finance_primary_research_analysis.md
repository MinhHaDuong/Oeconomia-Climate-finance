## TL;DR

Key methodological controversies center on what counts as climate finance (Rio markers), how to value instruments (face value versus grant equivalent), how to measure mobilized private flows, and how to avoid double counting and ensure transparency. The corpus identifies a compact set of reports and critiques that together form the methodological canon and the intellectual history needed for an Œconomia submission.

## Foundational accounting methodologies

This section identifies the most cited and methodologically influential papers for each accounting category in Part 1, drawing on OECD, MDB and academic technical reports that shaped practice and critique. Each bullet lists 3–5 core works that either developed methodologies, tested them empirically, or mounted systematic critiques.

A Rio markers and OECD DAC methodology

- **Roberts and Weikmans 2017** critiques the Rio Marker–based accounting system and documents how the DAC approach fragments trust and comparability in UNFCCC reporting [1].  
- **Weikmans 201?** empirically tests the adaptation Rio Marker in a country case and highlights credibility issues in Rio Marker application [2].  
- **Falduto and Ellis 2019** analyses reporting tables and options under SBSTA/CTFs, showing how DAC-derived tabular formats interact with UNFCCC transparency requirements [3].  
- **Koch and Aleksandrova 202?** situates Rio Marker use within broader reporting/accounting debates and flags limitations of the DAC approach for integrated climate‑development accounting [4].  

B Grant equivalent versus face value

- **Bachus and Bécault 2017** reviews methods to value non‑grant instruments and documents contested approaches to grant‑equivalent valuation for loans and equity [5].  
- **Pauw et al. 2022** discusses the practical implications of face‑value accounting versus concessional/grant‑equivalent metrics for post‑2025 targets and accountability debates [6].  
- **Bergsvik et al. 2024** examines transparency challenges when actors report loans without consistent grant‑equivalent calculations [7].  

C Mobilized private finance

- **Jachnik Caruso Srivastava 2015** provides a methodological framework for estimating publicly mobilized private climate finance and outlines decision points and trade‑offs [8].  
- **Caruso and Ellis 2013** compares definitions and methods used by 24 actors to estimate mobilized private finance and highlights wide methodological divergence and double‑counting risk [9].  
- **Jachnik and Raynaud 2015** tests sector‑level approaches (renewables case) and shows data and attribution limits for leverage ratios and co‑finance estimates [10].  
- **Atteridge and Dzebo 2015** interrogates the vagueness of what private finance “counts” under international pledges and the implications for accounting [11].  

D Additionality and new and additional

- **Stadelmann Roberts Michaelowa 2011** systematically tests eight baseline options for assessing “new and additional” finance and identifies a narrow set of viable baselines [12].  
- **Stadelmann Michaelowa Roberts 2013** surveys difficulties in accounting private finance and explicitly links additionality debates to leverage and concessionality measurement problems [13].  
- **Roberts and Weikmans 2017** situates “new and additional” disputes in political trust and accounting fragmentation under the UNFCCC [1].  

E Double counting and transparency

- **Forstater 2012** sets out transparency principles and MRV needs to limit double counting and improve comparability of reported climate finance [14].  
- **Caruso and Jachnik 2014** evaluates commercial and public data sources and highlights how methodological heterogeneity generates double‑counting risk [15].  
- **Caruso and Ellis 2013** documents specific double‑counting channels across actors reporting mobilized private finance [9].  
- **Shadow Report 2018** provides civil society evidence of inconsistent counting practices and transparency shortfalls in the $100bn reporting exercise [16].  

F UNFCCC reporting and SCF assessments

- **Falduto and Ellis 2019** maps SBSTA options for Common Tabular Formats and explains how MPGs/CTFs can harmonize reporting on finance provided, mobilized and received [3].  
- **Reporting of Climate Finance under the UNFCCC** (report) analyses how Parties currently use DAC/Rio approaches in UNFCCC submissions and reporting templates [17].  
- **Roberts and Weikmans 2017** argues the lack of modalities for accounting under the UNFCCC impedes meaningful assessment and trust, linking to SCF assessment challenges [1].

## Definitions and conceptual evolution

This section identifies works that explicitly trace how “climate finance” definitions evolved across policy epochs and institutional actors, and that compare institutional framings. Each listed paper documents definitional shifts or compares competing institutional definitions.

- **Same but different 2022** maps divergent definitions and accounting methods across actors, arguing that lack of convergence undermines the new collective quantified goal process [18].  
- **Roberts and Weikmans 2017** traces how Copenhagen/Cancun promises and the $100bn pledge produced a patchwork of definitions and accounting practices that persist into the Paris era [1].  
- **Atteridge and Dzebo 2015** analyses the Copenhagen commitment’s vagueness about which private flows count and how that ambiguity shaped subsequent debates [11].  
- **Caruso and Ellis 2013** compares 24 actor definitions of mobilized private finance and shows methodological plurality and non‑alignment between OECD, MDBs and other actors [9].  
- **Pauw et al. 2022** reflects on how the post‑Copenhagen face‑value practice persists and how definitional choices affect the design of post‑2025 goals [6].  
- **Forstater 2012** and **Falduto and Ellis 2019** respectively discuss the implications of transparency rules and CTFs for standardizing definitional choices under UNFCCC reporting [14] [3].  
- **Caruso and Jachnik 2014** surveys data sources and shows how underlying data architectures shape—and constrain—what definitions are operationally feasible [15].

## Political economy perspectives

This section highlights critical, STS and political economy treatments that interrogate how numbers are produced, endorsed and performatively used in climate politics. Each paper explores power, legitimacy or performativity in climate finance accounting.

- **Roberts and Weikmans 2017** offers a critical political reading of how accounting fragmentation reproduces mistrust and protects certain reporting interests [1].  
- **Purdon 20??** uses political economy to examine climate finance instruments, markets and state practices and the implications for effectiveness and authority in finance flows [19].  
- **Shadow Report 2018** mobilizes civil society critique to contest official reporting practices and to reveal politically consequential counting choices [16].  
- **Stadelmann Michaelowa Roberts 2013** interrogates how technical accounting choices (e.g., leverage ratios) embed normative views about development finance and concessionality [13].  
- **Bergsvik Gupta Weikmans Weikmans Möller 2024** assesses whether transparency reforms actually clarify or further politicize climate finance accounting [7].

## Institutional production practices

This section selects papers that analyze how major institutions (OECD, MDBs, UNFCCC, national providers) produce, standardize and legitimize climate finance categories and statistics. Each work documents procedural choices, joint methodologies, or institutional reporting products.

- **Jachnik Caruso Srivastava 2015** lays out an OECD‑oriented framework for estimating mobilized private finance and sets out methodological decision points used by providers [8].  
- **MDB Joint Reports 2015 and 2016** document the MDBs’ joint methodologies for tracking MDB climate finance and reveal institution‑level standardization efforts [20] [21].  
- **Caruso and Jachnik 2014** surveys data sources and shows how data providers’ classification systems shape institutional reporting [15].  
- **Falduto and Ellis 2019** unpacks SBSTA/MPG modalities and the role of CTFs in reconciling UNFCCC and OECD reporting formats [3].  
- **Forstater 2012** sets out transparency and MRV recommendations that influence institutional reforms and reporting expectations [14].
