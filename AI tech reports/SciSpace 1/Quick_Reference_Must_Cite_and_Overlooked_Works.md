## PART 1: MUST-CITE REFERENCES FOR ŒCONOMIA (13 Essential Papers)

These foundational works are essential for any *Œconomia* submission on the intellectual history and methodology of climate finance concepts.

### 1. **Jachnik, Caruso & Srivastava (2015)**
**Title:** Estimating Mobilised Private Climate Finance: Methodological approaches, options and trade-offs  
**Venue:** OECD Research Papers in Economics (Report)  
**DOI:** 10.1787/5JS4X001RQF8-EN  
**Why Essential:** Presents the structured framework of decision points for estimating publicly mobilized private climate finance. This OECD report has been central to international guidance and shaped subsequent MRV debates. Foundational for understanding how mobilization metrics emerged and became institutionalized.

---

### 2. **Caruso & Jachnik (2014)**
**Title:** Exploring Potential Data Sources for Estimating Private Climate Finance  
**Venue:** OECD Research Papers in Economics (Report)  
**DOI:** 10.1787/5JZ15QWZ4HS1-EN  
**Why Essential:** Reviews commercial and public data sources, assessing their classification systems, coverage, and transparency. Shows how data architecture constrains definitional and methodological choices—crucial for understanding the material infrastructure of climate finance accounting.

---

### 3. **Caruso & Ellis (2013)**
**Title:** Comparing Definitions and Methods to Estimate Mobilised Climate Finance  
**Venue:** OECD Research Papers in Economics (Report)  
**DOI:** 10.1787/5K44WJ0S6FQ2-EN  
**Why Essential:** Compares 24 actors' definitions and methods for counting mobilized private finance, documenting methodological heterogeneity and concrete double-counting channels. This comparative mapping is foundational for debates on standardization and reveals the political economy of measurement divergence.

---

### 4. **Jachnik & Raynaud (2015)**
**Title:** Sector-level approach to estimating mobilised private climate finance: The case of renewable energy  
**Venue:** OECD Research Papers in Economics (Report)  
**DOI:** 10.1787/5JRP02JNZL35-EN  
**Why Essential:** Tests two practical approaches for estimating mobilized private finance at sector level (investment database and leverage ratio extrapolation). Demonstrates severe data limits and sensitivity of leverage methods to attribution choices—key for methodological critique.

---

### 5. **Stadelmann, Roberts & Michaelowa (2011)**
**Title:** New and additional to what? Options for baselines to assess "new and additional" climate finance  
**Venue:** Climate and Development  
**DOI:** 10.1080/17565529.2011.599550  
**Why Essential:** Systematically assesses eight baseline options for operationalizing "new and additional" finance, concluding only two options meet key feasibility and equity criteria. This work anchors ongoing baseline debates and traces the intellectual genealogy of additionality from environmental economics to climate policy.

---

### 6. **Roberts & Weikmans (2017)**
**Title:** Postface: fragmentation, failing trust and enduring tensions over what counts as climate finance  
**Venue:** International Environmental Agreements: Politics, Law and Economics  
**DOI:** 10.1007/S10784-016-9347-4  
**Why Essential:** Critically interrogates how the lack of accounting modalities fragments trust and impedes UNFCCC bottom-up processes. Argues accounting disputes are political and institutional as much as technical—essential for political economy and critical perspectives on climate finance.

---

### 7. **Bachus & Bécault (2017)**
**Title:** Public climate finance: the challenge of reporting equity  
**Venue:** Research Papers in Economics  
**Why Essential:** Surveys valuation methods for loans and equity, highlighting unresolved debates on grant-equivalent calculation, especially for equity instruments. Influenced OECD DAC modernization discussions and reveals contested boundaries of concessionality measurement.

---

### 8. **Pauw et al. (2022)**
**Title:** Post-2025 climate finance target: how much more and how much better?  
**Venue:** Climate Policy  
**DOI:** 10.1080/14693062.2022.2114985  
**Why Essential:** Examines accounting practices (face value vs grant equivalent) and accountability challenges for designing a post-2025 target. Emphasizes the governance consequences of definitional choices—bridges technical accounting debates with normative policy design.

---

### 9. **Forstater (2012)**
**Title:** Towards Climate Finance Transparency  
**Venue:** Report  
**Why Essential:** Sets out transparency principles and MRV approaches to reduce double counting and improve comparability. Influential in shaping civil society and policy debates on reporting standards. Traces how transparency became a governance technology in climate finance.

---

### 10. **Atteridge & Dzebo (2015)**
**Title:** When does private finance count as climate finance? Accounting for private contributions towards international pledges  
**Venue:** Working paper/article  
**Why Essential:** Examines the Copenhagen pledge's inclusion of private finance and the resulting ambiguity about which private flows qualify. Frames later methodological debates about counting and attribution—shows how political commitments create measurement challenges.

---

### 11. **Weikmans (year TBD)**
**Title:** Vietnam, a Donor Darling of Adaptation Aid? The Credibility of the Rio Marker 'Adaptation' Put to The Test  
**Venue:** Article  
**Why Essential:** Provides a case study testing the adaptation Rio Marker and demonstrates practical credibility issues in marker application. Illustrates on-the-ground limits of marker-based accounting—essential for understanding gap between methodology and practice.

---

### 12. **Falduto & Ellis (2019)**
**Title:** Reporting Tables: potential areas of work under SBSTA and options - Part II: Financial support provided, mobilised and received  
**Venue:** OECD/UNFCCC report  
**DOI:** 10.1787/B0BA5A7E-EN  
**Why Essential:** Analyzes options for Common Tabular Formats under the Katowice MPGs and discusses how CTFs can facilitate comparability across UNFCCC reports. Connects technical table design to political feasibility—shows how reporting infrastructure shapes what can be known.

---

### 13. **Stadelmann, Michaelowa & Roberts (2013)**
**Title:** Difficulties in accounting for private finance in international climate policy  
**Venue:** Climate Policy  
**DOI:** 10.1080/14693062.2013.791146  
**Why Essential:** Surveys difficulties in accounting private finance and explicitly links additionality debates to leverage and concessionality measurement problems. Demonstrates how technical accounting choices embed normative views about development finance.

## PART 2: OVERLOOKED & NON-MAINSTREAM REFERENCES (10 Important Works)

These papers deserve greater attention due to their Global South perspectives, grey literature insights, or recent contributions that haven't yet accumulated citations.

### 1. **Ramos Farroñán et al. (2025)**
**Title:** Green Finance and the Energy Transition: A Systematic Review of Economic Instruments for Renewable Energy Deployment in Emerging Economies  
**Venue:** Energies  
**DOI:** 10.3390/en18174560  
**Why Overlooked:** Recent systematic review oriented to emerging economies with an Institutional Capacity Integration Framework linking instrument efficacy to oversight and market capacity. Valuable for Global South policy histories and instrument effectiveness debates. Published 2025, so not yet widely cited.

---

### 2. **Timilsena (2023)**
**Title:** Assessing Climate Finance Practices in Developing Countries: A Systematic Literature Review  
**Venue:** JJMR  
**DOI:** 10.3126/jjmr.v1i1.69931  
**Why Overlooked:** Focuses on country-level reporting, GCF mobilization, and local participation challenges in vulnerable states. Provides grounded perspectives often missing in global accounts. Published in regional journal with limited international visibility.

---

### 3. **Sopin & Techato (2023)**
**Title:** A Systematic Review on Nexus Between Green Finance and Climate Change: Evidence from China and India  
**Venue:** International Journal of Energy Economics and Policy  
**DOI:** 10.32479/ijeep.14331  
**Why Overlooked:** Comparative evidence from two large emerging economies useful for reframing how different development models shape finance concepts. Challenges OECD-centric narratives but published in specialized venue.

---

### 4. **Sarfo-Adu & Kokofu (2023)**
**Title:** Climate Vulnerability, Justice, and Financing Nexus: A Case For Optimizing Climate Interventions  
**Venue:** Global Journal of Science Frontier Research  
**DOI:** 10.34257/gjsfrhvol23is5pg17  
**Why Overlooked:** Integrates climate justice frameworks with financing design, centering equity debates that histories of climate finance must incorporate. Proposes the SAVE framework. Published in less-known venue but conceptually innovative.

---

### 5. **Desalegn & Tangl (2022)**
**Title:** Enhancing Green Finance for Inclusive Green Growth  
**Venue:** Sustainability  
**Why Overlooked:** Explicitly addresses regulatory and capacity constraints in developing contexts, offering policy-focused evidence from underrepresented regions (African focus). Challenges assumptions about finance mobilization in low-capacity settings.

---

### 6. **Agustini & Arifa (2024)**
**Title:** Climate Change Accounting and Disclosure  
**Venue:** The Indonesian Accounting Review  
**Why Overlooked:** Regionally grounded study on non-standardized disclosure that exposes the limits of global reporting frameworks in practice. Shows how accounting standards work (or fail) in middle-income country contexts. Indonesian venue limits visibility.

---

### 7. **de Aguiar, Haque & McCann (2024)**
**Title:** Mapping accounting literature on climate finance: identifying research gaps and reflections on future research  
**Venue:** Accounting Research Journal  
**DOI:** 10.1108/arj-06-2023-0162  
**Why Overlooked:** Brings accounting scholarship explicitly into climate finance, identifying gaps in accountability, audit, and reporting debates. Recent (2024) and in specialized accounting venue, so not yet widely cited in climate policy circles. Critical for methodological histories.

---

### 8. **Kumar et al. (2023)**
**Title:** Green Financing: an immediate need for sustainable development  
**Venue:** Ecology, Environment and Conservation  
**Why Overlooked:** South Asian focus with bibliometric and policy reflections that could enrich comparative intellectual histories. Published in regional ecology journal, limiting international reach despite valuable regional insights.

---

### 9. **Larrea (2024)**
**Title:** Climate Funds and Sustainable Development  
**Venue:** Sustainable Development Goals Series (book chapter)  
**DOI:** 10.1007/978-3-031-50218-7_2  
**Why Overlooked:** Grey-literature style chapter that combines case studies with fund governance critiques. Useful for bridging policy reports and academic histories. Book chapters often under-cited compared to journal articles.

---

### 10. **Carty & Le Comte (2018)**
**Title:** Climate Finance Shadow Report 2018: Assessing progress towards the $100 billion commitment  
**Venue:** Oxfam Report  
**DOI:** 10.21201/2018.2388  
**Why Overlooked:** Civil society critique contesting official reporting practices and revealing politically consequential counting choices. Grey literature from NGO often excluded from academic bibliographies despite methodological rigor and unique access to recipient country data.

## FINAL NOTES FOR ŒCONOMIA SUBMISSION

1. **Balance OECD-centric and critical perspectives:** The OECD methodological reports (Jachnik, Caruso, Ellis) are foundational but should be read alongside critiques (Roberts & Weikmans, civil society reports).

2. **Emphasize intellectual history:** Frame accounting debates as sites where economic concepts (additionality, leverage, concessionality) are contested and stabilized through expert practice.

3. **Highlight Global South voices:** Use overlooked papers strategically to show alternative framings and challenge Northern epistemologies.

4. **Connect to broader STS/history of economics literature:** Link climate finance accounting to literatures on quantification, commensuration, audit culture, and the performativity of economics.

5. **Identify your unique contribution:** The gap in intellectual/methodological histories of climate finance concepts is wide open. Position your work as filling this lacuna.
