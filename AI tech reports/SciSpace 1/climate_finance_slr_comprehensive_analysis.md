## Open research gaps

The supplied reviews collectively flag several high‑value gaps where history of economic ideas and methodology scholarship can contribute; these are actionable directions for Œconomia submissions.

- **Accounting and audit genealogy** — the accounting literature on climate finance is thin; intellectual histories tracing how accounting categories (grant‑equivalent, mobilized finance, concessionality) emerged across institutions would fill a key lacuna [4].  
- **Institutional ethnographies of number production** — detailed studies of OECD, UNFCCC, MDB and fund‑level practices that reconstruct how protocols and markers were invented and standardized are largely absent in the supplied abstracts and would clarify performativity and legitimacy dynamics [9] [12].  
- **Quantification performativity case studies** — empirical micro‑studies showing how specific metrics altered investment, policy or local outcomes are limited in the provided set; historians of economic thought can link metric invention to policy shifts [4] [1].  
- **Global South intellectual genealogies** — scholarship tracing Southern critiques, alternative concepts of justice and finance, and non‑Anglophone literatures is underrepresented and would correct skewed genealogies [10] [15].  
- **Methodological histories of mobilization metrics** — how "mobilized private finance" and blended finance leverage metrics became accepted (or contested) needs tracing; comparative archival work across MDBs/OECD is warranted and not available in the abstracts [17] [18].  
- **Transparency and double counting forensic work** — there is an absence of replicated reconciliations of UNFCCC/OECD/MDB reported flows in the supplied summaries; method‑focused histories could reconstruct how counting rules evolved and where incentives for double counting arose [4] [11].  
- **Interdisciplinary intellectual lineage analyses** — mapping how economics, development studies, accounting, and STS have each shaped canonical concepts in climate finance would illuminate conceptual borrowing and border disputes visible across the reviews [2] [5].

- Archival project reconstructing the development of OECD/DAC and UNFCCC tracking protocols and internal debates (institutional memos and meeting records).  
- Comparative case studies of how a mobilization metric (e.g., "mobilized private finance") was operationalized across three MDBs and how that affected project approvals.  
- Intellectual history tracing the genealogy of "additionality" and its migration from environmental economics to multilateral fund rules and national reporting.
