# Annotated Bibliography

## Climate Finance: Intellectual and Methodological Debates in Economics and Political Economy

*Companion to "Counting What Counts: A Critical Literature Review"*

---

## I. Foundational Policy Documents and Institutional Reports

### UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance (2022). *Sixth Biennial Assessment and Overview of Climate Finance Flows*. Bonn: UNFCCC.
**URL:** https://unfccc.int/topics/climate-finance/resources/biennial-assessment-and-overview-of-climate-finance-flows

The authoritative biennial assessment produced under UNFCCC auspices. Provides the closest thing to an official definition of climate finance, describing it as finance that "aims at reducing emissions and enhancing sinks of greenhouse gases" and "reducing vulnerability of, and maintaining and increasing the resilience of, human and ecological systems to negative climate change impacts." Essential for understanding how the UNFCCC has attempted to establish definitional authority, though the report acknowledges that "the lack of a clear definition of climate finance is a significant limitation" even within its own Enhanced Transparency Framework. Documents that global climate finance flows reached approximately $803 billion annually in 2019-2020.

### OECD (2022). *Climate Finance Provided and Mobilised by Developed Countries in 2016-2020: Insights from Disaggregated Analysis*. Paris: OECD Publishing.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1787/286dae5d-en

The OECD's official accounting of progress toward the $100 billion goal. Reports that the target was met for the first time in 2022 at $83.3 billion (later revised upward to $115.9 billion). Critical for understanding the dominant methodology: face-value reporting of loans, inclusion of mobilized private finance, and reliance on Rio markers. The disaggregated analysis reveals the composition of finance (approximately 70% loans, 26% grants) and geographic distribution that civil society critics have challenged. Methodologically important for historians as it represents the institutional consolidation of development economics approaches within climate finance.

### OECD (2024). *Climate Finance Provided and Mobilised by Developed Countries in 2013-2022*. Paris: OECD Publishing.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1787/19150727

Most recent comprehensive OECD assessment claiming $115.9 billion mobilized in 2022. Updates methodology and provides decade-long trend analysis. Essential reference for official figures that civil society analyses contest.

### OECD DAC (2023). *OECD DAC Rio Markers for Climate: Handbook*. Paris: OECD.
**URL:** https://www.oecd.org/dac/environment-development/Revised%20climate%20marker%20handbook_FINAL.pdf

The technical manual governing how bilateral donors apply climate markers to development finance. Explains the distinction between "principal" (score 2) and "significant" (score 1) objectives, acknowledging that "the methodology is based on the purpose of the activity and not the type of activity." Documents that markers were "originally designed to help members with the preparation of their National Communications or National Reports to the Rio Conventions" before being repurposed for climate finance tracking. Essential for understanding the inherited institutional infrastructure from development economics.

### OECD (2024). *Climate and Development Finance FAQ*. Paris: OECD.
**URL:** https://www.oecd.org/en/data/insights/data-explainers/2024/12/climate-and-development-finance-faq.html

Recent clarification document attempting to explain the relationship between "climate-related development finance" and "climate finance provided and mobilised." Acknowledges the heterogeneity in reporting: "In the UNFCCC and Paris Agreement data collection processes, each reporter may employ different underlying assumptions, definitions, and methodologies, and thus the resulting data are heterogeneous." Useful for understanding institutional efforts to harmonize competing frameworks.

### UN Secretary-General's Independent Expert Group (2020). *Delivering on the $100 Billion Climate Finance Commitment and Transforming Climate Finance*. New York: United Nations.
**URL:** https://www.un.org/sites/un2.un.org/files/2020/12/100_billion_climate_finance_report.pdf

Commissioned report assessing progress toward Copenhagen commitments. Acknowledges "two methodological problems have persisted" regarding climate-specific reporting and over-reporting by some bilateral providers ("in our view in the order of $3-4 billion"). Recommends MDB reform and capital increases. Important for understanding how official institutions have recognized measurement problems while maintaining commitment to existing frameworks.

### IIED (International Institute for Environment and Development) (2022). *The Case for a Functional Definition of Climate Finance*. London: IIED.
**URL:** https://unfccc.int/sites/default/files/resource/IIED_ClimateFinanceDefinition_May2022.pdf

Policy paper arguing for clearer definitional standards under UNFCCC. Proposes criteria for what should count as climate finance based on functional contribution to climate objectives rather than donor self-reporting. Important intervention in definitional debates from development-focused research institution.

## II. Civil Society Counter-Accounting

### Oxfam International (2020). *Climate Finance Shadow Report 2020: Assessing Progress Towards the $100 Billion Commitment*. Oxford: Oxfam.
**URL:** https://www.oxfam.org/en/research/climate-finance-shadow-report-2020

Landmark civil society analysis introducing the "Climate-Specific Net Assistance" (CSNA) methodology. Estimates that the "true value" of climate finance in 2017-2018 was $19-22.5 billion versus reported $59.5 billion—roughly one-third of official figures. Key methodological innovations include: converting loans to grant-equivalent using OECD's own Differentiated Discount Rates; applying more conservative coefficients to "significant" marked projects; excluding projects with questionable climate relevance. Influential in shaping subsequent debates about face value versus grant equivalent reporting.

### Oxfam International (2023). *Climate Finance Short-Changed: The Real Value of the $100 Billion Commitment in 2020*. Oxford: Oxfam.
**URL:** https://www.oxfam.org/en/research/climate-finance-short-changed

Updated shadow report finding that "the true value of climate finance provided in 2020" was "$21-24.5 billion as against a reported figure of $68.3 billion"—inflation of approximately 225%. Documents that "loans are dominating over 70% provision ($48.6 billion) of public climate finance, adding to the debt crisis across developing countries." Influential framing of climate finance as potential "crisis profiteering" by wealthy nations.

### Oxfam (2024). *Climate Finance Short-Changed: 2024 Update - CSNA Estimate Methodology Note*. Oxford: Oxfam.
**URL:** https://unfccc.int/sites/default/files/resource/Oxfam_Climate_Finance_Short-Changed_2024_update_CSNA_Estimate_Methodology_Note.pdf

Technical methodology document explaining Oxfam's grant-equivalent calculations. Details how "non-concessional loans are often extended at terms that can even lead to a profit for the issuing country" and explains use of OECD Differentiated Discount Rates and Minimum Premium Rates for more accurate accounting. Essential for understanding civil society counter-methodology.

### Oxfam and CARE Climate Justice Centre (2025). *Climate Finance Short-Changed: Two-Thirds of Climate Funding for Global South is Loans*. Oxford/Copenhagen: Oxfam/CARE.
**URL:** https://www.oxfam.org/en/press-releases/two-thirds-climate-funding-global-south-loans-rich-countries-profiteer-escalating

Most recent joint analysis finding that developing countries are "paying more back to wealthy nations for climate finance loans than they receive—for every 5 dollars they receive they are paying 7 dollars back." Estimates 2022 climate loans of $62 billion will generate repayments of up to $88 billion, representing "42% profit for creditors." Extends analysis to gender dimensions, finding "only 3% of finance specifically aimed at enhancing gender equality." Important for understanding how civil society critique has evolved from measurement issues to broader political economy framing.

### ODI (Overseas Development Institute) (2023). *A Fair Share of Climate Finance?* London: ODI.
**URL:** https://unfccc.int/sites/default/files/resource/ODI_A_fair_share_of_climate_finance.pdf

Analysis of burden-sharing among developed country contributors. Proposes methodology for "downscaling international commitments to the national level" based on economic capacity and historical emissions. Documents variation in country performance: some donors (Denmark, Sweden) provide higher shares of grants to LDCs while others (France, Japan) rely heavily on loans. Notes that "the OECD data captures climate-related loans at their face value, rather than as their grant equivalent, so the data used does not reflect the accurate scale of donor countries' underlying fiscal commitment."

### Development Initiatives (2024). *Climate Finance: Earning Trust Through Consistent Reporting*. Bristol: Development Initiatives.
**URL:** https://devinit.org/resources/climate-finance-earning-trust-through-consistent-reporting/

Comprehensive quantitative and qualitative review of bilateral climate finance reporting. Employs machine learning analysis of project descriptions to assess reliability of Rio marker application. Finds that "significant-marked projects are most likely to be wrongly categorised" and identifies France and Japan as outliers with particularly poor correspondence between project descriptions and climate markers. Important methodological contribution demonstrating how natural language processing can be applied to audit climate finance claims.

### Development Initiatives (2024). "Do we need a common definition of climate finance? It depends who you ask." Bristol: Development Initiatives.
**URL:** https://devinit.org/blog/do-we-need-common-definition-climate-finance/

Blog analysis invoking Goodhart's Law to explain climate finance measurement problems: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to become a good measure." Discusses UNFCCC Standing Committee survey revealing "stark divisions between developed and developing countries" on definitional questions. Accessible introduction to definitional debates.

## III. Scholarly Literature: Political Economy and International Relations

### Ciplet, David, J. Timmons Roberts, and Mizan R. Khan (2015). *Power in a Warming World: The Global Politics of Climate Change and the Remaking of Environmental Inequality*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/9780262029612.001.0001

Foundational political economy analysis situating climate finance within broader patterns of North-South inequality. Argues that climate finance governance has been shaped by power asymmetries that reproduce rather than challenge global inequities. Influential for framing climate finance as compensation owed rather than charity given. Essential background for understanding the justice-based critique of efficiency-focused approaches.

### Roberts, J. Timmons, and Romain Weikmans (2017). "Postface: fragmentation, failing trust and enduring tensions over what counts as climate finance." *International Environmental Agreements* 17: 129-137.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10784-016-9347-4

Influential early assessment of measurement controversies. Identifies "failing trust" between developed and developing countries as consequence of contested accounting. Argues that definitional disputes reflect "deeper disagreements about the nature of climate finance obligations." Important for establishing the link between technical measurement questions and political legitimacy.

### Weikmans, Romain, and J. Timmons Roberts (2019). "The international climate finance accounting muddle: is there hope on the horizon?" *Climate and Development* 11(2): 97-111.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/17565529.2017.1410087

Detailed analysis of reporting heterogeneity across donor countries. Documents how "the lack of a common definition of climate finance makes it impossible to trace whether climate finance is additional to or just a reshuffling of existing development aid funds." Proposes reforms including standardized grant-equivalent reporting and stricter climate relevance criteria.

### Roberts, J. Timmons, Romain Weikmans, et al. (2021). "Rebooting a failed promise of climate finance." *Nature Climate Change* 11: 180-182.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-021-00990-2

Commentary arguing that climate finance has "not been 'new and additional' as promised since the 1992 UNFCCC." Proposes reforms for NCQG including grant sub-targets and stricter additionality criteria. Influential intervention from leading climate finance scholars.

### Pauw, W. Pieter (2017). "Mobilising private climate finance: developed country perspectives." *International Environmental Agreements* 17: 55-71.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10784-016-9342-9

Critical analysis of the private finance mobilization agenda. Argues that "the Cancun Agreements were the first international environmental agreement whose implementation explicitly depends in part on the mobilisation of private finance." Documents developing country opposition to private finance counting toward public commitments. Important for understanding the theoretical and political stakes of the public-private boundary.

### Pauw, W. Pieter, et al. (2022). "Post-2025 climate finance target: how much more and how much better?" *Climate Policy* 22(9): 1241-1251.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/14693062.2022.2114985

Forward-looking analysis of NCQG negotiations. Argues that "it would be ineffective to simply increase the climate finance target without addressing" measurement problems. Identifies five priority elements: relation to Article 2.1(c); adaptation-mitigation balance; financial instruments; private finance mobilization; and additionality. Proposes sub-targets for grants to increase transparency and accountability.

### Pauw, W. Pieter, et al. (2024). "How do donors integrate climate policy and development cooperation? An analysis of the development aid policies of 42 donor countries." *Climate Policy* (online first).
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/14693062.2024.2390522

Analysis of how 42 donor countries integrate climate policy and development cooperation. Finds that "despite more than 15 years of debate on new and additional climate finance, donors still do not spell out the principle of additionality in their policy documents." Documents that only Luxembourg and New Zealand clearly separate development aid and climate finance.

### Purdon, Mark (2024). *The Political Economy of Climate Finance Effectiveness in Developing Countries: Carbon Markets, Climate Funds, and the State*. Oxford: Oxford University Press.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780197756836.001.0001

Book-length comparative analysis of climate finance instruments (CDM, REDD+, NAMAs) in Tanzania, Uganda, and Moldova. Argues that effectiveness depends on alignment with domestic development priorities rather than instrument design per se. Introduces concept of "developmental environmentalism" as alternative to "liberal environmentalism." Important for understanding recipient-country perspectives often absent from donor-focused debates.

### Cash, Corrine, and Larry A. Swatuk, eds. (2022). *The Political Economy of Climate Finance: Lessons from International Development*. Cham: Palgrave Macmillan.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-031-12619-2

Edited volume explicitly connecting climate finance to development economics literature. Contributors draw parallels between climate finance governance and earlier debates about aid effectiveness, conditionality, and ownership. Useful for historians seeking to situate climate finance within longer trajectories of development thought.

### Khan, Mizan, et al. (2020). "Twenty-five years of adaptation finance through a climate justice lens." *Climatic Change* 161: 251-269.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10584-019-02563-x

Historical analysis of adaptation finance evolution since UNFCCC adoption. Documents how adaptation emerged "as an afterthought" and traces its growing prominence through Paris Agreement. Applies climate justice framework to assess adequacy and equity of adaptation finance flows.

## IV. Scholarly Literature: Economics and Finance

### Stern, Nicholas (2007). *The Economics of Climate Change: The Stern Review*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511817434

Landmark economic analysis framing climate change as "the greatest market failure the world has ever seen." While focused on mitigation costs and benefits rather than climate finance per se, establishes the welfare economics framework that has shaped mainstream approaches. Influential in legitimating economic expertise in climate policy while drawing criticism from economists skeptical of its discount rate assumptions.

### Khan, Mizan R. (2015). "Polluter-Pays-Principle: The Cardinal Instrument for Addressing Climate Change." *Laws* 4(3): 638-653.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3390/laws4030638

Analysis of the Polluter Pays Principle as alternative theoretical foundation for climate finance. Traces PPP's evolution "as an economic, ethical and legal instrument" and argues it "has the potential of effecting global responsibility for adaptation and mitigation." Notes the contradiction that "while it rests on neoliberal market principles, the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change did not include the PPP as its provision." Essential for understanding justice-based alternatives to efficiency frameworks.

### Khan, Mizan R., and Sirazoom Munira (2021). "Climate change adaptation as a global public good: implications for financing." *Climatic Change* 167: 1-18.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10584-021-03195-w

Reconceptualization of adaptation finance through public goods lens. Argues that increasing "cross-border" climate risks undermine the traditional framing of adaptation as local good. Proposes that "adaptation should be framed as a global goal and global responsibility" with implications for financing obligations. Important for understanding how theoretical frameworks shape policy prescriptions.

### Bhandary, Rishikesh Ram, Kelly Sims Gallagher, and Fang Zhang (2021). "Climate finance policy in practice: a review of the evidence." *Climate Policy* 21(10): 1182-1196.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/14693062.2020.1871313

Systematic review of nine climate finance policy instruments (feed-in tariffs, loan guarantees, green bonds, etc.). Evaluates effectiveness using criteria including mobilization effectiveness, economic efficiency, environmental integrity, and equity. Finds that "feed-in tariffs, tax credits, loan guarantees, and national development banks are all effective at mobilizing private finance, but evidence to date is weak" on other criteria. Important for evidence-based assessment of de-risking paradigm.

### Newell, Peter, and Jon Phillips (2024). "Towards a more transformative approach to climate finance." *Climate Policy* (online first).
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/14693062.2024.2377730

Critical analysis of the "de-risking narrative" in climate finance. Argues that "an approach which focuses only on using public funds to lever and de-risk private ones is only addressing half the problem: the question of mobilising finance rather than regulating and redistributing it." Documents that "private finance has performed poorly to date in delivering climate finance" despite expectations—UNFCCC data shows private finance mobilized "dropped by 2 per cent in 2019 compared to 2018 and by a further 9 per cent in 2020." Important for understanding heterodox critiques of mainstream approaches.

### Sharma, Sarah E., and Milan Babic (2025). "Introduction: The international political economy of green finance." *Review of International Political Economy* 32(1): 1-25.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1177/10245294241310546

Introduction to special issue developing IPE research agenda on green finance. Proposes "ecosystem" approach examining "actors, institutions, instruments, ideas and politics" governing green finance supply. Argues for "deep engagement" with "what green finance is, how it operates, and its outcomes." Important for situating climate finance within broader green finance literature.

### Barbi, Fernando, and Andrea Bigano (2023). "A Review of the Global Climate Finance Literature." *Sustainability* 15(2): 1255.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3390/su15021255

Bibliometric analysis of climate finance scholarship. Documents exponential growth in publications since Paris Agreement and identifies four main research themes: renewable energy financing; climate risk and financial markets; investor preferences for green assets; and climate finance governance. Notes that "leading journals in finance and economics have so far published only a small number of articles" on climate finance, suggesting disciplinary gaps.

### IMF (2022). "Mobilizing Private Climate Financing in Emerging Market and Developing Economies." *Staff Climate Notes* 2022/007. Washington: IMF.
**URL:** https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/066/2022/007/article-A001-en.xml

IMF staff analysis of private climate finance mobilization strategies. Discusses leverage ratios, guarantee mechanisms, and public-private partnerships. Notes that "MDBs' climate finance for developing countries fell well short of commitments." Important for understanding mainstream institutional perspective on private finance mobilization.

### Buchholz, Wolfgang, and Dirk Rübbelke (2021). "Overstraining international climate finance: when conflicts of objectives threaten its success." *Climate Policy* 21(6): 762-771.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/14693062.2021.1902872

Theoretical analysis of tensions between climate finance objectives. Argues that multiple goals (mitigation efficiency, adaptation equity, development co-benefits) create "conflicts of objectives" that cannot be simultaneously optimized. Important for understanding why climate finance measurement controversies may be irresolvable through technical means alone.

## V. Additionality and Development Aid

### Michaelowa, Axel, and Katharina Michaelowa (2011). "Coding Error or Statistical Embellishment? The Political Economy of Reporting Climate Aid." *World Development* 39(11): 2010-2020.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.worlddev.2011.07.020

Early systematic analysis of over-reporting in climate aid. Finds evidence of "statistical embellishment" in Rio marker application, with projects of questionable climate relevance receiving climate markers. Important methodological contribution demonstrating how political incentives shape statistical production.

### Donner, Simon D., Milind Kandlikar, and Sophie Webber (2016). "Measuring and tracking the flow of climate change adaptation aid to the developing world." *Environmental Research Letters* 11: 054006.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/11/5/054006

Analysis of adaptation finance flows finding significant measurement problems. Documents "the lack of a common definition of climate finance makes it impossible to trace whether climate finance is additional." Influential in establishing empirical basis for additionality concerns.

### Mitchell, Ian, Euan Ritchie, and Atousa Tahmasebi (2021). "Is Climate Finance Really New and Additional?" CGD Note. Washington: Center for Global Development.
**URL:** https://www.cgdev.org/publication/climate-finance-really-new-and-additional

Empirical analysis finding that approximately 45% of reported climate finance may not meet additionality criteria. Compares climate-related ODA growth to overall ODA trends. Influential in quantifying additionality concerns with rigorous methodology.

### Kenny, Charles (2023). "How Much Climate ODA Is New and Additional?" CGD Blog. Washington: Center for Global Development.
**URL:** https://www.cgdev.org/blog/how-much-climate-oda-new-and-additional

Accessible summary of additionality debate. Argues that "evidence suggests donors are considerably stripping aid finance from development projects to support climate projects." Proposes two definitions of additionality and finds that under stricter definition, "none of what is supposedly additional climate finance supported by ODA is in fact additional."

### Kenny, Charles (2020). "On Aid and Climate, Don't Make the Poor Pay Twice." CGD Blog. Washington: Center for Global Development.
**URL:** https://www.cgdev.org/blog/aid-and-climate-dont-make-poor-pay-twice

Analysis arguing that "total ODA delivered to developing countries from members of the OECD DAC, excluding humanitarian support, was $56 billion in 2018, $53 billion in 2015, and $51 billion in 2008"—suggesting climate finance substitution rather than addition. Influential framing of equity concerns.

### Kenny, Charles (2021). "If We're Going to Fund Climate Mitigation from ODA, We Need to Double It." CGD Blog. Washington: Center for Global Development.
**URL:** https://www.cgdev.org/blog/if-were-going-fund-climate-mitigation-oda-we-need-double-it

Analysis documenting that "ODA accounts for around 84 percent of bilateral climate finance" and that "between 2013 and 2018 the share of outflows from ODA-eligible multilaterals focused on climate increased from 15 percent to 28 percent." Argues this suggests "aid finance actually used in developing countries for non-climate activities fell by about $9 billion."

### Bird, Neil, and Jessica Brown (2010). "International Climate Finance: Principles for European Support to Developing Countries." EDC2020 Working Paper. London: ODI.
**URL:** https://odi.org/en/insights/climate-change-finance-must-be-additional-to-finance-for-development/

Early ODI analysis warning that "if existing aid is diverted to finance climate objectives, this would change the very nature of ODA spending in different regions and sectors." Predicts shift toward Asia and away from Africa, and toward climate-related sectors at expense of health and education.

### GSDRC (2011). "Climate Finance Additionality: Emerging Definitions and their Implications." Helpdesk Research Report. Birmingham: GSDRC.
**URL:** https://gsdrc.org/document-library/climate-finance-additionality-emerging-definitions-and-their-implications/

Overview of four prominent additionality definitions in EU policy debates. Notes that "agreeing and implementing a new separate funding mechanism will be politically difficult" while integration risks aid diversion. Important early mapping of definitional landscape.

### Halimanjaya, Aidy (2015). "Climate mitigation finance across developing countries: what are the major determinants?" *Climate Policy* 15(2): 223-252.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/14693062.2014.912978

Empirical analysis of climate aid allocation determinants. Finds that "climate objectives are not influencing development aid allocations" as much as traditional aid determinants like colonial ties and strategic interests. Important for understanding disconnect between climate finance rhetoric and allocation reality.

## VI. MDB and Private Finance

### World Resources Institute (2023). "The Good, the Bad and the Urgent: MDB Climate Finance in 2022." Washington: WRI.
**URL:** https://www.wri.org/insights/mdb-climate-finance-joint-report-2022

Analysis of joint MDB climate finance report. Documents that "between 2019 and 2022, debt-related instruments... averaged 84% of total climate finance from MDBs to low- and middle-income economies." Notes that "over 90% of MDBs' climate finance was raised through debt, and three-quarters was not concessional."

### World Resources Institute (2024). "Multilateral Development Bank Climate Finance: The Good, Bad and the Urgent." Washington: WRI.
**URL:** https://www.wri.org/insights/mdb-climate-finance-2023

Updated analysis finding that "both the share and absolute amount of MDB climate financing as grants decreased from 10% ($6.08 billion) in 2022 to 6.7% ($4.98 billion) in 2023." Raises concerns about debt sustainability given that "three-fifths of low-income economies are at risk of, or are already in, debt distress." Documents continued MDB fossil fuel financing alongside climate finance.

### World Resources Institute (2024). "New Climate Finance Goal: 3 Elements Under Negotiation at COP29." Washington: WRI.
**URL:** https://www.wri.org/insights/ncqg-climate-finance-negotiations-cop29

Analysis of NCQG negotiations noting that "a $100 million loan for renewables in Mexico or Indonesia that needs to be repaid the same as a $100 million grant for adaptation in Malawi or Tuvalu. The grant is much more valuable to the recipient and costs donors far more." Important for understanding face value versus grant equivalent stakes.

### Climate Policy Initiative (2024). *Understanding Global Concessional Climate Finance 2024*. San Francisco: CPI.
**URL:** https://www.climatepolicyinitiative.org/publication/understanding-global-concessional-climate-finance-2024/

Comprehensive mapping of concessional climate finance landscape. Finds that "approximately 360 institutions provided international concessional finance for climate action between 2019 and 2022" with "about 10 institutions providing 70% of concessional finance." Documents that "total international grant finance reached USD 38 billion in 2022" with average grant ticket size of $3 million versus $74 million for loans.

### Attridge, Samantha, and Christian Novak (2022). "An exploration of bilateral development finance institutions' business models." London: ODI.
**URL:** https://odi.org/en/publications/an-exploration-of-bilateral-development-finance-institutions-business-models/

Analysis finding that leverage ratios of DFI and MDB blended finance arrangements are "often less than one." Challenges claims about private finance mobilization and questions efficiency of de-risking approaches. Important empirical contribution to debates about leverage.

### Toetzke, Malte, et al. (2022). "Consistent and replicable estimation of bilateral climate finance." *Nature Climate Change* 12: 897-900.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-022-01482-7

Machine learning analysis of project descriptions to assess climate relevance of reported finance. Develops methodology for "consistent and replicable estimation" across donors. Finds significant variation in reliability, with France and Japan identified as outliers. Important methodological innovation enabling independent verification of climate finance claims.

### Finance for Development Lab (2025). "Climate Finance: An Ill-Designed Instrument for Development and Environment Assistance." FDL Policy Note 25.
**URL:** https://findevlab.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/FDL_Policy-Note_Climate-Finance_April-25.pdf

Recent analysis arguing that climate finance reporting systems show "aggregate finance flows reported by bilateral agencies are likely significantly overestimated." Cites research finding "the climate content of many projects labeled as 'climate finance' was exaggerated, if not fabricated." Important critical perspective from development finance institution.

### Chatham House (2024). "Closing the Climate Finance Gap: Using Public Finance to Mobilize Private Finance." London: Chatham House.
**URL:** https://www.chathamhouse.org/2024/11/closing-climate-finance-gap/03-using-public-finance-mobilize-private-finance

Analysis noting that "different institutions make numerous claims about the leverage ratios achieved when they deploy public finance to mobilize private finance" but questioning reliability. Proposes alternative MDB architecture that "genuinely leverages private finance" through reformed risk-sharing.

### CGD (Center for Global Development) (2024). "Concessional Finance for Addressing Climate Change: A System Ripe for Reform." Washington: CGD.
**URL:** https://www.cgdev.org/blog/concessional-finance-addressing-climate-change-system-ripe-reform

Analysis of climate Financial Intermediary Funds (GEF, CIF, GCF). Notes that "donor contributions to the GCF have grown most rapidly in recent years, though it has been the weakest performer" among major funds—suggesting donors don't closely examine performance when allocating resources.

### CGD (2024). "Has China Really Provided More Climate Finance to Developing Countries than the US?" Washington: CGD.
**URL:** https://www.cgdev.org/blog/has-china-really-provided-more-climate-finance-developing-countries-us

Comparative analysis finding that "expressing climate finance in grant-equivalent terms can drastically alter the picture of contributions—especially in China's case." Documents that China's bilateral climate finance is "primarily delivered in the form of loans, often at or near market rates, with only 3 percent of finance provided directly as grants." Important for understanding non-Annex II provider contributions.

### Development Initiatives (2024). "Climate finance from the World Bank: pluses and minuses." Bristol: Development Initiatives.
**URL:** https://reliefweb.int/report/world/climate-finance-world-bank-pluses-and-minuses

Analysis finding that "46% of [World Bank] total climate finance commitments made between 2013 and 2021 were considered concessional" but that "the Bank's status as the largest climate finance provider is merely a function of its overall scale, rather than an overt emphasis" on climate.

## VII. Theoretical and Conceptual

### Gardiner, Stephen M. (2011). *A Perfect Moral Storm: The Ethical Tragedy of Climate Change*. Oxford: Oxford University Press.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780195379440.001.0001

Philosophical analysis of climate change as ethical challenge involving "theoretical ineptitude" of existing frameworks. Argues that spatial disconnect between causers and sufferers, combined with temporal complexity, creates conditions for moral failure. Important for understanding why technical solutions to climate finance measurement may be insufficient.

### Schlosberg, David (2004). "Reconceiving Environmental Justice: Global Movements and Political Theories." *Environmental Politics* 13(3): 517-540.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/0964401042000229025

## X. Data Sources and Methodological Resources

### OECD Creditor Reporting System (CRS)
**URL:** https://stats.oecd.org/Index.aspx?DataSetCode=crs1

Primary database for bilateral development finance including climate-marked activities. Allows filtering by Rio markers, recipient country, sector, and financial instrument. Essential for any empirical analysis of climate finance flows.

### UNFCCC Climate Finance Data Portal
**URL:** https://unfccc.int/climatefinance

Portal providing access to Biennial Reports and Biennial Transparency Reports submitted by Annex II parties. Includes Common Tabular Format data on climate finance provided. Important for understanding how individual countries interpret reporting requirements.

### Climate Funds Update (ODI/Heinrich Böll Stiftung)
**URL:** https://climatefundsupdate.org/

Independent tracking of dedicated multilateral climate funds including GCF, GEF, Adaptation Fund, and CIFs. Provides data on pledges, deposits, approvals, and disbursements. Useful for analysis of multilateral climate finance architecture.

### Climate Policy Initiative Global Landscape of Climate Finance
**URL:** https://www.climatepolicyinitiative.org/publication/global-landscape-of-climate-finance-2023/

Annual assessment of global climate finance including domestic and private flows. Provides broader picture than UNFCCC-focused analyses. Estimates total climate finance at approximately $1.3 trillion annually, contextualizing the $100 billion goal.

### AidData Global Chinese Development Finance Dataset
**URL:** https://www.aiddata.org/data/aiddatas-global-chinese-development-finance-dataset-version-3-0

Database tracking Chinese development finance including climate-relevant projects. Important for understanding non-Annex II provider contributions not captured in OECD/UNFCCC systems.

### UNFCCC (1992). *United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change*. New York: United Nations.
**URL:** https://unfccc.int/resource/docs/convkp/conveng.pdf

Original treaty text establishing climate finance obligations. Article 4.3 establishes that developed country parties "shall provide new and additional financial resources" to developing countries. Essential primary source.

### UNFCCC (2015). *Paris Agreement*. Paris: UNFCCC.
**URL:** https://unfccc.int/sites/default/files/english_paris_agreement.pdf

Treaty establishing current climate finance framework including Article 9 obligations and NCQG mandate. Primary source for contemporary governance architecture.
