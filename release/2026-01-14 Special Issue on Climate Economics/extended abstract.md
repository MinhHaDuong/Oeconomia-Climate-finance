# History of climate finance as an economic object: accounting categories, controversies, and economists as policy experts

## Extended abstract anonymously submitted to OEconomia, 2026-01-15

This paper offers a history-of-economic-thought analysis of the emergence of climate finance as a distinct economic object from the early 1990s to the mid-2020s. It examines how economists actively shaped the categories, metrics, and boundaries through which climate-related financial flows were defined, stabilised, and contested. It thereby contributes to the history of climate economics by shifting attention from models to accounting conventions.

Rather than treating climate finance as a technical extension of environmental or development economics, we situate it within a longer and non-linear trajectory of climate economics. Early work on externalities and collective environmental constraints (Ayres and Kneese) preceded, and only partially informed, later modelling efforts centred on growth, damages, mitigation, risk, and time (Nordhaus, Manne, Stern, Weitzman). The international burden-sharing dimension embedded in models (Negishi) remained largely disconnected from parallel debates on public goods, aid, and international responsibility. Climate finance thus emerged in the 1990s not as a direct outgrowth of integrated assessment modelling, but through its entanglement with international negotiations, development finance practices, and attempts to translate climate commitments into financially actionable terms.

At this juncture, economists embedded in international organisations played a decisive role. At the OECD in particular, figures such as Jan Corfee-Morlot contributed to building statistical infrastructures---notably the Rio markers---that rendered climate finance measurable within official development finance frameworks. They defined what could legitimately count as climate finance and how responsibility could be claimed.

These accounting categories quickly became focal points of a deeper divide within economics. On one side, economists working within a market-failure framework emphasised efficiency, leverage, and capital mobilisation. Drawing on welfare economics and Pigouvian reasoning, they framed public climate finance as a catalytic instrument to correct risk mispricing and crowd in private investment. This perspective underpins blended finance, de-risking instruments, and leverage metrics promoted by the OECD, multilateral development banks, and the IMF, and resonates with Nicholas Stern's call to green international finance.

On the other side, political-economy-oriented economists challenged both measurement conventions and normative assumptions. Axel and Katharina Michaelowa highlighted incentive-driven over-reporting embedded in Rio marker practices, while Romain Weikmans and J. Timmons Roberts interpreted recurring accounting disputes as manifestations of unresolved distributive conflicts and fragile North--South trust. From this perspective, climate finance appears less as a problem of efficiency than as one of responsibility, equity, and institutional accountability, echoing Inge Kaul's arguments on the distinct financing requirements of global public goods.

We trace how this divide crystallised through four recurrent controversies: the valuation of concessional loans, the credibility of Rio markers, the attribution of mobilised private finance, and the boundary between development assistance and climate-specific obligations. In each case, economists acted not only as technical experts but as agents of boundary work, redefining what climate finance is and ought to be.

Climate finance history showcases economists as designers of policy-relevant knowledge in global climate governance. Precisely because the category resists stable definition, it requires---and rewards---the kind of pragmatic quantification that economists, rather than natural scientists, are equipped to provide.
